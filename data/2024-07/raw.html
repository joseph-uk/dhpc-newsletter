Aptitude Tests

My application for Army flying training had been accepted and I was called forward for testing.  It was over 30 years ago so please forgive me if I can’t remember the exact process, but it did involve a very thorough medical examination: ECG, EEG, X-Rays and all manner of prodding and poking.  I nearly fell at the first hurdle.  A history of migraines is a real no-no.  Had I remembered (and declared) that I used to get them regularly, after cross country running at school, then I would have been out.  In my defence it had been some 12 years beforehand without any recurrence in the interim.  Fortunately, I’d never been to the doctor about it so there was nothing in my medical records.  (This did come back to haunt me many years later when I had a migraine whilst on duty and declared it.  After much deliberation by a very senior doctor in the RAF I was allowed to continue flying but only with a co-pilot – no problem at the time because I was flying a 2-pilot aircraft.)

Part of the medical was a hearing test.  The candidates all sat in individual booths, facing outwards, with a small partition between each desk.  We each put on a headset.  ‘Press the button when you hear a noise.’  At one stage I remember glancing across the room and seeing a special forces chap sitting well back and trying to copy the actions of his neighbour.  He must have known that he was a bit deaf, probably due to exposure a few to many loud bangs over the years.  I never saw him again.

We then underwent a series of aptitude tests.  Computers were still in their infancy – think ‘bat and ball’ on a green screen.  One test was to use a joystick to try to keep a dot inside a circle.  (The museum of Army Flying has an analogue version of its predecessor.  You control a flat plate in 2 dimensions using a joystick, trying to keep a ball bearing in the centre.)  It’s a bit like using one of the three main controls (the cyclic) when trying to hover a helicopter.

Another test showed a picture of an aircraft performing a manoeuvre (a combination of climbing, diving, banking, etc) and you had to select, from a number of options, which set of flight instruments correctly matched the manoeuvre.

We’d all been told to brush up on some basic maths, and there were time, distance, velocity, and fuel burn problems to solve.  All in limited time.

Those of you who have done the coaching course will be familiar with trying to remember a sequence of random numbers that flash on a screen.  I was overwhelmed at about 6, and later wished that, rather than a coming up blank, I’d focussed on just the first 6, no matter how many digits there were.  I’ve no idea if it would have improved my score.

Each individual was interviewed by a board of very senior officers.  When asked what I could offer the Army Air Corps I cheekily replied that I’d heard that they were short of pilots and that I could be a ‘bum in a seat’.  That didn’t go down particularly well at the time, but I must have been right because I was selected for the next stage: ‘flying grading’.

10 to 100.0

Joseph Edmonds reports

Pete Logan, Chief Coach, asked me to help breathe some lift back into the Dales dXCe - 10 to 100 group. This was a group originally set up by David May and Pete and targeted those pilots in the long, often painful struggle from their first flops over the back and up up to achieving 100km in XC distance.

This period is notoriously difficult and frustrating. One of the primary reasons is that XC flights can end up being really short - one thermal climb and then over the back and onto the ground - a one climb wonder. Getting that second climb is a joyous moment. Actually being able to stay in the air in XC for hours at a time, jumping from climb to climb and actually getting the airtime required to really start to build the mental tooling and experience required to achieve proper XC flights is really magical - but the time and dedication required is significant.

And so it was that on the Solstice day in 2024 that I set off in pursuit of the impressive gaggle of XC pilots that had arrived at Windbank in pursuit of a flight to the coast. When I say in pursuit, I mean that the very start of the flight I was at the wrong end of the ridge and as I watched them all climb out and leave, I knew it was all or nothing time. I had to find my own climb and then hope that I can hang onto them enough to at least get some benefit of the gaggle.

So what is the benefit of the gaggle?

Simply enough, going XC is a continuous game of survival. You are constantly descending and the only thing keeping you airborne is to find thermal climbs that are going up faster than you are going down. Doing that on your own is certainly possible, but it can be really difficult and absolutely unforgiving of mistakes.

When you are flying with a few other pilots in a gaggle, you can work as a team - each person trying to maximise the climb but also doing their share of the workload of searching out the next best climb. Once a climb is found, then a great gaggle will also use the radio to highlight when a climb is found and roughly how strong it is. That means that the best climbs can be found and the chances of finding the next climb are massively improved.

Chasing the Gaggle out of the Dales

One significant temptation that must be avoided when chasing the gaggle is to leave your climb too early. A real balance has to be struck between being patient and getting enough height. On the other hand, you have to make some moves or you’ll never catch them. So it’s all about picking your moment really.

For me the moment was a really nice looking convergence cloud that let me find a really strong climb and catch up. You can see the tell tale tendrils on the cloud. Apparently this convergence is quite a common thing on this particular route out of the Dales. It did look pretty spectacular!

Up there with the Big Boys

Once I was caught up, it was time to do some real gaggle flying. Some great memories sharing these epic moments with these guys. It’s amazing how reassuring it is just to be able to ask something like “is it really OK to fly across this big airfield looking thing” and also just be part of the team decision making process. A good quality radio headset really helps.

Into the Blue

Thankfully I caught up just in time, as the next challenge was going to be crossing a very blue looking Vale of York. This is where the gaggle really comes into its own. Never mind the difficulty of finding climbs when there are clouds that give you at least a basic idea of what's going on. When it is fully blue then you really have nothing to go on other than the landscape and trying to figure out thermal sources and triggers. Nothing of course, apart from the gaggle!

Getting Low near Sutton Bank

Not far from Sutton Bank, it all started to look pretty desperate. I’d failed to hang onto the farts that were giving low zero-point-somethings and was below 600 metres.

 I was on my own again and needed to find a climb. I searched down the ridge and actually picked out my landing near the town of Wass. No sooner had I described my dire situation to my go pro than a few furtive bleeps were to be heard. I took a few turns and I was established in another climb.. Phew!

Into the North Yorks Moors

Back up with the gaggle we approached the North Yorks Moors. At this point the searching had pretty much spread out into a few smaller gaggles. I ended up being primarily with Chris Fountain and with Pete Morris on his infinite glide machine out front and up high telling us all how great it was as we desperately searched around lower down for much weaker lift.

Through the Moors and on Towards the Sea

Unfortunately my go pro + battery pack all ran out of juice over the North Yorks Moors, however suffice to say that we managed to connect with Jake Herbert who had flown his own route along with James Goldsborough. Unfortunately Jim had gone down somewhere around Carlton Bank and Jake was now solo. Signature orange jacket and flying an open harness he was unmistakable.

We got a great climb and then Chris and Jake decided to go to Whitby. By this point I was in totally unfamiliar territory and had no opinion at all on what was best. I was very happy to follow!

Gliding to the Coast

A long, luxuriously buoyant and sea breeze free glide towards Whitby saw me get to the sea with plenty of height. Jake and I had chosen to land at the golf course which offered perfectly manicured grass and right next to the main road.

I did a little explore out over the water and then as I saw Jake land and nicely demonstrate the wind direction and strength for me, I did my landing approach and touched down in a perfect little packing away area.

The rest of the gaggle had landed further north, around Saltburn and Marske. As there were 7 of us, we opted to organise a minibus taxi to take us all back to Windbank to collect the cars. Perfect! I organised the taxi and Jake organised the rabble…

100k Achieved

I actually logged exactly 100.0km of straight line distance, which I think is pretty amusing. It does beg the question of if I’m still allowed to be part of the 10 100 trainees or not - arguably I have still to exceed the 100km mark 🙂. Turnpoint distance was over 108 km so I guess I can regard it as achieved 👍

As a keen XContest user, I was very quick to register my flight and claim my free t shirt, wahey

X-Lakes 2024

Paul Rowntree reports

Beginner's Fumble into Hike and Flying - Episode 4

What happened in previous episodes:

Episode 1 - X-Dales 2022 - 18th

Episode 2 - X-Lakes 2023 - 1-day - 5th

Episode 3 - X-Dales 2023 - 6th

Since last year's X-Dales, I passed my pilot's exam on the second attempt, thank you Pete L for all your time and help, and I have embraced the Northern Challenge Series and Northern Challenge Trophy Hike and Fly with great enthusiasm, to the extent that I'm only allowed into the hills if I can assure the kids that I will collect some points!

On Thursday 20th June, Grasmere's Sports & Showground started filling with tents and the sky was full of gliders enjoying the smooth buoyant air on the neighbouring Rydal Fell.  As the forecast was so windy for the following day, I squeezed in a couple of top to bottoms later on, one just after sunset, beautiful.

At the briefing, the 1-dayers (Academy event) received wonderful news,they were moving our event to Saturday, we had a day off.  The 2-dayers (Hardcore event) had a day of hiking followed by a wet night for those choosing to camp out.  The overall format of the event was slightly different this year, the Lakes Charity Classic (LCC) was running on the same two days rather than being slightly off-set.  With 75 LCC pilots in the A&B competitions, 17 in the coaching group and 46 X-Lakes pilots from all over the country, and world, there was a good buzz.

Last year's H&F Tri-nations holder, Westie, was running the competition and gave Friday night's briefing - "it is going to be thermic tomorrow, you're going to have great flights".  No rules were set this year, we could go wherever we wanted to bag as many of the 214 Wainwrights as possible.  10 points for the central ones, 20 points for the ones further away with a 10 point penalty for every 10 mins late arrival after 4pm, no points if arriving after 5pm.

 The forecast was westerly starting with north in it moving to more south in it and the wind strengthening as the day progressed.  RASP forecast 2 stars in the eastern fells.  Westie said we wouldn't need to worry about the sea breeze.  There was a little apprehension amongst the pilots, Westie's view on it not being too windy would be different to ours!

Yellow = 10 points, Red = 20 points

The 2-dayers had to stop at 9pm, 13 hours on the hoof for most of them, although one or two managed a hair-raising ridge run early in the day.  The top pilots/athletes/fell runners were on c500 points bagging c30 Wainwrights, very impressive, especially carrying all their kit.  Unfortunately X-Alps star guest Kinga Masztalerz woke up on Saturday morning full of an English cold and had to withdraw, she was gutted not to put the glider in the air and fight for the trophy, she still finished 15th out of 33 though!

8am, the hooter goes, we're off.  Everyone heads to the neighbouring west facing row of 15 Wainwrights, which includes Helvellyn.  It is c14km long, known as the Milk Run to local pilots.  The athletes split into circa four routes up the slopes.  My general strategy was to head north along the Milk Run then when an opportunity to go over it arose, I’d go towards the higher scoring eastern fells, where there is a long west facing ridge and then somehow get back along the lower fells above Ambleside.  

Grassmere with the Milk Run on the right of road/Thirlmere, start/finish at the showground

Adopting my walking strategy, I was one of the last out of the showground and headed up Stone Arthur.  It was into the wind on the far side and by 9:15 I was first in the air overtaking the fell runners, bagged 3 more Wainwrights and landed just below Dollywaggon Pike, great name!  The slope I’d hoped to be into the wind wasn’t steep enough, so I hiked to the ridge and walked along it.  Looking behind me, Oli Beswick was in the air gaining height and then it occurred to me that I probably had a decent opportunity to go over the back at that point, anyway, that thought didn’t cross my mind at the time and north I went towards my next take off at Helvellyn, which the cloud had now lifted off.  Thermals were starting and whilst I spotted what looked a more appealing place to go over the back below White Side, I thought I’d use this buoyancy to continue along the run first.  On my return, I couldn’t quite push around a hill, so had a touchdown, with a scruffy high wind landing opting for the pulling in as much of one break line as possible option – this wasn’t the time to go over the back, then a short walk with a bunched up glider.  Alex Coltman cruises past me in the air on his return run.

Looking over the back of Helvellyn, Ullswater in the distance

Back in the air, mission go over the back.  I gained as much height as I had patience for then went over at my chosen more rounded spot with the intention of gaining lift off the face of Catstye Cam.  Going over the back was never going to be a smooth ride.  Bobbing about travelling at nearly 30mph across the ground with good height over the valley bottom with the pre-flight briefing message of “be very careful going over the back” echoing around my head quickly resulted in a change of strategy to not test the levels of turbulence on the face of Catstye Cam, space between me and the ground is good.

Landing halfway up the slope to Birkhouse Moor, after scoffing my lunch on the hoof, I stopped to take a layer off.  My sunglasses hit the deck, which I thought - I’ll put them back on when I’ve closed my bag.  

At take off a few 100m later, they weren’t on my head.  School-boy error, 20mins wasted, but I was lucky to find them backtracking on my tracker.  Once found, it was 12:10.  Westie’s last tip to us all, either be in the air or on take off at 12.  No time to waste.  12:30, in the air and heading towards Glenridding, will I land there? Make it over the bottom of Ullswater?  Better than that, some bonus buoyancy took me to the foot of Place Fell which gave me a thermal, we’re into the 20 pointers!  14 x 10 pointers already nabbed.

Lots of specks of wings below this pilot looking south towards Grasmere, probably mostly LCC competitors.

Little did I know, but Oli B and Lucy Scott were already there running around the 20 pointer peaks fighting to top the leaderboard, they went straight over the back to begin with.

The wind was still fairly strong, thermalling, and drifting further east in doing so, was not an option I was keen on, but pressing forward into lift worked and then traversing along the ridge.  Tom Hodgkin, last year’s X-Dales winner, local pilot and had just completed an amazing self-set Bob Graham challenge that will hopefully be in a Skywings shortly hence not competing, was watching the trackers and could see what was about to happen to me.  I was heading for Threshwaite Mouth and unbeknown to me, the sea breeze had arrived.  The wind was no longer a westerly, but a howling southerly through the gap.  Full speed bar on, with tip toes, it seemed like I was descending vertically but by sheer luck, I cleared the mouth and slowly crept away from it.  The lesson learnt there is not to rely on a forecast, but keep an eye out on changing conditions, it was very buoyant leading up to that and the wind direction and speed on my machine started to change, which I presumed it was a little confused as I was speed bar on and off and there may have been clues in the clouds, I wasn’t searching for it.  I was very close to instigating my back up option of turning around, landing wouldn’t have been possible.

L-R, Glenridding, Ullswater, Place Fell

Pressing on, the rest of the Wainwrights on the ridge were bagged then it was the turn for home.  I wasn’t fully concentrating flying up a cloud street and nearly missed Troutbeck Tongue, it is only a little one, then landed next to Kirkstone Pass.  Whilst buzzing from the flight, I must admit that I was ready for some time out of the saddle.  There was then a shout from Oli who was running past on the road.  He clearly had a plan on how to get back.  A moment later, Lucy was waving at me from a field away, we’d all landed in a similar spot.  I saw Oli running up Wansfell, which I presumed must be a logical way to go.  When I got to the top, I couldn’t see him, it turns out he was running all the way back.  I took off and hopped across the valley landing next to a footpath.  I bunched up the glider to get around the corner then set off again and picked up some lucky buoyancy to get me to the foot of Nab Sar, or so I thought.  The wind, unsurprisingly in hindsight, was whistling around the side which resulted in a long bracken landing.

I was chuffed to bits that I’d got this far with 50mins to spare, time for a walk to the top and glide into goal I thought.  Extracting a glider from bracken and then packing it in high winds is not a skill I am close to mastering.  Once packed up in a complete mess, I had 35mins left, so I changed the plan to making it to goal on foot, which I will admit I trotted for part of it to just make sure.  Lucy took a slightly different route and landed around the corner behind me.  She went for the hike to the top and fly in option bagging an extra 10 points.  Oli’s impressive running meant he finished before me and he even bagged another Wainwright.

The top three scores were Lucy on 350, me 340 and Oli 320, but Lucy arrived late and 20 points were deducted.  The other noticeable finisher was Alex in 4th who overtook me early in the day.  Clearly a good pilot, he took off once and landed back at the showground bagging the most Wainwrights in the 1-day competition, 25, but they were all 10 pointers.  He had a couple of hours to spare, but ran out of nearby options.  Stats from my tour – 24 Wainwrights, 2hrs 25 min flying out of 8hrs in 6 flights, 40km flown, 15km hiked, max height c3,500ft.

My track, flying in red, hiking in black

In the 2-day comp, Jacob Aubrey, 2nd in X-Dales 2023, stayed the night in Grasmere, so had a very similar day to the 1-dayers, except could start an hour earlier.  After a run around a few local hills, he flew over the back at the same point as Lucy and Oli and had a field day chomping up 24 x 20 pointers, mostly in the air.  He showed us how it was done scoring 560 points in the day to take overall second place.  James Whitmore, 3rd in X-Dales 2023, finished 10 points behind in third place with 1,000 points in total.  The winner, for the third time, was Keith (Bud) Paterson, who started the day in the western fells, amounted a massive 1,170 points with 75 Wainwrights tagged.  Would Kinga have beaten Bud on his home turf? A debate for another day.  Dave Ashcroft claimed the Vol-Biv trophy (highest placed person who slept under the stars rain clouds).  Bud’s win also meant he was crowned the Tri-Nations Hike & Fly 2024 Champion, well done.

At the presentation, there was also a raffle with what must have been c150 paragliding prizes.  The regulars buy lots of tickets and win lots of prizes, they know what to do!!  In total, the event raised over £6,000 which local charities will apply for a share of, some of whom spoke to say how grateful they were.  Well done Cumbria Soaring Club.

To compare my efforts against the 2-day competitors, say I scored c300 points hiking for 13hrs on Friday, I would have finished 10th.  To top the leaderboard, I think you need to be a fell runner as well as a top pilot.  I lucked out with some flying that kept me in the air long enough, but it is obvious how much more can be done the longer you are in the air.  I suppose fell runners can push the limits as to where they fly.  They know that if they land at say point X, they can be back at base in 2hrs, whereas for me it might be a 3hr hike.  I did take the risk knowing that if I landed later on in my route that I wouldn’t make it back in time, but I thought if I can fly that far in the first place, I would be happy with my day and you’re not going to win playing safe!

All great learning.  I possibly should have turned around to gain more height before my touch down returning to Helvellyn, I should have been more patient to gain more height before going over the back, I did a full pack up when a bunch up would have been a quicker option, look out for the sea breeze more, arrive further in front of a hill where the wind will be whistling around it, etc.

I had a rough plan, which I was stoked that it came off.  Topping the leaderboard was a bonus.  Flying in the Lakes is amazing, but those fells need to be respected.  Bring on X-Dales 2024!

The Rest of the Lakes Charity Classic ‘24

Pete Logan reports

Paul wasn’t the only Dales flyer at the LCC. From memory, we had Rhian who was supporting Kinga Masterlerz on the two day X-Lakes, Ed Cleasby helping with coaching and the B comp, Rob U and Alan O in the B Comp, new member Chris along for the coaching and myself, Sonny and Gary for the A comp.

I made a mad dash there on the Friday morning to register in time after getting back from an XC a little before midnight on the Thursday night. I needn’t have bothered, the wind was strong and would mean we couldn’t put a comp task on safely. Quite a few of us elected for a trip to the beach near Millom to see if some coastal flying was possible. There’s a continuous SW facing crumbly red (friable Triassic desert sandstone if you’re into rocks https://geologyviewer.bgs.ac.uk/ ) cliff that can be flown for several kilometres in smooth sea air. A group of fifteen or so of us convoyed off there, trailing Simon from the Pennine club who knew the site well. After some faffing about on the beach (thanks Alan and Chris for sorting that) I launched first and with the grace of an Albatross on land took to the air. There was just enough to stay up and not too strong and off to the south that you couldn’t make it back at trim. If you go there yourself, there are wonderfully crinkly bits of cliff which make for interesting viewing and little pulses of extra lift.

Simon and then Sonny joined me in the air after a while and we had a pleasant 30 minutes or so of glassy lift. I did wonder why we didn’t have more people in the air, more on that later. A fourth wing joined us low down on the tall cliffs but I lost sight of it as I turned to fly another beat. Curious I flew over to where it would have been and saw the pilot down in a hollow lying on the grass with a couple of beach walkers running over.

Simon was already in the process of landing to assist followed by Sonny. I tried to top land so I could walk down and still have a phone signal at the top but during our time in the air the wind had gone off further to the south and the best I could have done was “arrive rapidly” near the top amongst lambs. A bottom landing was the better option, I could still scramble to the top for signal if needed. Our pilot had bounced downwind off a low down slope and then pitched into a fence post as the wing collapsed behind him. Pain in the ribs, back and hips but with full movement and no sign of shock or internal bleeding, he was walking wounded. The first aid course Simon and I had been on last winter proved useful in us being confident we didn’t need to bother mountain rescue. We walked him off to a waiting van and onto hospital for a check over of what turned out to be bruising and a cracked rib. BTW this is a great time to mention Rob Urselman’s first aid course which is in the planning stage right now. Contact him for more information.

Meanwhile, back at take off…. The combination of beach launch, manoeuvre up the slope and TO in wind off to the south had caused all manner of hapless incidents. Two wings draped over barbed fences, a frontal while ground handling causing the leading edge to clonk a pilot on the forehead and an involuntary take off half way up the slope causing a tumble down the slope. I’ve got some notes below in the Coaching section on improving beach launches. Feel free to have a look through.

Day Two brought less wind, but certainly not none. After the One Day X Lakes athletes were dispatched we all hiked up from the Gramere camp to Alcock Tarn for a task set and briefing.

The B Comp was given a cats cradle task, up, down and then up the Helvellyn Ridge. The A Comp was given a turnpoint out in the valley, then Helvellyn, after which it was onto to Appelby in Westmorland on the far side of the Eden Valley with dire warnings not to throw ourselves over the back into the St Sunday Crag / Grisedale Valley. Apparently it’s a bit gnarly there unless you’re straight lining it at base. Go south or north of it was the advice.

Most of the A Comp pilots walked further up Heron Pike but Murphman and I decided to stop about a third the way up as the wind was already top end. Most other pilots did the same and we took off early in the task in order to be in the air if it did start to blow out. In the end the wind on TO wasn’t a concern so we soon had the best part of sixty gliders on two tasks adding to the X Lakes one dayers already in the air. What a sight!

I tagged the opening cylinder above Grasmere early after a climbout where I didn’t even have to try. Half the air above Heron Pike was just taking you up to cloudbase. Then topped up some height and north following the line of Dunmail Raise onto Seat Sandal. I reached it with a bit of height wondering if I’d made the right decision as there was someone struggling low on it. It was fine - I took a bitty climb that got me onto Dolleywaggon and a great view of walkers and hike and flyers setting up below.

The Helvellyn cylinder was also an easy tag but then the first fly in the ointment made itself known. Lots of easy lift does make for plenty of cloud and that had lowered base so that I wasn’t comfortable popping through the gap of Helvelly peak and the cloudbase 200m or less above it. Plus we were now in ridge lift as the clouds had shaded out the Thirlmere valley. Some milling about then started for a lot of us. I decided that I should head back south as there’d been good climbs there before and the lower edges of Willie Wife Moor were steep. This worked and I topped out at base and a little extra so I had to bar it out front with some ears in to get into better visibility.

I had the height now and used it to get further north. The peaks drop down there which means more space between them and cloudbase as you go over the back to Appelby. There were gliders to the north too and I sped past Helvellyn again, Raise, Stybarrow Dodd and Watson’s Dodd to reach Great Dodd. The sun was on the ground below at St John’s in the Vale so it was just a matter of time until we (Sonny was with me at this point I think) had our ticket off the ridge and onto the next challenge.

A gaggle of five of us at least left base heading for Ullswater. We thinned out as we stopped for what we thought was useful lift, each knowing that as you leave high ground staying conservative with your height is the best strategy. I had another glider with me as we got to Gowbarrow Fell which would have been the next logical place to climb. There were three gliders after Gowbarrow though in an absolute stonking thermal just getting hooked in and elevated up. Full bar to get there before it disappears and also careful to head for the upwind side of this one. The glider with me took a track that put them solidly downwind of the thermal and easily lost 1500ft of height gain compared to me. I didn’t question it too much but the source of this one was probably a couple of small fields by the lakeside in the wind shadow of Gowbarrow. That’s not exactly in the textbook but I wasn’t questioning it as I was back at base and heading across Ullswater to Pooley Bridge.

Barton Fell was in shade and 90 degrees off the wind so it was an easy decision to go for the sun on ground to the east of Pooley Bridge. Two of the gliders that I’d seen climbing there had headed this way and I soon joined them in a climb here. All is good, I’m early at base and with some other gliders to spread the load of finding climbs. I find a fairly crappy sustaining climb at Lowther Castle before the M6 and try to call the lead glider back, but they’re too far away. The other one joins me and we decide it’s not worth the time. The lead glider has found something over a solar farm on the far side of the M6. This is what the doctor ordered but oddly the yellow glider leaves me after a while and presses on getting quite low whilst I take this one back to base, it only needed a little patience.

The problem now appears to be that the ground is shaded out all the way to Appelby and my height isn’t enough to glide it in. And now for some actual coaching:

Expanding on the issues at Silecroft (LCC article above), it was clear that the nature of the site, beach launch, short steep slope, wind 20 degrees off, were an issue for a lot of the pilots there. However, this represents an opportunity for improvement which will be great for the rest of your ground handling.

Dutch pilots who go dune gooning are well practised at this. Almost all Dutch “hill” launch sites are on the west coast. I was lucky enough to try beach launches before because I occasionally fly Formby dunes on the Sefton Coast. The trick is to launch the wing on the beach and kite up the slope until you’re at a sufficient height to take off and for the wind up the slope to sustain you. Sounds like a tall order but it can be broken down into smaller problems.

1 - Launch the wing and hold it over your head.

This shouldn’t be an issue. We’re on a flat surface and although the wind is usually strong, since we’re at the coast flying small cliffs or hills, it is steady and non turbulent. Use some of your ground handling outings to just hold the wing above you for a minute or five minutes. At least until you don’t have to look at it to be able to hold it above you.

2 - Kite up the slope.

This is the part where you get to really improve your GH on our bigger hills! Kiting back up a slope, balancing the pressure of your wing and the rate of ascent up the slope is useful to have in all sorts of circumstances. You can continue a side landing by walking the rest of the way to the top of the hill without having to posy up. You can move around obstacles on launch. You can choose where to move to drop your wing if the ground behind is going to lead to a tangle etc.

You have two methods:

        A - With braking, drop your wing to one side, shown in the photo above.

Oppose that force with your hips and weight and fine tune and correct on the opposite brake to keep your lower tip from dragging on the ground. It becomes a bit like sailing or windsurfing at this point. You’re the keel and the wing is now providing a force you can use to ascend (crab up) the slope at a shallow angle. Be mindful that as you ascend, you partially unweight the wing so it will want to drop. Be ready on your top brake to keep the wing from dragging as you walk up. The downsides are that it could ensnare your wing tip in an area it’s difficult to deal with. You also need a slope that’s amenable to shallow ascent. Rocks, clumps of grass, fences that limit sideways movement are all going to be issues.

        B - Walk backwards up the hill.

Brake both sides of the wing to do three things at once; keep it pressured/inflated, take weight from your feet to allow easy ascent, drop the glider a little further back to pull you back onto slope. It can be a fine balance but does have the advantage of keeping your wing safely above you and being a shorter length of ground to cover. The downsides are that you’re (like Spike Milligan*) walking backwards, potentially on sand with less grip than you’d normally have. Being confident about dropping your wing back a little to provide that grip and impetus up the slope on uneven ground is a must.

3 - Nearly there, launch time.

Since you’re at a small cliff or dune site your lift band is narrow. If the wind is slightly off, it’s narrower still. Take off sideways along the line of lift rather than straight forward, which would then necessitate a turn right after take off, whilst out of the lift band. You’ll know you’re good for take off by how light you are on your feet by this point.

On the day at Silecroft, shown above, there was a tricky conundrum. The wind was about 20 degrees off to the south but the cliffs and lift got bigger to the north. Usual TO is at the start of Coteley Bank, having walked in from Rayrick. Coteley can be ascended on foot but is smaller. By the time you get to Walkhole Bank, it’s steep and the height of the slope is likely to have driven the wind 90 degrees off at the bottom - meaning no lift at all unless you’re high on them. BTW there is a top take off at Silecroft but our local expert said that if there’s enough wind to fly the site it’s often too strong to TO at the top and quite rotary.

I opted to immediately take off right into the downwind leg which means I’m fast over the ground but heading to higher cliffs. My safety if I sunk out being a turn into wind to land at low speed on the beach. Taking off into wind meant heading for shallower slopes and then a turn onto the downwind leg, potentially being lower down the slope which then forces a faster beach landing. The pilots that flew, elected for both on the day.

You don’t need a long drive to a coastal site for getting some practice in. Five or ten minutes on the steep section just in behind Knipe Scar would be a great area or on a strong wind day, football pitches at local parks often have a terrace bank so that the field can remain flat on a slope. Learn to ascend that bank by one of the methods above. Then feel how take off differs when the wind is off. Who knows? If the wind is strong enough, you may even find a new soaring site close to home 🙂

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Pod Life

Last year I bought my first pod harness.  For most people the reasons often stated are for increased warmth and glide performance.  I was happy to accept these benefits, although my primary objective was different from most others in that I don’t like looking at my feet when high up!

This brief article is to draw attention to what I’d wished I’d been informed about when flying a pod harness for the first time.  That is:

  1. When launching, you should lean forward in order to stretch out your legs in an initial downward motion to then lever your body into the harness.  As opposed to initially leaning back into an open harness.
  2. For stability when flying, it is advisable to tuck your legs under you to increase the ‘pendulum’ effect.  When under stress for many people, the natural reaction is to push forward with straight legs yet this is far less stable.  Noting that before doing any SIV manoeuvres the first step is to tuck in your legs for increased stability.
  3. It is advisable NOT to move to a pod harness and change wings at the same time.  I didn’t do this and can only comment that I changed wings this year, 1 year and several flights with a pod.  I’m very glad of this given that for me, the new wing has been initially more challenging and noticeably more dynamic in flying.

In the last year I’ve talked with several flyers who have also recently started using pod harnesses and discovered that they were not aware of these recommendations.  Indeed I only learnt them from advice given by highly experienced instructors on different trips.  Understandably it’s not the seller’s remit to impart flying advice - even if it is critical for safety.  Therefore, I hope this brief note helps any club flyers either thinking about acquiring, or have recently acquired a pod harness.  There are other considerations regarding flying in a pod regarding the potential for increased yawing and how much of a supine versus upright sitting position is desirable. Given the wider complexities and individual nature of harnesses and flyers, it’s advisable for a new pod user to discuss further flying characteristics with an experienced coach and/ or instructor.

BONUS TIP:  Attaching a small carabiner connecting the foot strap to the boot lace of the ‘stretch leg’ for getting into the harness, ensures that it stays on foot and doesn’t slip off.  - Courtesy of Pete Logan.

Pete: Clive asked that I throw in my two pennies as chief coach…

Getting the right harness is often like those hermit crabs from the documentaries swapping shells. You know when you’re in the right one. It feels secure and most things fall to hand.
Regarding safety, legs under your kaboose should be a natural reaction anytime something is going wrong with the wing, whatever harness you’re in. Modern pods seem to keep your torso fairly upright which helps to stop risers twisting. I’ve very occasionally had issues with lines running down to the footboard. If they loop over themselves it can leave you with a lot of weight shift applied to one side. It’s all part of the pre-flight prep and check, which a pod makes more time consuming. I think it’s worth it because a warm and comfortable pilot is free to concentrate on the flying.
