Your Club Needs You! New First Aid Committee Role

Joseph Edmonds reports

The committee, after careful consideration, have decided that first aid is something that we all agree is of vital importance to the well being of club members as a whole. However, becoming trained in first aid, especially focussed for paragliding, can seem complex and potentially expensive.

We have now decided that the club should actively organise and promote first aid training. The general idea is that the club would be willing to contribute a percentage towards training costs for members. The exact details are to be confirmed, as is exactly which training course or provider we should engage with.

What we are looking for now is a new person to join the committee with the goal of organising first aid training and coordinating funding to generate the maximum amount of benefit to the club as a whole. The committee is willing to allocate substantial funds to the venture, and there is also the possibility that this person might decide to try to get increased funds by setting up a system of voluntary contribution so that members who may not feel like doing the training themselves can at least chuck a bit of cash towards the effort.

The more people we have in the club as trained first-aiders, the better the chance that there will be a trained first-aider in the vicinity should you be in the unfortunate circumstance of needing one. This is a risky sport and anything we can do to help to minimise the risk and improve outcomes when things do go pear shaped has to be a really good thing -   hope you all agree!

If you have a bit of spare time and a willingness to pick up this challenge and make a real difference to the club, please get in touch with me or any other committee member so we can discuss further.

Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

I’m just back from a 10-day trip to Morocco.  I normally meet up with a group of ex-military mates at this time of year for a bit of flying and quite a lot of drinking.  In the past we went to Hotel California in southern Spain, but prices have risen significantly and one of our group was rather spooked by the conditions (in May) last year, so we thought that we’d try something a bit more mellow.  (I can already feel myself slipping into the category of what Joseph would call Retired Ridge Soarers!)  I was more than happy to jump on the bandwagon and try a country that I’ve never been to before.

There were 10 of us in total.  Half of us had never flown in Morocco before, but the remainder, of which 4 were instructors, were happy to show us the ropes.  Most of the participants are soft southerners so the group decision was SleasyJet flights from Gatwick to Agadir.  The pain of the 4-hour drive from God’s own country down south was more than balanced by a bed for the night courtesy of a fellow participant who drove us to Gatwick.  He works in the airline industry so we also benefited from free parking and a VIP package.  Nice to see how the other half (premier league players and pop stars) live.

Night driving isn’t the best introduction to Morocco.  The main roads are pretty good, but not well lit.  This made it tricky to spot the pedestrians crossing the road, trucks without lights and mopeds riding on the wrong side of the road.  Our first couple of days were unseasonably hot: 36°C, but then it dropped to the low 20s which was a lot more comfortable.  It also rained on a couple of days, which we were told is unusual in late March.  We flew 8 days out of 9 and my instruments tell me that I amassed 13½ hours of airtime.

We stayed at the Hotel Abertih in Mirleft – the perfect venue.  It’s about a 90-minute drive south of Agadir.  It’s owned by Frenchman Damien Lecire, who is a BGD team pilot (Bruce Goldsmith and his wife were there for Bruce’s birthday) who speaks excellent English and is happy to advise you on where to go flying each day.  If the forecast looks good, he’ll probably join you.  The rooms are fairly basic with options for ensuite or shared bathrooms.  I opted to stay in the main complex, but some of our party stayed in rented apartments, 30 seconds walk away, which benefited from a very cold pool and air-conditioning.  Our package included half board and hire cars.  The staff were really helpful and friendly and the set evening meal was delicious.

Mirleft itself has plenty of shops and restaurants, although it’s probably best not to be too adventurous when it comes to eating out.  One of our party, who had been there the week before, had eaten in a back street establishment and suffered with an upset stomach for a few days.  The hotel was walking distance from the ruins of Fort Tidli, a relic of a time before Moroccan independence, when the French and Spanish were vying for overseas territory.  The beach, and a scenic cliff top walk are about the same distance away.  Sadly, the natural beauty of the place is spoiled by litter – rubbish everywhere.

The coast can be very busy in summer when Moroccans flock there to escape the intense heat inland.  The coast is popular with surfers, but I got the impression that there are strong rip tides waiting to catch out the unwary.

Morocco is a Muslim country so alcohol is only served in hotels.  Our visit also coincided with Ramadan so the staff were not allowed to serve us during daylight hours.  They were, however, delighted to show us where the fridge was, and invited us to help ourselves!

Yours truly flying past Eagle’s Nest.  Credit Pete Gallagher.

We flew at 3 sites: Nigel’s (20min drive), Aglou (40min) and Tifnit (75min).  All are probably best described as coastal sites, although Nigel’s is a 2km and 1,000’ glide away from the beach.  Nigel’s is a big, easy gravel take-off, with a café, car parking and toilets.  It’s ideal for top landing as long as you are reasonably confident of not landing short on the buildings, vehicles and cacti.  There is also an easy bottom landing field.  There is a £2.50 per day flying fee.  Across the road, on the same ridge, is Eagle’s Nest which has accommodation, restaurant, concrete take-off and infinity pool.  I met up with Dales club member John Garrett, who was staying there with a group of Derbyshire pilots being hosted by Tim King.  The flying there was part ridge soaring and part thermic.  It did get busy in the air in marginal conditions.  The XC challenge was an out and return to Aglou, but I chickened out when I got too close to a real eagle’s nest and got dive bombed by a concerned parent.

Aglou.  Note the 8 wheel drive monster parked up.  He wasn’t about to get bogged in.

We only flew at Aglou on one day.  I admit to being a bit intimidated by the limited beach available for bottom landing, especially since a Czech pilot had drowned there the week before. (We had been concerned that, following a fatality, the police would stop flying across the region.  They did prevent pilots taking off late in the day, but it didn’t affect us.)  In the end it was easily soarable, if a tad windy on take-off.  The views were stunning.  I don’t think that I have even flown in such a photogenic location.  One of our group was flying tandem and his wife was a dab hand with a camera.  The perfect set up for obtaining photos of yourself flying – something that I think we are all vain enough to crave.

Aglou.  A Czech pilot drowned here the week before.

We spent a couple of days at Tifnit, near a huge desalination plant with lots of associated fruit and vegetables growing in the fields nearby.  The scenery was just as good as Aglou and conditions made it perfect for practising take-off, top (spot) landings and wagga.  Like Aglou, the drive to take-off is through some dunes and care is required not to get bogged in.  There was some sort of desert rally going on whilst we were flying and it was great fun to ‘race’ the cars along the route.

Tifnit.

I had a bit of an accident on day 2.  No, not flying (or drinking)!  I was trying to walk forwards down a steep embankment when both feet shot out from beneath me and I landed on my coccyx.  I was badly winded and (I think) bruised my ribs.  I was worried that might be the end of my flying trip but, with the aid of ibuprofen, I managed to soldier on.  If you haven’t seen me on a UK hill recently, that’s because I’m waiting for my ageing body to heal first.  It only hurts when I laugh!

Would I go again?  Yes, probably.  The flying is easy and relaxing, so ideal if you’re a novice or just want to get current after a long winter lay-off.  Not for the aspiring XC pilots, and pod harnesses are entirely superfluous.  I believe that there are inland sites, but we didn’t visit any.  Morocco is cheap.  Our package (half board and cars) cost about £34 per day.

The combination of sun, salt and sand doesn’t do much for your wing.  My 10-year-old Rush 4 was already on its last legs (Skywords Mar 23) so I left it with Damien, who promised to pass it on to a member of the local club.  He assured me that they would squeeze another few years out of it!

Farewell to my trusty Rush 4.  After 10 years I’ve decided that I quite like the colours.

Morocco can be quite bureaucratic.  We had to send in some forms 90 days ahead of arrival, but never got a response.  Unless you speak Arabic, French is your best chance of communication with the locals. You can’t obtain local currency until you get there.  The cashpoints at the airport charge a fee, but it’s as well to have some change in your pocket for tipping (Euros will do), and ‘negotiating’ with the police if you get stopped whilst driving.  My phone provider covers the EU but not Morocco and my phone is too old to cope with an e-sim.  I could have bought a local sim card but in the end I didn’t bother.  It was quite nice to leave my phone in the hotel and just check messages once re-acquainted with the wifi.

Diversity, Equity and Inclusion in Paragliding

Rhian Ebrey reports

Last night, I presented at the last club night of the season on diversity, equity and inclusion (also known as DEI, or EDI).  As commonly the only female in the room or on the hill, people frequently come over to chat to me.  Inevitably, the conversation turns to why aren’t there more women in paragliding and what can we do about it?  So I thought I’d do something about it.  

Here’s the summary of my presentation (video version also soon to be available on the DHPC youtube channel):

Diversity covers the variety of identity traits that make us all unique: gender, ethnicity, culture, age, sexual orientation, religion, neurodivergence, social class, disability, political opinions… the list goes on.  However, we are all acutely aware that the paragliding community isn’t very diverse.  

Thanks to some digging in the BHPA statistics, in 2022 the average age of BHPA members is 51 and female participation has raised from 6% in 2022 to 7.5% in 2023.  This is similarly reflected in other EU countries, although there appears to be a potential correlation between age and female participation rates (ie, younger average ages tend to also have a greater proportion of women engaging in paragliding.  I’m not a mathematician though, so don’t quote me on correlation vs causation to know if this is true).  But these figures don’t reflect the general population breakdown of the UK, so why is this?  To understand why, we need to understand intersectionality.  This explores how a person’s identities, such as their gender, ethnicity, sexuality etc, affects their access to opportunities and privileges.

When exploring why there are fewer women participating in sport, this insightful video “Run Like a Girl” highlights that society teaches girls from a young age that women aren’t as good as men, and sport isn’t for them. This can be hard for women to identify this narrative and challenge it for themselves.  

The book “Eat Sweat Play” further examines how society has alienated women from sports if you want to read more into this.  And this is having a big impact.  Numerous organisations have found that women’s participation in sport is much lower than for men, with Women in Sport reporting that only 30% of parents believe playing sport is very important for their daughters, compared with 41% of parents for their sons.  Today, they released statistics that 4 in 10 elite sportswomen earn less than £10,000 a year, 1 in 4 have received social media abuse of a sexual nature, and 3 in 4 have experienced sexism in sport.  This Girl Can reported in 2014 that 1.75 million fewer women were exercising regularly compared to men, with reasons ranging from body confidence, to not being good enough, to being told their priorities should be different, including prioritising family.  This is exacerbated for women of colour, and the Muslimah Sports Association found that while 97% of surveyed British Muslim women want to increase their participation in sport, 43% of current sports facilities don’t meet their needs, including having women’s only spaces and facilities.  This reflects my work, in which exclusively Muslim outdoor community groups reached out to me, keen to know how they can get involved in paragliding, but a potential lack of female instructors and tandem pilots could be a barrier to some Muslim women.

The British Mountaineering Council (BMC) produced this video, Outdoors for All to highlight the range of challenges for different groups from accessing the outdoors.  These range from having caring responsibilities, relationship with parents, mental and physical health, access to correct equipment, vehicle access, and seeing other people like you in the outdoors.  This is all before we even get to the difficulties of getting into paragliding, which, as we all know, is expensive, the weather isn’t great and it’s a hidden sport.  My research of non-paragliding outdoor communities, though, returned one unanimous question: is it safe???  This indicates that some basic understanding of paragliding and risks (such as the passive safety of EN A gliders) can help to overcome this barrier.

DEI stands for diversity, equity and inclusion, but what does equity mean?  Equity is different from equality.  Equality provides everyone the same resources to help everyone equally.  However, equity acknowledges that different people will have different needs and barriers to get to the same start point.  Equity therefore provides resources proportionally to people’s needs to allow everyone to be the same.  In the picture below, equality provides everyone with a box to allow them to see over the fence to watch the game, regardless of whether they need it or not, and it still doesn’t allow everyone to see over the fence.  Equity provides more boxes to the person who needs it the most, allowing everyone to see the game.

In order to be inclusive, we really need to explore our own culture.  A live poll on the night, along with the same survey to the UK women’s paragliding group, revealed that there is a problem with discriminatory/ inappropriate/ abusive language and behaviour in our clubs, with similar results yielding for both groups, although with a smaller sample size for DHPC attendees.  

The results on the left are the DHPC results from the night; right are the women’s results:

  1. Have you ever heard or seen (not directed at you) discriminatory/ inappropriate/ abusive language or behaviour:

  1. Have you ever had discriminatory/ inappropriate/ abusive language or behaviour directed at you?

  1. Have you ever received unsolicited advice?

  1. Have you ever felt unwelcome or not included?

  1. Do you feel like you have similar peers in your club(s) that you can relate to?  This could be age/ gender/ ethnicity/ sexuality etc.

  1. How big of a problem do you think discrimination/ inappropriate/ abusive language and behaviour is in paragliding?

  1. Have you thought about quitting paragliding as a direct result of lack of inclusion/ abuse?

From the female paragliders survey, a number of serious incidents of sexual and abusive behaviour were shared, which I’m happy to report that the BHPA responded to the most serious case and now has a safeguarding policy in place (see what is safeguarding?).  However, these incidents have reportedly also come from people in positions of authority: instructor/ coach/ committee member.  

There have also been challenges reporting such behaviour, which have either been ignored or required the victim to move/ get other members to address the issue.  Overall, while clubs are generally very welcoming, which is particularly noted for female paragliders, a general ‘laddish/ boys club’ culture is widely reported.  Multiple people said that when feedback has been given for inappropriate comments, this has been listened to though.  From the chat, it was recognised that newer pilots are more likely to be excluded.  Several women noted that club committees request female paragliders to join their committees to ‘tick the diversity box’.  However, this disproportionately places a burden on minorities to deliver diversity work.

In order to address the diversity issue, we need to practise inclusion on purpose.  This requires us to actively work to overcome exclusionary systems for different groups.  As written by Ruchika Tulshyan in her book, Inclusion on Purpose, “I urge them to lay down individual defences - and remind them that even if they’re white, male, heterosexual, and financially privileged, it doesn’t mean that we want them to be shamed about their privilege or that we’re attacking them because of their identities.  My focus is always on understanding and dismantling systems of oppression rather than blaming individuals.  The problem isn’t men, it’s patriarchy.  The problem isn’t white people, it’s white supremacy.  The problem isn’t straight people, it’s homophobia.  Recognise systems of oppression before letting individual defensiveness stop you from dismantling them.”

So what can you do about it?

We need to generate a truly inclusive community.  As identified, we need to shift away from accepting a culture of ‘locker room talk’ - call it out and do not let this be acceptable in any form.  Would you want someone to talk about your mother/ daughter/ loved one in the way being discussed?  If not, saying nothing is being complicit and accepting of this behaviour.  If you are someone from within a majority (ie, if you are white, male, heterosexual etc), then you have less to lose from calling out bad behaviour compared to marginalised groups.  Practice inclusion on purpose: become an active ally with a growth mindset, and learn about DEI.  

This is a challenging and uncomfortable topic for all of us.  We need to be uncomfortable, reflect on what we don’t know, invite feedback, understand that defensiveness doesn’t help us, grow from our mistakes (this is a difficult area to navigate, see this week’s news on the recent Scottish hate crime law as a good example of this) and expect that changes are going to take time.

We also need leadership from everyone in positions of responsibility: instructors, committee members, coaches and any other positions of leadership.  Support creating safe spaces to discuss these issues, and facilitating the development of the tools and language we need to do this is paramount, including having a zero tolerance for inappropriate behaviour and mechanisms to support reporting and safeguarding, as necessary.

Other work I’m doing, now with the support of John Westall ‘Westie’, includes outreach and engagement work with outdoor diversity communities to try and promote, inform and inspire future paragliders into this great sport.

If anyone is interested in helping out with this work, please do get in touch with me.

First Attempts at Northern Challenge Series

Paul Rowntree reports

Ever since the Northern Challenge Series was created in 2018, I’ve thought it is a great idea and I’d love to give it a go.  Six years later, I now have an electronic device that can show me what to do in the air. I now know how to use it, and as the kids get older, it seems I am being given more days in the Dales!

On Easter Friday, I uploaded the .gpx file to my machine, changed the cylinders to the required size and I was ready.  It was a breezy start to Saturday on Nappa Scar, but when it dropped a little, I switched the machine on and activated the competition.  After 30 mins of dusting off the winter cob-webs, I thought I should turn my attention to the task.  This required a little more on the job learning how to use my machine in the air but I was away, let’s bag turn point 1.

TP2 was above Askrigg in front of the ridge.  A few 100m away pushing into the wind I was a ridge height and realised that the chances of me bagging it and making it back to the ridge were limited, but you never know how much lift there could be near the bottom.  I bagged it, but sure enough, landed at the foot of the hill.  Some Hike and Fly training walking back to the top, take 2.

Aware that time taken influences the points, I thought perhaps TP1 was set up so that I could simply ridge soar to it and make it back.  I did get TP1, but landed shortly afterwards.  A short walk up a rounded grassy hill was enough to enable me to start my third attempt.

This time there was a little more buoyancy and TP1 was completed with a little more ease, but bagging TP2 above the village required some height.  With a bit of patience, pushing forwards, realising it isn’t enough and retreating until some magical late afternoon thermals started working above the village.  Not only did I make it to TP2, but I also got to cloudbase above it, which was enough for TP3 & TP4.  Back to the village and the thermal, or the next one, was still working.  Not quite as high this time as the day was coming to an end, but enough to make TP5, TP6 and goal by about 10m, miraculously, and more importantly a short walk to the car to make it back in time for a concert.

Admittedly there was a lot of luck, but the NCS lived up to expectations.  There is no pressure yet it provides some focus and a challenge.  Without it, I may have done all my day’s flying without landing and boated about in front of the hill improving my currency, but I wouldn’t have pushed myself and learnt a few more skills.  Back home, plugging the machine into the computer, I clicked upload flight on the NCS webpage and a few seconds later, there was my name in the table with some points that were magically calculated with a picture of my route, as above.  Thank you Ed for all the work in creating it.  For anyone who hasn’t tried it yet, I recommend giving it a go, you get points for just leaving the starting cylinder!  The link to the challenges - https://xcmap.net/index.php?c=Northern%20Challenge%20Trophy&y=2024

Coaching

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

I received a couple of suggestions from pilots who’d attended the 2022 dXCa session to restart in some way. Dave May had moved to France and I’d started a new job which kind of knocked the wind out the the sails - pun intended. Joe Edmonds has agreed to pitch in, so we will find a way to restart these sessions that will be useful to folks who are wanting to speed up their XC learning. The first thing to do is to sign up to the Telegram group below:

https://t.me/+5hVxX1PGUBQ0MDM0 

There’s 30 or 40 people on there already and we’ll sort out where and when we’re meeting via that. In the meantime here’s the original launch article with the intentions of what we wanted to pass on…..

The Dales 10 to 100 Academy (dXCa)

Recently, our Chief Coach Pete Logan and I were talking about the various programs and structures that have developed in our sport to help pilots progress from novice all the way up to world champion. Hopefully your first introduction to the sport will be a school. There are various syllabuses but they all cover the same material and skillset and the student graduates at more or less the same level. In the UK that is CP.

For a long time this was it – pilots left the school at CP level and found themselves on a hill with very little idea of what to do next. Clubs provided what support they could but it was disorganised and ad-hoc. The budding pilot was effectively left to their own devices to learn as best they could. To help fill this void, the BHPA developed the Club Coaching program, the Pilot and Advanced Pilot ratings and more recently the Pilot Development Structure (https://www.bhpa.co.uk/sport/bhpa/welcome.php) to help the progression from CP to Pilot level and beyond.

At the other end of the spectrum, we have the British Paragliding Racing Academy (https://pgcomps.org.uk/?page_id=2064) with the aim to help develop top level competition pilots. Although only started a few years ago, judging from the recent World Championship results, the BPRA certainly seems to be working – First Team and Overall Individual World Champion in Russ Ogden.

But we are missing a program for XC. Flying XC is still very much in the ‘trial and error’ school of learning. Compared to other sports, XC flying is particularly cruel in this regard. It’s not like practicing a penalty kick for example where you can repeat until your leg drops off, take a break then start again. It’s more akin to that same kicking practice but blindfolded –you miss, the session is over, nobody has seen what you did wrong and you don’t know when you can try again. Surely there is a better way to learn.

The Dales 10 to 100 Academy (dXCa)

With this in mind, we hope to put together a program to support pilots develop their XC skills with an emphasis on flying XC from the Dales and the surrounding sites. From the first 10km flight to the ultimate goal of achieving a 100km XC flight. It will be called the Dales 10-to-100 Academy or DXCA for short.

It’s an experiment of sorts and we have yet to figure out the format. It needs to be more a collaboration of like-minded pilots than a classic classroom experience.

There are many factors that come into play to fly XC well and we will try to cover them all. The challenge is not a lack of information – there is no shortage of articles, videos and books written on the subject. And we also have a wealth of expertise in the club as there is a very active XC scene here. The challenge rather is to tap into this font of knowledge, simplify it out into usable chunks and to make it stick in the mind for when the need arises. For example, the Speed-to-Fly theory is perfectly valid but how much use is it to a pilot just starting out on their XC journey?

From my own limited experience of flying XC and analysis of my flights, comparing them to others flying from the same site, on the same day and along similar lines, one factor eclipses all others: decision making. Your ability to climb to base, the class of wing you fly, what instruments you use or even the conditions on the day come a far second. Short of strapping a motor to your back, if you can’t make good decisions consistently you’ll very quickly find yourself back on the ground.

Which I often do. I get to base without a clear idea of where to go next – no plan A, let alone B or C. It only takes 2 poor decisions to get from base to ground – the first leaves you out of position and low. Then under pressure, you miss a potential save and land. Personally, I hope to work on my decision making this year. Perhaps it’s the same for you, perhaps not.

Although the dXCa will be open to everyone, given the focus on XC it would be best if you can satisfy the following prerequisites. It might seem a lot but you can practice all of these without leaving the hill – some without even leaving the ground:

  • You should be Pilot rated or intending to be Pilot rated. The Pilot exam covers Meteorology, Airlaw and Flight Theory
  • Can you launch and land safely (in tightish spots)? Do more Ground Handling and make each landing a spot landing practice.
  • Some weather forecasting ability. At the very least be able to read conditions and terrain in flight so you can decide when and where it might be prudent to land.
  • Are you comfortable under your wing and have you developed your active flying skills? Did you think it was a bit rough some days? What did others think? Does your wing collapse, even little tip collapses? Do you feel your wing moves around a lot where for others it seems solid overhead?
  • How does your thermalling measure up? Can you consistently get into the top half of the stack when out soaring?
  • Spend time hanging in your harness at home to make sure it is comfortable and set up as you like it.
  • Do you understand how your instruments work and can you enter a task and navigate? If you use apps such as XcSoar or XcTrack, spend time understanding them. Try personalising the display to suit your needs rather than just adopting the default setup. Of course the default setup might suit you fine, but make it a conscious decision. Even if you don’t use much of what is available you can learn a lot just by understanding what they do.
  • A great way to practice navigation is to attempt the NCT tasks (https://www.xcflight.com/northern-challenge-series-2024/ - thanks Ed)

Finally, here’s a rough outline of the topics we hope to cover, in no particular order. They are designed to be more discussion points than lecture topics. In fact, we don’t plan on making this lecture based. There will be discussion/presentation whilst there and homework / analysis before and after. We’ll be discovering what makes useful guidance stick in people’s minds.  

Decision Making

  • Conservation, height, speed, time.
  • Speed to fly
  • Leaving climbs
  • Low saves
  • Lines on glide
  • Leaving the hill

Observation

  • Ground Speed
  • Sources, triggers
  • Instruments
  • Visualising air
  • Cloud shadows

Skills

  • Thermalling
  • Team working/buddies: on the ground & in the air.
  • Weather, aerology, energy of the day. 
  • Head / thoughts in the right place.
  • Confidence. Willing to leave guaranteed air and friends.
  • Radio comms - thermal calling, avoiding committee flying. 
  • Exploring out. 
  • Hunting as a pack for the climbs out. 
  • 2nd thermal en route e.g. sitting above Park Rash.
  • Consolidation at 3 or 4 thermals and planning for the day - airspace, coast, sea air.

Gear - largely irrelevant, they all glide similarly when heading downwind. Just be happy, trustful and comfortable in it / with it. All of our wings are way more comfortable and performant than the generation used to do the first 100k flights from the Dales.

How does all of this get simplified to be usable in the air?

  • In the UK stay in the air! You’ll get blown there in the end.
  • Take every climb to the top.
  • Do not give up because of lack of patience.
  • Be ready and flying early in the day.  
  • Have intent about your day - a goal in mind. 
  • Reliable thermal triggers at hills / house thermals.
  • Talk to yourself. It focuses your mind on what you're doing.

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Careful what you wish for…

I mentioned to Pete Darwood that for whatever reason Cross Country couldn’t process my Worldpay payment and therefore, my subscription got cancelled.  Nor could I access the digital version.  Little did I know that he’d then drive round to my house with 5 years back issues!  Hence the reference here to the Jewish proverb of ‘careful what you wish for’.

Feeling beholden to not be ungrateful, it’s taken me several months to gradually work my way through the magazines - with this photo of a much reduced initial pile.  By admission, I didn’t read every page but scanned through each one, reading relevant safety, flying techniques and the weather articles.  

What have I learnt from 5 years of Cross Country magazines?

  1. The length of a glider's brake lines is correlated to the EN class of glider.  With D class gliders having much shorter travel to input than A class gliders.  *
  2. The average life of a glider is 500 hours - double that if counting ground-handling hours.  Noting that ground-handling has an affect on glider age since the process of unpacking and re-packing is when a glider is experiencing a considerable amount of wear and tear.  Lightweight gliders have a lesser shelf-life but still have a good level of hours use.  Note: this is sourced from one article and manufacturer guidelines on age and maintenance should be followed.
  3. As a glider is used and ages, the C lines tend to shrink whilst the A lines can expand.  If so, the angle of attack becomes greater, plus if the brake lines have also shrunk, there is a risk of hitting the stall point unwittingly. Knowing this likelihood of changes to line lengths it’s evidently prudent to get a glider serviced regularly.
  4. I’ve become much better at identifying a make of glider through noticing the different design signatures. A bit of fun here and it’s incredible how many manufacturers there are for a niche sport.
  5. I didn’t read one bad review of a new glider.  It takes a lot of reading between the lines to identify what glider to buy to suit one’s own flying needs - outside of the obvious class and glider measurements.  Some form of score - even if subjective or, plus points and any negatives/ considerations would be useful - akin to car magazine product reviews.
  6. One old Bruce Goldsmith article mentioned how glider performance  had improved significantly with aspect ratio increasing by 0.5 within the B class of gliders.  Interesting and remains to see how far this could increase even more in the future with performance improvements within the same defined passive safety.  The advances in glider design and materials afford tremendous benefits in these areas.
  7. Many of the weather articles were interesting but for me somewhat technically advanced and I’d have benefited more if there were some that were oriented to basic observations and interpretations.
  8. Reaction times extend with age of the pilot.  Plus eyesight degrades with age.  Approximately it’s a couple of extra seconds to react for a sixty year old compared to a peak at aged twenty-four.  A considerable lag when thinking about the 2 second rule for driving a car or, needing to react to a collapsed wing.
  9. It’s amazing to see where paramotors have flown.  Appreciating that this is in praise of the ‘darkside’, however, their ability to fly in areas that would be either too challenging or out of bounds often results in spectacular photographs of the scenery flown in.  
  10. It’s a very positive community in the sport and highly varied.  Ranging from speed flying, vol biv, epic travel adventures, competition, paramotor, to recreational.  You can go as far you like within the sport whilst still constantly learning to the level you wish.

Thanks Pete for the magazines.  Browsed through over several months in the winter period, keeping my enthusiasm going, and a source of learning.  I’m sure there’s more that I’ve absorbed than what’s sprung to mind in the above list, although please don’t test me!

Safe flying,

Clive.

* This became very real for me from changing gliders recently. On my first flight in a high B glider I found the brake noticeably more responsive (less travel required) to my previous low B glider.  Even knowing this didn’t diminish the initial shock when first turning and then starting to adjust to it.  From this experience I truly appreciate the advice to only change one thing each flight in order to focus on its differences and not be overloaded.
