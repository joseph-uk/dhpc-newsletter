Chairman's Chat

Following the cancellation of the meeting planned for January, the committee has bounced some ideas around on the forum, and I think that we’ve even managed to make a few decisions.

Both of your newly elected sites’ officers are getting to grips with their new roles.  In a new initiative Tom is adding links from each site page on the website to the NCS/DFC tasks available from that site.  The sites’ tour (see later) that I did last month revealed a couple of errors in our sites’ guide and Tom has those in hand.

Sites officers are encouraged to use their discretion when it comes to communication with new landowners, but as a general principle we have agreed not to contact new owners unless there is a problem. ‘If it ain’t broke; don’t try to fix it.

Travel expenses will remain at 40p per mile for committee members acting on behalf of the club.

In previous years, at the request of the farmers, we have attempted to conduct our annual prize giving at the Farmers’ Dinner.  This (last) year we did it in front of members at the AGM.  We all agree that this is a better forum, to be repeated in future.  Due to the poor lighting in the function room at the Horse and Farrier, the Treasurer’s Report was almost impossible to read on the night.  That said it had been available for several weeks beforehand on the forum.  Most members are probably only interested in the headlines anyway.

Much as I despise social media, I’ve been forced to concede that members prefer instant messaging to the club forum.  To that end we’ve adopted the Telegram group as a formal means of ‘chatting’.  Stef and Pete Logan will act as moderators.  That means you’ve got WhatsApp for flying plans, Telegram for chatting and the Forum for more serious/in depth matters.

About a year ago we were alerted to the fact that our website is vulnerable to hackers.  Alex has confirmed that there is no easy upgrade and that he isn’t able to fix the problem.  We’re faced with either paying to move to a different CMS, or taking the risk (the content is backed up).  Joseph has volunteered to act as project manager and is talking to Alex about how best to proceed.

Joseph has also raised the subject of First Aid courses and kits.  It’s quite a challenging subject because, by definition, First Aid is something that mostly benefits others.  Getting trained and carrying a first aid kit is a selfless activity, and is only really beneficial if adopted by the collective as a whole.  A few years ago, the club did offer a small subsidy towards the purchase of a paraglider related first aid kit: the take up was negligible.  Several committee members have attended first aid courses recently (at their own expense) for comparison purposes.  The difficult bit now is deciding how much of a subsidy to offer.  There is general recognition that currency is important and that we need a long-term strategy.  You will have seen Joseph’s poll on the forum and on Telegram, where you can express your views.

It's a sad fact that there are fewer and fewer active hang glider pilots.  In 2022 only one club member entered a hang glider flight in the league.  Last year only 2 flights were logged, both from the same pilot.  If we continue on this course then we’ll get to the stage where we are awarding a once fiercely contested award for a flop over the back.  This devalues the achievements of previous winners and doesn’t really justify the cost of engraving, or replacing trophies that get lost or broken.  For the 2024 season onwards the committee reserves the right not to award trophies and awards unless a minimum of 3 pilots have submitted qualifying flights.  This applies to all trophies and awards, not just hang gliding ones.  It will be a shame if long established trophies don’t get awarded, but the solution remains in the hands of members: if you want a trophy to continue to be awarded then pull your finger out and log some flights!

The club’s Reserve Repack took place on 14 January and was attended by 23 pilots.  It wasn’t fully booked and we were lucky to break even.  Clive will run a survey of members with a few to obtaining your preferences prior to the next one.

Rumour has it that spring has arrived.  Fly safely,

Martin Baxter

BP Cup

Kevin McLoughlin reports

The BP Cup is a beginner friendly paragliding competition

The UK based paragliding competition for pilots in the non-skygod category. Our aims are – flying, fun and learning – in that order!

Why enter the BPCup?

It is a friendly competition which gives you an opportunity to hone the skills needed to fly XC successfully. You will be assisted to program your instruments and fly competition tasks, learning in the process that flying with other pilots and having a goal makes XC flying so much more enjoyable. If you struggle with the technicalities of instruments, don’t worry, there will be offers of help.

In a competition you will discover that the Meet Director takes care of all the onerous weather forecast interpretation, site choice and task setting, leaving you to just take off and fly the task – simple!

Time permitting there will be a daily task debrief, led by either the Meet Director or a senior experienced competition pilot.

You will have a great time, meeting a lot of fellow pilots while learning an incredible amount of new skills to help make your flying better and safer. If you are tempted but still not sure then take a look at some of our previous years reports, we had such fun.

Coaching

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

I think we can say the flying season has started. The last two Sundays had wings high over the Litton Valley and other sites in the Dales too. I took the opportunity to practise a few things to start getting current again and it got me thinking about how we make our flying challenging and hitting the place where you’re stimulated / interested and wanting to come back for more.

Take time to think about what will work for you; here’s my approach….

I have a new harness. No biggie you think, but I was on an SIV this Autumn where it was highlighted that the spin recoveries I was dealing with would go better if I was flying a full seatboarded harness rather than the lightweight hammock style I had. Furnished with a Niviuk Arrow my challenge is now to relearn how flying feels. I was concerned I would just be blundering through thermals not feeling a thing but if the last two Sundays are an indication just the concentration and questioning of how the air feels different… it seems to be going okay. The stimulation here is that I know I have to change, how can I make the learning go faster.

I met up with a few locals out on Baildon a week or so ago. One offered me a go on his Flow Mullet. If you haven’t come across one of these, they look like a paraglider and then fly like a hang glider. You can swoop at speed and recover height easily. They have a reputation as being a bit full on but that hasn’t stopped them becoming pretty popular. My first impression was that up was down and down was up on the brake / riser pulley combo. A little ground handling helped with that. Then for a little fly - stimulation time 🙂 That certainly engaged me enough for me to be thinking about every move and change.

This is all directed towards the goal of being good enough to do well out in Brazil in October this year. It’s flying that demands technique, currency and a bunch of other things.

For your goals this year, what are the challenges that will get you there?

Flow Mullet on Baildon. Thanks Simon and Stephen.

Sites Tour

Martin Baxter reports

L-R Simon Byrne, Phil Mackereth, John Dean, Nigel Bailey, Alan Oliver, Martin Baxter, John Garrett, Pete Logan, Chris Timson, Andy Braithwaite. Photo: Andrew Nightingale.

Eleven participants in the end. Apart from Pete Logan and Phil Mackereth, mostly CPs who qualified last year. I did this tour a few years ago and it went pretty well, but when we tried it again the following year there were no takers. This year, rather than asking people where they wanted to go on the day, I set a programme in advance so that pilots could dip in and out as they saw fit (a couple met us at Tailbridge rather than driving down to Hawes, only to drive back up to Talbridge again.)

I chose a day in February on the assumption that it wouldn't be flyable. My plan nearly came to grief when it looked as though there might be a brief flyable window at Tailbridge. Indeed it was flyable, but only if you were prepared to launch IMC. That made for quite a challenging site brief!

Having admired the views looking down Swaledale and stopped to marvel at Buttertubs, we had a quick walk up Stags Fell. I've concluded that it is worth briefing novice pilots about the site so that they know the dangers and can judge for themselves when they are ready for it. The wind was beginning to pick up so we had lunch down by the cricket pitch.

Parked in the field at Semer Water and the sun came out. Walking between the shoulder and the top Pete couldn't resist a quick geology lesson; NNW face and back down. The ewes in attendance had clearly been having fun with the ram, but didn't look anywhere close to lambing yet. Need to keep an eye on that one.

     

Blowing a hoolie by the time we got to the turn off to Wether Fell. It wasn't possible to drive up because of the snow drifts so we walked there and back. Fortunately the road to Dodd was clear so it was only a short walk to take-off. The wind there was a bit lighter - probably the only advantage that Dodd has over Wether for a novice pilot. At this point it started raining, which made us realise how lucky we had been with the weather (2 days earlier and all the roads would have been blocked with snow). A couple left at that point and the rest of us adjourned to The Fountain in Hawes for a debrief or two.

I'll probably do the same again next year. It would have been nice to include a few other sites: Brantside, Whernside and Nappa; but they all involve driving or walking or both, and daylight hours are limited at this time of year. My advice to any novice is to walk every site at your own convenience. Identifying parking, route up, take-off, landing and hazards will give you a lot less to worry about when a flying opportunity finally arises.

Martin Baxter, our man on the BHPA,  reports

Photo: Kev Gay receives his BHPA Award of Merit from BHPA Chair Jenny Buck.  Note Kev’s BHGA tie and BHPA badge.  Who knew that Kev actually owned a Jacket and tie?

Citation

Kevin Gay has been a stalwart of the Yorkshire Dales hang gliding scene for many years, a distinctive figure on the hill with his wide-brimmed hat and long hair.  A committed and excellent competition pilot, he is also a Club Coach and a qualified dual pilot with an enthusiasm for demonstrating the appeal of hang gliding in the most direct way possible: by taking people flying.  In the British Open Series a few years ago, on an untaskable day at Wether Fell, Kevin safely conducted repeated dual flights, landing on top, to the delight of passengers and spectators alike.

As well as flying, Kevin has served on the Hang Gliding Competitions Panel for over ten years, including a spell as Deputy Chair, during which time he has been called into service on several occasions to stand in upon the absence of the Chair.  He is always a positive presence on the Panel, not shy to state his opinion but open to the opinions of others, and he has been central to the organisation of the British Open Series for a number of years, in particular in relation to site permissions, airspace compliance and notams.  In 2023, for example, Kevin’s public relations efforts, and open and engaging nature, mitigated the worst effects of inconsiderate actions by some pilots who caused difficulties with local farmers in the Yorkshire Dales.

During 2022 and 2023 health problems have restricted Kevin’s own flying but this has not reduced his capacity for supporting new initiatives in the Panel.  He was an enthusiastic participant in the current efforts to re-focus the British Open Series on supporting and encouraging new and low airtime pilots – an initiative which is already showing positive results.  Kevin supported this initiative both in organisational ways on the Panel and in practical ways as a Club Coach, supporting low airtime pilots on the hill.

Kevin is retiring from the Competitions Panel at the end of 2023, to spend more time with his own glider, but will continue to support the Panel wherever possible, including continuing to manage sites access for the British Open Series when it takes place in the Dales.

We are indebted to Kevin for all the time, effort and enthusiasm he has put into hang gliding competitions, both in his official roles and in his general attitude, approach and generosity, over many years. An award would very appropriately demonstrate and recognise our appreciation of his service.

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Are you a ‘Sayer’?

If you’re a fan of the TV series ‘Peep Show’ you’ll know immediately what a ‘Sayer’ is.  For those that aren’t aware of the term, it came from when David Mitchell’s character met his girlfriend’s family for the first time and he observed that they couldn’t stop making comments or stating random thoughts out loud. In short, they couldn’t stop themselves from ‘saying things’ no matter how irrelevant, uninteresting or more likely both!

You may be asking - how is this related to paragliding?  Well, it occurred to me how some people are more forthcoming at giving feedback to others regarding their flying or safety than others.  Some of us feel more compelled to speak up and say something to another person whilst others may have observed the same person’s actions but decide to hold their council.  This isn’t intended as a critical comment on either group, however, there are serious considerations here with potentially serious implications.

Speaking up could be met with rejection by the recipient.  Plus, if not an officially certified coach is one qualified to give feedback and does this cross over the line to coaching.  Also, in today’s environment what are the legal repercussions between giving feedback and unqualified instruction with potential liabilities?

Against this, if a person doesn’t speak up, how would they feel if the person requiring feedback repeats a dangerous behaviour and this results in a serious accident?

The official line is that only BHPA certified coaches are covered by the BHPA insurance policy to coach other pilots.  Plus, this should be done once permission has been given by the person to be coached.  So where does this leave a non-coach approved pilot if they see something and consider it warrants saying something?

Thinking about this, I can only tell you how I’ve gone about this.  What follows is not intended as professional advice and the reader is responsible for their own words and actions.

Last year I was on a trip and during the course of the week I witnessed one pilot not fully inflate their wing before take off whilst forward launching in many instances.  The guide on take-off either hadn’t noticed or for whatever reason chose not to say anything.  This troubled me given the potential consequences.  How I approached this was whilst sitting next to this flyer by a pool when we were relaxed, I asked them if they were open to feedback.  After they said yes I informed them about their wing not inflating fully and asked if they were aware.  Initially they were a bit defensive, but once they saw that my intent was sincere, I think they took my advice on board.  Thinking back, I would have done this differently, in that I would mention what I observed to one of the instructors, for them to coach if they considered it appropriate.  However, this is hindsight and I standby trying to help someone.

Another time, I was out for the day and noticed a pilot wearing a climbing helmet and not one designed for paragliding*.  Again, I asked them if they had a paragliding one and that it’s designed for a reason with the relevant EN certification.  Similarly my intent was to help not to chide but what would happen if they had a head injury which could have been avoided and I hadn’t said anything?

On a general basis, many people I’ve flown with, and been ground-handling with, have been very kind in giving quick bits of guidance and feedback to help me.  For the record, I’m open to any and all feedback from any source.  It’s in thinking about this that maybe given the legal liabilities and nature of personalities receiving feedback that one of key points in this article is that maybe each of us needs to be more explicit in letting others know we’re open to comments.  Furthermore, after being out flying, are safety or flying techniques and events discussed for lessons learnt?

For me, flying is fun but equally learning how to get better every time I get my wing out is also not just fun and rewarding, but also helps my safety.  Each pilot is responsible for their own safety.  This article's intent is to get the pilot to think about how approachable they are to others’ feedback and equally to consider if they either are, or want to be, a ‘sayer’. Being in mind that feedback and informal guidance is distinct from coaching with the best approach to how to do this taken into account.

Safe flying,

Clive.

Martin Baxter comments

I found Clive’s article very thought provoking.  One of the most challenging areas to address is close proximity flying and lookout.  I recall ridge soaring with a large group in Slovenia.  We were all below ridge height and scratching.  Discipline was good but afterwards a less experienced pilot approached me explaining that he had been scared that I would force him into the trees as we passed.  I’d never considered that what I considered a good safety margin wasn’t enough for others.  Admitting that something has scared you is often a good way to broach a subject.  

If somebody approaches you try not to be too defensive.  I was once approached by an instructor, in Annecy, who accused me of not maintaining a good lookout.  I apologised explaining that I had been distracted watching a pilot under a reserve land in trees.  ‘Correct answer’, he said, ‘my student has it on video’!

*Note: Wearing a helmet that hasn't been approved might invalidate your insurance.  That doesn't matter much if you only hurt yourself, but it could be critical if somebody else sues you, perhaps after a mid-air collision or causing a horse to throw its rider.
