Coaching

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

Ben Lewis’ almost disastrous 20 minute trip up into a Cu Nim in Bir last month is all over the news. The coaching takeaway is simple. These things start in a compelling way. We’d run away from 20m/s lift and turbulence in a flash but a wide area of 1 up is fine, what’s the problem? Then it becomes 3 up and then 6 up. I’d use the boiling frog analogy, except that frogs in hot water, apparently have the sense to jump out. If the flying is that good, keep questioning and always keep embedded Cu Nim as a possibility in the back of your mind. Like the frog, the quickest route out is the one you came in by - turn 180 degrees around or if terrain is in the way, 90 degrees to the nearest edge. Spirals have their place - which Clive talks about in this issue but they will keep you in the air that is giving you problems.

To give us an idea what goes on in one of these things I did a little analysis on Ben’s tracklog in the cloud…..

The incident begins at 14:17 IST with starting altitudes of 2281m pressure 2164m GNSS

1277 seconds into the incident peak altitude is 7004m pressure & 7374m GNSS.

For acceleration data there are some very large jumps - 50 and 99m in 1 second, not surrounded by similar figures. These could well be glitches in the data. I simply averaged both altitude deltas to get the broader trend.

Event at 1000s to 1040s with sudden reversal of climb and likely shock opening of wing below Ben in severe turbulence.

Similar events at 975, 1375.

The first shock opening resulted in a force of 4 or 5 G being experienced, both up an then down. That’s positive G while being accelerated downwards! This doesn't take into account any force Ben might be experiencing from a spiral\rotation he talks about in the Cloudbase Mayhem podcast.

At the top section from 1075s to peak altitude at 1277s Ben was climbing at an average 10m/s or 22mph but had been 23 m/s.

From 1300, there are about four descent events with some peak descent rates that exceed 30m/s or 67mph.

This would indicate more than just a locked in sprial dive. Possibly heavy sink / microburst which would tie in with hail and rain on landing.

The last and longest descent event is from 5000m and is consistently above 17m/s (38 mph) until Ben hits the tree with a fairly mild impact of ~1G.

Very likely any surface other than trees would have been fatal. Also, 7000+ m was probably not the full extent of the cloud. The photo someone took shows a developed anvil to Ben could have been in there to 10 km or more, worsening exposure and the chance of lightning.

Glad to hear Ben is recovering well and I hope that continues.

Hear Ben’s first-hand story on the latest Cloudbase Mayhem podcast.

Farmers’ Dinner 2024

Martin Baxter reports

We don’t pay site fees in the Dales.  Instead, our sites officers deliver to each farmer a bottle of their favourite tipple at Christmas, and we host them for a free 3-course dinner and drinks.  This year the dinner was on Saturday 12th October, returning to the Knights Table in Stainforth.  We laid on a minibus for those in and around Upper Wensleydale.

The committee had decided to separate the dinner from the Flyin, feeling that the dinner rather detracted from the atmosphere around the campfire at the Flyin.  We’ve also decided that it is more appropriate to present club awards at a club night (AGM) rather than in front of the farmers.  Fearing that this would reduce the number of members attending, the club paid for the meals of committee members acting as hosts.  In the end 7 committee members hosted 29 farmers with Alan Oliver being the only member who attended.

Everybody agreed that the dinner had been excellent, and the drinks flowed freely.  Even the club chairman played his part by keeping his speech (relatively) short.  David Airey responded on behalf of the farmers with an even shorter speech, commenting that he wished that other sporting clubs behaved as well as we do.

Thanks go to Stef for planning the event.  Unfortunately, he couldn’t make it on the night, but Mike picked up the baton ensuring that everything went smoothly on the night.  Dave co-ordinated the responses, Tom booked and co-ordinated the minibus, and the sites officers did a sterling job of hosting ‘their’ farmers.

The farmers’ dinner really is a ‘force multiplier’: a little good will goes a long way.  We send invitations to numerous farmers knowing that if they all accepted, we wouldn’t be able to find a venue big enough, and that the club would be bankrupt; but experience has shown that they don’t.

Site access and goodwill has been secured for another year.

Weather Station

Martin Baxter reports

Link to live weather data here.

There is quite a history to the club’s remote weather station.  It’s on the site of an old TV mast on Yorburgh, the hill above Burtersett in Wensleydale.  The first incarnation, constructed by club members, was called Hamish.  Then when Rod Buck started the network of stations called Wendy Windblows the club sold (!) Hamish to him.  In 2021 the club invested in an ‘off the shelf’ solution called Holfuy.  The project was masterminded by Simon Tomlinson and Trev Birkbeck, with financial assistance from Active Edge.

Photo: Martin, Trev, Simon, (Max) and Liam

Some of you will have noticed that a couple of the functions of the weather station had failed, most notably the predicted height of cloudbase and, after 3 years of enduring the elements, the station was overdue for a bit of maintenance.  I think that Simon left the proposed visit until the autumn just to make it a bit more challenging!

As so it came to pass that the day after the Farmers’ Dinner, with only slightly fuzzy heads; Simon, Trevor, Martin and Liam Toothill embarked on the task.  When they installed the station Trev burst a tyre driving up the track from Burtersett, so this time wisdom dictated that we drive along the Cam High Road past Wether Fell.  We passed a few hopeful parawaiters but it was to prove too light for soaring all day.  We we’re all dressed for the conditions: Trev in a flying suit; Liam in shorts!

Photo: Trev failing to get Liam to take matters seriously.

We parked the car as close as we could and then set off on the half hour walk to the weather station, partly on footpath, and partly cross country.  With remote weather stations there is always a risk of vandalism or theft.  All I can say is that any miscreant would have to be pretty committed!

Photo: Clearly Max was the brains of the operation.

Trev has cleverly designed the mast such that it hinges down, allowing easy maintenance of the electrics: no ladders required.  We released the hinge and one of the 4 wire guide lines at ground level.  To the end of the detached guide line we attached a rope and I was given the job of anchorman.  I had visions of being catapulted into Wensleydale but, with great teamwork and coordination, the mast was lowered safely to the ground.

Photo: Must be technical; Simon has got his glasses on.

Simon opened the box containing most of the electrics and we were all pleasantly surprised to find not a hint of dampness or rust.  Well done Holfuy!  Faulty parts were replaced, moving bits were lubricated, and copious quantities of self-amalgamating tape were applied.   We then conducted some rigorous testing to ensure that everything was working properly.  I must apologise to anyone looking at the App at the time: the wind varied between calm and strong, direction changed by 180 degrees several times, and 74mm of rain suddenly fell out of a cloudless sky.

All working, we hoisted and secured the mast back into position, and adjourned to the Board Inn for refreshment.  I’m sorry to have to report that Simon has turned to the dark side: he has been seduced into the sport of sand yachting and has decided to give up paragliding.  However, I’m delighted to report that Liam has generously agreed to take on the role of (Weather) Station Master.

The weather station may not be as useful as the forecast if you live some distance away, but once local I find it really useful for spotting the trends (i.e. the wind is picking up, or the wind is veering).  It was designed to report the actual conditions for Wether Fell (NW), but it isn’t as accurate in other directions.  The following day I flew at Semer Water (SE) when the station was reporting ENE.  As the wind picked up and backed slightly the station accurately reported the direction and strength as ESE.  The local topography obviously influences the reading in light winds from other directions.    

So, You Want To Fly 300km?

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

I’d say you’ve got two options.

1 – Fly thirty or so years, plan a lot, get out on most windy days that look flyable to remote hillsides a long way from home and just maybe you’ll become one of the very few to have flown 300km in the UK.

2- Go to Brazil in October and become a tiny spec riding on the trade winds that setup there at this time of year… oh and learn how to tow launch.

If you went for option two, here’s how it works…..

You’re up at 5:20am, likely earlier, since the bloody cockerel has been at it since 4am. Dress and drop your wing downstairs ready to go. Breakfast and get some water for the day. October near the equator is hot and if you find yourself walking you will get through a single water bladder easily. Take two. Load into the pickup where your driver will take you to the airstrip.

At the airstrip, bag your spot among the twenty other pilots on the tarpaulin and under a tent for a bit of shade. Prep for the day’s flying as you normally would and then discuss a goal with your fellow pilots…. 300 or more km to the west. Far enough away that the sun sets noticeably later. It’s 7am now and you have a gamble to make. Are you good enough at staying up that you can launch amongst the early folks for a longer day flying but risking a sink out or do you wait in the queue to launch at 7:30 to 8:30?

That queue is where you see everyone’s technique in high wind put to the test. You know you have the same thing coming yourself. You launch from a tarp on the airstrip threshold. It’s not big so you don’t have too much luxury in messing up and relaunching. There’s unforgiving gravel and thorny bushes to the sides. There are plenty of helpers keeping your wing down during the stronger gusts as they come through. It’s nothing to fear most of the time though. You can choose the moment and launch in something that would be familiar to anyone who’s been up Whernside and taken off in the compression there.

Wing successfully above, the reassuring launch marshal (Roland) is shouting “away!” to the driver in Portuguese and if you aren’t quite awake now, you soon will be! The driver speeds off down the runway but you just go forward and up at a light jog. He’s paying out line with a low tension pull to reduce the danger of lock out. Once enough is out, it gets serious and you might see five up on the vario towing. You look down as the car shrinks below and starts to crawl along the second half of the runway. If it’s windy up high it’s kiting you up and can only edge forward without putting too much tension on the line. Eventually the truck U-turns 800m below you and the beeping eases slightly. Release the cable from your bridle and now you’re on your own.

This is a wonderful contrast. You’ve gone from three minutes of terror with the wing straining against every bit of lift on the way up to calming down so you can be sensitive to every scrap of lift. Your best strategy is to be very Zen so that you settle into survival mode. It’s five hours before midday and even on the equator, thermals are still fickle and bubble like this early. Base is about 1000m and you hang onto it like glue as you drift back over the power station. Yes, the power station with multiple high tension lines of pylons, thousands of square metres of solar panels, substation etc. 5km over the back. Mince over that like there’s no tomorrow, it focuses the mind. A few more kilometres after that and there’s respite – the rise up to the Sertão plateaux is only 50m or so but it’s enough for the air to reliably kick off and you’ll be back to base every time.

Now you’re 20 km in and committed. The Mossoro road disappears off to the north and a dry, brushy terrain stretches for dozens of kilometres ahead. It consists of ten foot high brush punctuated by the odd orange dirt track, small oilfield or tiny settlement with a few surrounding dusty fields. This is going to be your first 100km and survival is still foremost in your mind. It’s too far away to get a retrieve and tow again but too early in the day to be sinking out, kicking yourself that you’ve not made the best of it. And there’s the anxiety – you’ve come to the other side of the planet for a few chances to fly a PB. At any moment sink could combine to put you down. You might not find the next thermal. Will the wind mean that you’re going backwards into thorns and trees which will shred your wing? If you do fly into the afternoon, what about dealing with the stress of a 6m/s climb from some desperate low save because that’s the only way you’re going to join back up with your gaggle? Can you even last the ten hours in the air?

I’m over egging it. If you do sink out it’s mostly not too bad. There might be a friendly farmer to give you a ride to the nearest settlement on a moped or donkey cart. Once there the locals will likely be offering you sweet coffee and the chance to tickle the tummy of an armadillo. If you’re lucky you might even disrupt the morning’s school for an entire village😊.

That wind will push you along most days, and you should see 100km in the first three hours, but that doesn’t mean you can hang about. To get 300 km or more, that’s 30 to 40 km average speed until the sun is starting to set. It does help when you see 75 km/h on glide with a bit of bar.

Late morning should be your first big change in landscape as the plateaux suddenly drops and the do-Norte valley comes into view. It’s 20 km wide and full of towns, plantations and lakes. Very much civilisation again. The sun is high and you can dealing with some rough air around here. It can be a contrast to fly as well. The drop in terrain can mean scrabbling around looking for a low save or you might connect with a booming set of thermals off the last of the plateaux as the hot air is forced to rise and find yourself in a kilometre wide 4up beastie that threatens to put you in the white room.

It's the Land ‘O Lakes for the next 50km. Mixed rolling hills and damned lakes of all sizes. You might get low here but shouldn’t go down. There’s always some friendly Urubu (vultures) to find the cores. I wouldn’t trust them on a glide though. Below 1000m they should be around somewhere, so keep your eyes peeled. You might be lucky enough for one of them to join you for a while if you’re going their way. They’ll ride in front of your pod and surf the leading edge. Occasionally they’ll join you in a climb – that’s when you know you’re getting good, when you’ve stolen a march on the vultures.

The mixed terrain is coming now that we’re past lunchtime. First it’s the long line of low hills called the Serrote Pedra Branca. They’re just a few hundred metres high but are enough to provide ample trigger points. By now base has risen to 2500m and you’ll be cold up there if you haven’t worn a couple of thin sleeved tops. Winter running gloves will be fine for your hands. It’s time to be powering ahead with long glides, only stopping for climbs that are 2up or more. You’ll definitely see the larger peaks of the Serrote Branco Park next at 900m height. You’ll be at 200+ km by this point and the big prize of any flight out there is in view. Quixada and the surrounding Inselbergs. Isolated peaks begin to dot the landscape, like some kind of approach to Mordor in the Lords of the Rings. They’re the eroding remnants of a Pre-Cambrian upland that has supported this region of South America since before there was anything more than single celled creatures clogging up the place.

You need to tackle uplands now and this could be a problem. If it’s late afternoon you could be loosing lift and not able to commit to going low over peaks in high wind with few tracks. Ideally you have the opposite problem-  that you have big, gentle afternoon thermals which lift you up to sucky clouds where you’ll need to skirt around them. Whichever way it works out, you’re knocking on the door of 300 km at this point. Eke your flight out as much as possible in the late afternoon. One random thing to be careful of is that you’re gliding into the setting sun in fairly buoyant air and it’s going to be on your exposed forehead for a while. Keep it protected or you’ll be living with a horizontal tan line across your forehead for days.

In the plains after the highlands you’ve bagged your goal. Well done! It might even be difficult to get down in places since there are large areas of restitution like lift in the afternoon. You will need to get down though. Sunset is a bit after 5pm and it starts to get dark very quickly in civil twilight. Come down near a village or road and there are always power lines that need spotting and rechecking. Don’t discount that lines can be strung across the centre line of a football field, not just the sides. Pack up quickly to avoid the dark or bring a head torch.

Send a pin and tell the chase drivers that you’ve landed via InReach. They’ll have been tracking you all the way so it shouldn't be too long getting to you. If all works out well, you’ll be picked up with some other stray fliers within an hour or two and then it’s a seven or so hour drive back. Be sure to buy your driver some food at a churrascaria (meat on a stick self service restaurant) on the way back. Share the driving, have a podcast/album or three ready to go. At times you’ll need the Zen patience you showed early in the flight in navigating almost invisible speed bumps and epic numbers of potholes on the way back.

Repeat the next day if you’re up to that kind of punishment.

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Learnings from a trip to Bassano, Italy

Earlier this month, with a few others in the Club, I went on a trip to Bassano del Grappa.  We organised the trip ourselves and were very fortunate to have Pete Morris as a lead instructor with Andy Reid in support.  The above photo is of the town which lies at the foothills of the Alps from which the flatlands then extend all the way to the Venetian Coast.  It’s a very popular flying site being within easy reach of the German border and only an hour’s drive (or train) from Venice Airport.

We stayed at the renowned paragliding hostel that is Tillys with two of us staying the following week at a B&B venue known as Kiki.  Both highly recommended with Kiki having its own landing field adjacent to the property.

Below are a few of the key learning points from the trip:

  • In rain don’t apply Big Ears to get down more quickly.  This poses a risk of the folded wing parts sticking and not being able to come out for landing. Big ears also increased your angle of attack, making a stall more likely, especially with a trailing edge full of water.
  • It is more advisable to apply approximately 15% of speed bar or, even better, to have read the weather conditions to not be in the air.
  • To get down quickly in normal conditions, one big ear spiral dive can be applied.  This generates less G force than a full spiral dive.
  • If accidentally flying in clouds, keep to a course heading, trusting the direction of the instrument.  Knowing one’s heading at all times is essential to be able to note before becoming engulfed by clouds. NOTE:  article below on the danger of flying in clouds
  • Glide ratio - know the min sink for one’s glider to extend glide ratio if required to go further to reach a chosen destination.
  • Vario setup.  I obtained an Air3 earlier this year and it was extremely useful to learn first hand how to adjust the screen for individual setup.
  • Foehn wind.  In the Alps attention needs to be paid to the level and direction of the Foehn wind.  This can dramatically affect the level of turbulence from either a northerly or southerly direction and if severe enough, it may be advisable not to fly.
  • Use of Centre ‘As’ only for forward launching.  I used to use both A risers for forward launching.  After guidance I moved to using just the centre ‘A’ riser which I found to lead the centre part of the glider more smoothly with the glider less likely to be unbalanced on one side during the initial part of the launch.
  • Forward launch techniques:  thoroughly check lines clear before launching; take up initial slack in A riser; keep pressure on chest with positive forward motion; have arms remain in a horizontal ‘W’ position before release and quick brake, then direct assertive forward launch motion itself
  • Landing - reminder to keep to a baseline and evaluation of height before final approach from strength of the wind at the landing site
  • Thermal tip - set vario to 0 ms climb rate sensitivity.  This means that the Vario can give better forward notice for weaker thermals that may only be 0.5 ms once in them, which may build in strength or at worst allow an incremental height gain.
  • Safety - don’t wear a peaked cap under a helmet.  For the record I’ve never done this but this was seen on a few flyers.  Wearing a peaked cap will severely restrict the flyers visibility.
  • Have a flight plan.  May sound obvious but I find this a useful reminder in regard to where to most likely get lift post launch, or avoid other flyers at a busy site.
  • Busy skies - At times there were a large number of flyers in the air.  This required active lookout and even a shout out to alert others.  Ultimately I was reminded of the phrase that as ‘a flyer I’m part of the problem’ and to change this, if it’s not possible to fly away from others, the choice is to land.  On one occasion, we chose to postpone flying when there appeared to us that there were too many flyers within a narrow range.

If you’d like to know more about Bassano for flying then please feel welcome to contact me.  Or my fellow companions: Chris Baird, Andrew Nightingale and Andrew Fenton - thanks to them for their excellent company for a great trip.

Crash, Crash. ….   I’m ok  ….  Not

Rob Urselmann reports

This story is mostly an update on my flight log written the day after on the club forum, but followed by fresh analysis and hindsight observations.

Part I: Magic

Thursday October 10th promised just enough to go try a local flight.

About six of us launched at Park Fell low down just above the first wall, conditions a bit strong and some turbulent on TO to turn to smooth wavey conditions. Before long pilots went high all over, some High and ChrisK HighHigh (5000+ft in the blue along the clouds). Having crossed to IB following Ed and Chris earlier, I caught a cloud and rode it climbing to 1200m/4000ft on the edge of it as it spread out from IB to Whernside south end. There I lost the lifty line. On the way back, over the valley road and still touching base near 1200m, at 12:57 the Hawk pilot who had rung me earlier following up on my 07:30 submitted NOTAM for Semer flew by way low under me and send his greetings by wiggling his wings. Awesome!

As lift was turning to sink locally under my cloud, I got back to IB and, knowing from experience I wouldn't make it back to Park in this kind of northerly, I ended up soaring and scratching IB’s north face for quite a while, patiently waiting for the next cloud to go over and things kick off again. Whilst there were a good half dozen or so pilots around, the conditions were such that we were widely spread out all over the Ingleborough-Whernside valley, from on the ground to 5000ft, and I was on IB on my own. The air was silky smooth but seemed to get lighter and I was just thinking of toplanding on IB summit to wait it out.

Then, the wind dropped even more and I lost height to below the summit, leaving me scratching just in front of the rocky boulder area on E of the N face. Then, just as I was in front of the big outcrop, a gust hit me, slammed me sideways and as I looked up my wing had collapsed all way to just the very left tip.

Crash location from my track log plotted on Google map

Part II: A Bloody Idiot.

I was only ~5m up but hit the rocks hard somewhere on my right side and screamed out in pain, and found myself backward draped over a rock under my back, wrapped in my harness and lines. I don’t actually remember the exact impact. To my surprise, with the summit only 10m above me, no walkers responded and I suddenly felt very much on my own. Time of impact 13:23:00 exact.

Whilst in immediate severe pain on my right side and rump, I was also extremely uncomfortable laying bend backward wrapped in my harness wrapped in lines, and I had to move to get to my gear. For some reason I was thinking I impacted on my radio and was "merely" badly bruised. I awkwardly unwrapped myself to get the radio from under me so it could have line of sight, and being badly winded just managed to radio a "crash crash" so hopefully somebody would at least be aware something had happened. Having wriggled out of my harness, I guess I figured I was ‘ok’, despite not being able to weight bear on my right leg and having lava hot shoots of pain every time I put any weight on it. Really…     So when Joe replied and asked for more details, probably still trying to gather my senses I said I was ‘probably ok’ instead of ‘had a bad crash onto rocks. Needs help’. (Adrenaline Idiot decision #1).  It would turn out I had fractured my R hip (undisplaced acetabulum wall fracture), ripped off 4 of my R lumbar vertebrae transverse processes (the little bones on the sides) and had internal bleeding. Oh, and fractured the tip of my R elbow and off course massive bruising on my R rump and thigh.  Looking back later on my vario logs, my impact velocity was only 2~3 m/s vertical but horizontal still 21 km/h (5.8m/s) for a total impact velocity of 6-6.5 m/s!

So, now thinking I was on my own and had to get out, I slowly and painfully packed up my gear thinking my "bruises" would improve with movement (Adrenaline decision #2). This ended up taking the better part of an hour. During this time, Chris Kay, back from his trip to orbit and still flying, was in touch on radio. He heard me say “I’m ok” but didn't like the tone of my voice and decided to topland to come check me out anyhow (Hero decision)!  

I had finally packed up and was going to try to scramble the 10m up to the summit and the flat track. Then a wing flew smack over me at just above ridge height. I shouted out but wasn’t heard or (more likely) not located as was directly underneath.  I slung my pack on and from the instant shoot of pain it was clear that wasn't going to happen with the pack on. Exactly then was when Chris arrived. So what Chris saw was a person up and about (first response DR ABC tick) who’d already neatly packed up his gear. In his shoes I’d most likely also not be particularly concerned at that point. He carried my pack as I slowly, awkwardly and painfully clambered up on hands and feet.  However, at the top I realised I really couldn't walk proper even on the flat and ( Idiot decision #3) accosted two random passing walkers, not exactly young and fit themselves, and pretty much confiscated their walking poles!!!  They suggested I call for mountain rescue instead (good idea!) but I just took their details to get the poles back to them and then totally ignored them.

Chris, now carrying two packs!, scooted off ahead as I slowly shuffled my way down.  Having arrived at the bowl, although no idea of real time, I realised I would be home later than expected so figured I’d better let my wife know. I send her a message “Had a hard landing on Ingleborough.  Slowly walking down, with assistance for pack. Will be a slow trip. Just bruised”.  I wasn’t paying attention to time and it sure didn’t feel so long, but looking back on the time stamps it was already 15:14 (two hours after impact) and I’d moved 500m….

By this time Tim O, who’d landed by the time I crashed, had made his way to the Hil Inn Road end, dropped his gear and come up again to see if he could help (Hero decision #2). He met us as I was halfway down the waterfall track. Tim tried to check me over proper but as I was still moving and rather cold (and on a steep track), I refused. He then took over my pack from Chris.

I made it another bit down the track until I started feeling dizzy and nauseous and, now 3.5 hours after impact, seized up and just couldn't proceed anymore. I asked Tim to call rescue (finally!, a decent decision). Tim did and then found a more sheltered spot and, now badly shivering from hypothermia and delayed shock despite in still all my winter flying gear, packed me onto my opened-out glider bag and under my wing whilst we waited for the CRO (cave - & mountain - rescue). By now I was just hanging in, but at least I gradually warmed up and stopped shivering. Later we learned that hypothermia not only makes you cold, it also stops your blood from clotting so could have aggravated my internal bleeding. Top marks to Tim!!!

It was 5(?)pm by the time the CRO team arrived, and they were amazing. Their secondary survey immediately picked up on my sharp inhales when palpating my hip and lumbar spine, and I was duly packed up and immobilised in their vacuum bean bag. The immediate reduction in pain level was much appreciated, and the subsequent Entonox kept it at bay whilst I was being lifted onto and then stretchered off the hill, into a waiting ambulance, bean bag and all.

Arriving at ED at 9pm things were a bit odd. Despite being in an ambo and what must have been handed over by CRO as a potential spinal injury, the expected wait time was 7+ hours and would be in the ambo. However, the ambos sort of strongly suggested wait might be less if I could possibly sit up and go into the waiting room (maybe they were at the end of their shift??). Being the kind idiot I am, I agreed. The moment they opened the bean bag the pain returned, and the moment I had moved from stretcher to wheelchair I threw up. Still I was wheeled into the ED waiting room and left to fight for myself. I never saw any ED staff or triage.

Around 11 my wife, having had the car returned by my mates and now seriously worried about me, showed up in ED. Around 2am, still in pain hanging in a chair and not haven eaten since breakfast, I had a bar and promptly threw it up.  That was when my wife started hunting down ED staff. Finally realising I was in shock and getting worse, at 3 am I was finally brought through and onto a stretcher again. Now suddenly also suspecting possible head injury, the ED doctor ordered a full body CT scan and, rather ironically after 6 hours on a chair, now I was suddenly supposed to stay flat on my back and had two pathetically ineffective Styrofoam “blocks” taped to the side of my head supposed to keep me immobile. If it wasn’t real it would be funny. The CT scan revealed the extend of my injuries (nothing critical!) and at 7am I was rolled up to the Othopedic ward, 17 hours after impact.

In the end I was very lucky to have ‘got away’ with undisplaced fractures and no spinal or other nerve damage, and no surgery required. As I write/update this article, three weeks after the crash, I’m halfway through six weeks of minimal-mobility and crutches to let the bones and soft tissues heal. Complications excluded, I should make a full recovery and able to return to the skies in spring.

A MASSIVE THANK YOU to Chris, Tim and the CRO team, and everybody else around who assisted unaware to me or just was ready to assist if needed.

Causes:

Having had time to reflect and discuss, below is my (current) thinking of what happened and why.

-          That part of the Ingleborough north face is known to be potentially turbulent, and I normally give it a good berth either in front or above. However, although it was fair turbulent on takeoff, the air turned very smooth and was silky smooth all the time I was soaring Ingleborough. I got lulled into complacency.

-          Although I had some weight shift on the valley side as per Jockey, I wasn’t flying active enough with only a little pressure on the brakes despite scratching a known potentially turbulent area.

-          Having had pretty benign flying not requiring a lot of action, despite being in all my winter gear I was probably a bit cold after an hour in the air and might have been just that bit slower.

-          On my last turn before the crash, at the bowl end, the wind dropped and I ended up lower than I had before which put me rather close to the rocks on my left. However, instead of decisively going further out as I’d done before I stayed close in (on the zoomed track you can see I’m either in but high -blue- or low but further out -red- on all other passes).

-          The strong gust came through without warning and slammed my wing sideway over/alongside the outcrop. It might have rotored already from the steps lower down, or rotored over the boulders as I went sideways.  Either way, even if I had been flying fully active and caught it, I’m not sure the result would’ve been much different as the energy went toward the hill and over the outcrop and I was on the deck in under 3 seconds.  The holes in the cheese had all already lined up at that point

-          The wing ended up perfectly and loosely laid out in direction of original flight on the rocks just in front of me, so I suspect I hit the deck before the wing had time to fold or hook onto something. There had been no turn.

 

Reflections:

- Even with my first responder background and being in a lot of pain I fell prey to the adrenaline fuelled "I'll be alrigh" error.  The method-of-injury (fall from height, backwards onto boulders) is a classic first-responder scenario to immediately suspect potential spine and/or hip damage. Whilst ‘ideally’ I should not have moved and used my radio, phone or even my satellite messenger which sat right there in my flight deck pocket to call for Rescue. Had I been there as an immediate first responder instead of the lone casualty, I would almost certainly have secured the person, kept them warm, told them to not move and called for a (chopper) rescue. However, adrenaline’s “fight or flight” does weird things to a person and I went into primal survival tunnel vision. Maybe if I hadn’t been able to walk/shuffle at all, I would’ve been forced to concede and would’ve ended up making the rescue call right there.

- My impact velocity was only 2-3 m/s. Inspecting my harness, I landed either just to the side of the bum protector or also partially on it. As the wind collapsed from the right and I was slightly weight shifted to the right (to counter left , and certainly not fast enough to weightshift to the left, I suspect I ‘rolled’ to the right as that side of the risers dropped. Or I may have just hit a rock the wrong way… Either way, I like to think that even if the impact protection absorbed just a fraction of the energy, it likely saved me from a lot more grief. The Delight 3, whilst good impact foam below, has no impact protection on the side. Few harnesses do, and those that do get really heavy. It’s a balance we all need to strike. The article “The Future of Paragliding Harness Back Protectors” linked below made for interesting reading.

- Questions I still have:  Would I have been better off if I’d flown a lower grade wing, or a wing with internal stability (RAST on Swing wings).

Suggestions:

- Always carry a radio, make sure it’s charged and working even if just going for a local soar.  

- If you're in the vicinity of a ‘bad landing’, DO go check out the person and assist on scene. In the worst case you lose some flying time, in the best case you may save a life.

- Do a First Aid course.   Ironically, I missed my own DHPC course this weekend.

- I’m not the only casualty to feel ‘ok’ and try walk down a hill this year, even just here in the Dales, and I’ve heard several other stories in the past three weeks. Don't accept the casualty's "I'm ok" but insist on a proper secondary survey if any signs of injury. If need be, be a hardnose: they’ll be grateful later I promise!

- Seeing the message, my wife’s instinct was to call me and check, but she decided to be ‘cool and calm’ and didn’t.  Had she called me, she would’ve likely recognised something was seriously off with me and quite possibly would’ve been able to pull me out of my tunnel vision and accept rescue.

- After my message, my wife didn’t hear anything until Tim rang her once we stopped two hours later. She said these were some of the longest two hours. Keep loved ones informed and able to track you. (I had switched off my LT24 tracker during packing up).

- If somebody needs to go to ED, make sure there’s going to be somebody with them to support them and, if required, advocate for them. Without my wife there, I’m pretty certain I would’ve been in much worse shape (like unconscious on the floor worse!) before being looked at.

- Use a harness with good back (and side) protection.

- Given the numerous not-spots even in the local area, consider a satellite emergency beacon/communicator.

- Put the What-3-Words app [https://what3words.com/products/what3words-app] on your phone, it’ll enable you to give a highly accurate location to the emergency services. [W3W QR code]

Note 1: the Delight 3 harness has a dedicated radio pocked on the R side, but checking it afterward- in flying mode it actually sits in the ‘open’ triangle between hip, body and carabiners. Instead of landing on the radio, I more likely just hit one or more of the boulders I crashed into.

PS: Being a geek with now several weeks time on my hands, I had a close look at my track records (XCtrack on phone and XCTracer MaxxII, only slight differences) and pulled in the Ordnance Survey 1 meter spread, 15cm height-resolution resolution laser for a detailed plot against my track.

PG Tips 🫖 (Paragliding tips)

Martin Baxter reports.

I came to the launch technique of using A&Cs rather late in my career (I previously used the 3-axis technique), but it’s now my preferred choice in a decent breeze.  However, I was puzzled when the wing occasionally veered off to one side and I had to abort the launch.  Initially I assumed a knotted line which had then released itself when the tension was removed.  The clue came when I realised that it always happened on the same side.

My rear risers are fitted with toggles for steering/active flying whilst on bar.  On my last flight of the season I realised that one of my brake lines was looping around one of the toggles, effectively shortening that brake line dramatically.  Because I always turn the same way, the wing always veered the same way.  Problem solved: I just need to remember to check that it isn’t looped as part pre take off check.  Geordie would point out that his Cat always checks that his Control lines are free-running.    

First Aid course (2nd Nov 2024) provided by Allan Shaw

Marian Manson reports

First on scene: what would you do?

A machete attack, an unconscious rough sleeper and friends, a car crash with 2 people with life threatening injuries and a crashed paraglider pilot... Not what you expect to deal with on a Saturday afternoon in November.

Thankfully these were not real incidents but simulated scenarios with either great acting from pretend casualties (who knew paraglider pilots could act) or using dummies and fake blood - wow.

The course was a jam-packed session from 0900-1600 and we covered all the fundamentals of first aid and discussed lots of paraglider specific injury situations.

My big take aways from the course from a paraglider incident perspective (it was only yesterday and my old brain doesn’t remember everything) :-

1.    If there are lots of people around, don’t all gather around the casualty when an incident happens. Let someone take the lead and stay quiet whilst the lead person listens carefully to the casualty. They may go unconscious at any point so “knowing where it hurts” is key – it’s harder to do this when they are unconscious.

2.    Not breathing or bleeding out (internal or external) – these are probably the two most important things to deal with and ideally if there are more than 2 people, then one person should concentrate on getting them breathing and the other concentrates on stopping the bleed out. If you are on your own then obviously breathing is the priority.

3.    Internal bleeding signs – apart from pain, sore abdomen, nausea/vomiting, clammy skin, thirst and being unconscious, there were some great specifics relating to lower limbs which could be life threatening

a.    hip / pelvis – if the casualty’s feet are completely splayed out and the sides of their feet are flat on the ground, this could be a broken hip/ pelvis. Tie their shoe laces together which will bring their hip into more alignment and help with blood loss. We learned how to apply a hip tourniquet using a space blanket.

b.    limbs – if one leg is longer than the other then the bone is likely broken and could be internal bleeding. We used a couple of tourniquets to help with this as you may not know where the brake is.

4.    Keeping the casualty warm as quickly as possible – this was a VERY important theme including having some jam sachets or equivalent to rub into their cheek. Keeping warm, reduces blood loss and helps with so many issues.

Andrew Braithwaite modelling the latest fashion in outdoor survival gear.

5.    Using the paraglider harness to help with the recovery position is ideal but you may have to undo leg straps to deal with leg or hip injuries

I was surprised that keeping the helmet on was now considered old school and as Allan explained you cannot deal with a breathing situation if the helmet is on and also inspecting the head. But I think carefully removing it is still key.

Debriefing the scenario.

We did all the key CPR stuff which was excellent and also used different automatic external defibrillation machines – some of them give the count for compressions, so no need to sing “stayin alive”!  Also the importance of having aspirin and epi-pen to help with heart attack casualties.

I also loved Alan’s emergency basic kit (when out on the hill) with the gaffa tape wrapped around the plastic container to be used for securing dressings and the concept of NOT taking loads of dressings with you but using a T-shirt or any other clothing.

It was a really good course but I am hopeful that I don’t ever have to use any of it for real (yes I am a wimp) but it’s better to do something rather than nothing. Definitely get yourself booked on the next course.
