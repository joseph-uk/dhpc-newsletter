Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

Whilst you were watching the King’s coronation, Clive Sury and I were mixing with the great unwashed from the North of England at Leeds Bradford Airport, enroute to a week of paragliding at the Hotel California in Spain.

 

Having sailed through a very relaxed Leeds Bradford Airport in March last year, I again opted for an afternoon flight, hoping to avoid the early morning melee.  Fail: the airport was rammed with long queues and nowhere to sit, probably because it was later in the season.  To add insult to injury we sat on the aeroplane for an hour waiting for an ‘ambulance’ to drop off 2 disabled passengers.  The aircraft on either side of us were similarly affected.  From an airspace perspective, it’s further evidence that Leeds Bradford Airport is operating beyond capacity.

 

I found evidence in the visitors’ book of a previous DHPC visit in 2004.  Older members may recognise some of the names.

This was my 21st visit to Hotel California and I’ve written about it many times before: first in 2007 and again last year (when Liam had hair!).  I’ll try not to bore you by repeating myself: just a few highlights this time.  I advertised the trip on the forum here but Clive was the only club member brave enough to join me this time.  With the Safety Officer in attendance at least it guaranteed an incident free trip!

Not one of our group, but we did offer moral support from our vantage point at the bar.

Most of my previous trips have been in March (when the weather is crap in the UK) but, since this was also a bit of a reunion with some of my old Army mates, availability dictated that we went in May this year.  Unsurprisingly it was much warmer than on previous trips.  Spain is already in the grip of a drought.  We had wall to wall sunshine and flew every day, though it was quite feisty at times.  Getting down safely could be challenging as thermals disrupted that perfect approach pattern.  We experienced quite a few dust devils. Two of my ex-Army mates are paragliding instructors and I was quite pleased that their tolerance of rough air was less than mine; or perhaps I was just more desperate for airtime.

 

Yours truly in the dusty landing field at Cenes

On the first day we flew a site further along the ridge from a round of the PWC.  I kept well clear but flew over their take-off soon after they had departed and saw a couple submarines at close quarters.  Watching the gaggle from the air was fascinating – I just had to hope that I hadn’t strayed into their next turn point.

Three of our flying days consisted of a lumpy flight in the mountains, followed by a smooth flight near the coast.  I can confirm that beer tastes much better when you have just landed next to the beach bar.

 One of the few occasions when the air was smooth enough for me to get my camera out.

We only did one ridge soaring flight.  Unlike Dales ridges, this one consisted of sea cliffs, luxury villas and swimming pools.  Conditions were quite light and slightly off the hill.  The bowl, just to the left of take-off, was allowing pilots to stay up but, as more pilots took off, the pressure was on to either push further along the ridge, or land on the beach.  The trouble with flying down the ridge was that it took you further away from the beach, which was the only safe landing option.  Towards the far end of the ridge was a large tower crane.  The advice over the radio from Dirk, our guide, was that it was OK to soar upwind of the crane at the same height and, if you could get a bit higher than it, move downwind of it where the ridge was a bit steeper.  I managed to do just that, leaving enough space for my friend to start soaring upwind of the crane.  What could possibly go wrong?  Yes, you’ve guessed: the crane started moving!  First it rotated away from the hill forcing my friend out of the lift and over the sea.  He promptly headed back to the bowl and the umbilical cord to the beach.  I continued soaring downwind of the crane and slightly above it for a while.  Just as I decided that discretion was the better part of valour, the crane swung inland effectively sweeping me off that part of the hill, and back into the melee of the bowl.  Lesson of the day - beware of moving obstacles!

 

Final beer on the beach.  Note the offending crane over my left shoulder.

Spain made it easier for us to say farewell for another year with some much-needed rain on the final morning.  Clive and I had booked an evening flight home which allowed for a relaxed final day: a scenic walk and a final drink on the beach once the rain had cleared and the sun came out.  The final bonus of a late flight home was that we completely missed the Eurovision Song Contest.

It’s not all about the numbers

Pete D reports

Stop press: the North, and the Dales in particular, kicks arse!

We often get a good spell of flying weather in the northern part of the country in the spring, before the stability of the summer sets in and all the big flights move south.  However, this year we are approaching the end of June, and most of the big flights are still occurring north of the Watford gap, or the Greggs-Pret A Manger boundary if you prefer.  Furthermore, the wind directions have been atypical, if there is such a thing these days, with the season starting off with northerlies and then a long spell of easterlies, with typical super high cloud bases.  Not a big Dodd/Wether fell day in sight!

The result of all this is that we have an incredible four pilots in the top ten of the national XC league, with Richard Meek sitting at the top of the pile with an amazing 1438 points.  Furthermore, in the club league the Dales has nearly 1800 points more than our nearest competitor.

The Dales is also topping the very popular UK XContest, with six pilots in the top fifteen of the open ranking and Jake Herbert topping the sport ranking (max EN-C).  

It is not all about the top guns either, with three Dales pilots getting their first 100km open distance flights so far this season, all of which are eligible for a Cross Country XContest 100 tee-shirt (see https://www.xcontest.org/united-kingdom/ for details).  Well done Gary Senior (100km from Model on 27th March), Liam Toothill (103km from Pen-y-ghent on 16th June), and Rahul Basu (103km from Cautley on 16th June).

And finally, the depth of the Dales club continues to impress with 39 pilots registering flights in the Dales XC league, with more than half identifying as Dales pilots, and 20 Dales pilots registered on the UK XContest.  Let’s hope we can continue these successes throughout the season, with many pilots doing the Dales club proud!

Hints & Tips

Pete Logan reports

I’m quite keen to minimise faff on launch and one of the things I’ve managed to sort out well has been my radio, at least until last Friday when no one could tell what I was saying. It turned out I’d broken my antenna which was only occasionally transmitting. In Blue Peter style I made a new one so I thought I’d share it with you…

It’s called a ¼ wave whip antenna and should perform better than most of the default rubber duck antennas that come with radios. I.e it will transmit further and receive more clearly.

If you don’t know the materials to use already, just contact me (coaching@dhpc.org.uk) and I’ll talk you through it.

I put an eye hole in my flying coat (equipment from your local haberdasher 🙂) so that I can stow the antenna up my sleeve. The placing allows me to cup my hand over the radio body so there’s no wind noise when I transmit. The benefits are that there’s no awkward antenna catching lines or sticking in your shoulder. Also, no headset, which I do not find a convenience at all. They have wires which you have to route on launch, plugs that don’t take bending forces very well and a nightmare when someone goes open channel for an hour or two. They’re also a reason that radios go onto constant transmit / open channel which is very annoying for anyone else.

Here’s the finished item….

One other thing I’d like to drop in is https://www.theflightvario.com/ The Flight Vario app which I’ve only just started taking a look at. It doesn’t have the full functionality of XCTrack’s app but it does have a really good vario for a phone. It’s still being built too, so more functionality will be added.

Some notable features are the very reactive vario, better than XCTracks and well worth having just as a backup if your main vario fails in flight. It will include several sources of thermals from past flights in the area you’re in. Very useful in the Alps and probably somewhat useful in the UK. Airspace on screen but I’m not sure it's loading NOTAMs yet. Flight tracking and an upload to a flight logbook.

Definitely worth having a look at.

Repack Reserve Repeat

Stef Sykes reports

Simon was already at the take off on windbank when we met in early April this year. I'd been at work and knocked off a bit early to catch the flyable day. We agreed that it was a bit top end, but decided to give it a go. I hadn't flown for a month or so, so I gave out an extended check of lines, straps and maillons. However, it had been a bit of a busy period so I'd missed the repack this year, although my reserve handle was all in place.

Finally getting the wing in the air in a good patch of air, I double-checked lines whilst walking around a bit to get the feel of the wing and the air around me then stepped forward and flew straight followed by a turn into the slightly southerly skewed wind. The wind strengthened slightly as I lifted. I hung around a little while wondering what Simon was doing still on the ground. A short radio check informed me his lines had twisted, so I headed off toward Hawkswick and Arncliffe. With plenty of lift about I was easily at 2000ft and on the way to Arncliffe when I decided to head back to check on Simon. Then I needed to pee, so decided to top land.

It was when I brought a turn into a spiral to reduce height in order to land that I now realise I must have also inadvertently pulled on the outside brake a little too much. The wing collapsed and I began autorating. Never having had this happen before, I panicked, realised I was only about 400ft from the slope and after looking up to see a shred of a wing I reached for the reserve handle and yanked the bugger.

It stayed as a lump on the handle. I shook it roughly then chucked the whole thing backwards, bizarrely, my panic turning to perplexity. It still didn't open.

By now, in line with all key technical documentation detailing use of brakes and placement of hands in most emergencies, I had let go of the brakes and the wing had righted itself to provide a smooth sail along the hill at slightly above take off.

I felt rather sick with relief, but also fear for what may have been the end.

Simon came on the radio “You've got something hanging from your harness – is that your reserve?”

I told him I'd had to deploy it, that it hadn't opened and that I would pull it up and gently go to land by the cars.

I sent the reserve off to Ginger Nomad to sort it out and made a diary entry on the 1st of February 2024 to remind me to book onto the next Repack - same for 2025 and 26.

Clive Sury reports

ADVANCE NOTICE: The Dales Club Reserve Repack Day for 2024 is scheduled for Sunday 14th January.

More details to follow.  Providing this information now to allow people as much notice as possible to plan their diaries.

Paraglider wing fully inflated before launching

Before launching, the paraglider wing should be fully inflated to enable maximum lift potential and minimise risk of collapse.  To ensure this, it is recommended that before launching the pilot checks their wing.  However, how many of us have seen only partially inflated wings being launched by pilots (or been guilty of this ourselves)?  The consequences of taking off with a partially inflated wing can be severe to catastrophic and therefore, the importance of this cannot be overstated.

Hence why this year I’m focussing on this area as much as possible and learning how to reverse launch with both A’s and C’s.  So far I’ve found this has enabled me to lift up the wing in a much more controlled manner, with more practice still required on my part to be fully competent.  Thanks to Stephen Craven and Pete Logan for their coaching to date here.  

I mention this aspect not to talk about myself but because on a recent holiday one of the party took off with a wing that was only 20% inflated and this was a key learning point.  Plus, how many of us have seen someone (or directly been guilty ourselves of) not checking the wing is inflated before turning on a reverse launch?

This message is intended to help others to review their launch practices and revise if required.

Safe flying.

Clive.

Semer Water - Hang Glider Complaint

Martin Baxter reports

The attached letter is the best summary of the incident that took place during the BOS last week.

o0o

Dear Martin,

I’m writing to you in my capacity as Chair of the BHPA Hang Gliding Competitions Panel, and also as a member of the DHPC, about the landowner situation which developed at Marsett, in Semerwater, during the British Open Series hang gliding competition earlier this week. I was present at the competition but did not fly that day, so I watched what happened from launch.

I am sure you will have discussed this with Simon Tomlinson, your Northern Sites Officer, to whom I have copied this letter. I believe he was contacted at the time by Trevor Birkbeck, our Meet Head, and Kevin Gay, who was looking after sites’ access on our behalf.

Briefly, about ten hang glider pilots launched from the top of Semerwater and, after soaring for varying lengths of time, ultimately landed in a large field at Moor View Farm, owned or tenanted by Mr and Mrs Woof. About five other pilots landed in the official landing field below launch, and two others went over the back. Most of the pilots in Mr and Mrs Woof’s field derigged and carried out to the road. Two, however, decided to drive their cars along the track and into the landing field to collect their gliders, opening and closing two gates on the way. They made no serious attempt to determine who owned the field or to obtain permission to drive in, and were strongly advised by other pilots not to do so. They were intercepted by the Woofs, who were justifiably very cross, and who ordered them to leave, with demands for details of insurance, and threats of demands for compensation. They did, in fact, subsequently contact the BHPA. The situation was slightly complicated by pilots tending to another pilot who had suffered a heavy landing, fortunately with no ill effects as it turned out.

When challenged by the Woofs the two pilots concerned were very apologetic, and with the calming influence of other pilots and retrieve drivers the situation became less heated, eventually becoming a more polite conversation about the difficulties of farming in general, and that field in particular. As I wrote earlier, I was not there personally, but I requested written statements from most of the pilots present. They are consistent in describing the events as I have laid them out above.

Later, Trevor and two of the people who had taken the lead in soothing Mr and Mrs Woof called at the farm with further apologies, gifts and vouchers for a meal, and promises of dire retribution to be visited on the two guilty parties. The gifts were funded by a compulsory contribution from the pilots who landed in the field, with double from the two who drove in: all of them gave willingly. I gather that it was a friendly meeting and the Woofs appeared satisfied. However, I was subsequently told that a paraglider pilot who lives in Hawes was later approached in the street by two or more farmers who demanded to know what happened, and were apparently extremely annoyed. The story has therefore spread into the local farming community with all that that implies for landowner relations with the club.

Trevor, Kevin, the HG Competitions Panel, the BHPA and I all take an extremely dim view of the actions of the pilots who drove in. They are both very experienced competition pilots and both should have known better – in fact, they do know better, but they decided to be lazy and ignore their own better judgement and the advice of other pilots given at the time. They were both strongly, and publicly, reprimanded at briefing the following morning and again at the final prizegiving, and both were disqualified for the day in question. Our freedom to act was constrained by the slightly vague wording of the local regulations for the competition. I, as a new HG Competitions Panel chair of only six months standing, will address this personally before round 2 of the competition this year. At the prizegiving I left all the gathered pilots in no doubt as to their responsibilities, or the consequences of infringements in future. Of course it is a condition of entry that the pilots abide by the local regulations. I will write to all of the entrants for round 2 and require them to read the new, clearer regulations, and withdraw from the competition if they cannot agree to them. I cannot imagine that any will object.

For me, it was encouraging that both pilots approached me independently to apologise for the trouble that they had caused. In turn, I apologise to you, to Simon and to the DHPC for the trouble which this has caused you. I sincerely hope that it does not have any further consequences for hang gliding and paragliding at Semerwater or any other Dales sites.

Yours sincerely,

Oliver Moffatt

Chair

BHPA Hang Gliding Competitions Panel

Tricky Ending - Friday 26th May 2023

Richard Meek Reports

Day 1 of Guy Anderson's British Paragliding Race Academy (BPRA) Training Camp in the Derbyshire Peak District.  We roped in Richard Carter for his expert knowledge and over a full English breakfast at the Derbyshire & Lancs Gliding Club  pored over the forecast.  

Guy (R ), Richy (C ), myself (L) planning over breakfast in the Derbyshire & Lancashire Gliding Club

Summary; light south easterly winds, five stars to start then some cloud moving in from the north west to shut things down.  Richy proposed a 100km triangle starting from Man Tor (east), he doesn't get out of bed for anything less, but thought there was a low chance of success.  First leg south east beyond Chesterfield, north to Wombwell then back.  As we drove to launch a message came through that we'd be going to Rushup as the wind, contrary to forecast, was a little west of south.

Around ten of us were on Rushup ready to rock n roll in advance of our anticipated 11AM start.  The sky was looking good with plenty of sunshine and cumulus around but light wind and few signs of thermic action on the hill. First hiccup of the day - flat battery in the radio ! Fat lot of good that's going to be - I'm going to miss out on Guy's expert feedback in real-time drat and double drat.

Sky looking good on launch.  (That’s me hidden under the submarine harness)

A bird thermalling to the east was just the sign we needed; Richy was off first, I attempted to follow seconds later but fluffed my attempt and missed that opportunity.  Perhaps just as well given Richy landed and had a long, sweaty walk up.  

It was 11:30 by the time the next launchable cycle came through, this time Guy was off first and straight in to a good climb making it look effortless.  Rest of us had a real battle to stay up and climb out, it was midday by the time Indy and I tagged the start cylinder at 4,300'.

As this was race training I was out on the submarine harness.  After a number of test flights I had managed to resolve my droop and wrinkle problems, on the harness that is, I'm still as wrinkly as ever !  Or so I thought.  Feedback after the flight was that it was pretty much the worst sub set-up anyone had seen, so bad in fact anyone who cast eyes on it immediately felt queasy.

Leaving the Peaks highground into the flatlands.

Guy and Richy were already a couple of km down the course but we regrouped over the quarry at Bradwell. Climbed 500' back to 3,600 before crossing the valley to Bradwell Edge for another 500' to 4,300. Our gaggle of four (Guy, Richy, Indy and myself)  stayed together and worked well until just before 2PM.  

Guy and I were leading out and took two turns in not-much-of-anything which turned in to a nice 2m/s for Indy & Richy just 2 mins behind. I pushed back to join the Y-Men climb but didn't connect to the same strength core. The Wise Brothers (get it ?) left at 4 grand catching up with Guy whilst I was still scrabbling in broken lift.  And hat dear readers is which is how we got separated.

Over Chesterfield just before we got separated. With Richy (L - Alpina), Guy (C - Zeno 2) and myself (low R - Zeno 2). Photo by Indy

The Three Musketeers bagged TP1 at 14:07.  My climb eventually perked up to 3m/s topping out at 4,500' but I was seven minutes behind them as I bagged the turnpoint.  On my first glide after the turnpoint I actually over took them, correction, under took them and we were together as we got a climb over Chesterfield hospital. Trouble was me still being 1,000' lower so we weren't really in the same phase as we progressed northwards over the gorgeous countryside.  

In England’s green and pleasant land

I tried to put the others to the back of my mind and just flew the sky following the best looking clouds.  This involved deviating a little west of the leg two course line directly over Sheffield city centre.  Steel city is a big ol’ place, landing options were front and centre in my mind as I sunk to below 2,000’ AGL.  It took me about 30 mins to transit across the city which put me further behind the others.

Lower than I would have liked over the centre of Sheffield.

We were in the same area around turnpoint 2 above West Melton but again I was low (1,200' AGL) while the others had skied out (4,800').  I found a climb but the south easterly breeze drifted me away from the turn point which I had to return to but luck was in my favour for once - there was a 2up climb slap bang above TP2 which took me to 5,000’.

Looking south-east across Wath upon Dearne from turn point 2

Turning to head south west on the last leg looking into the Peaks the overcast was obvious with huge swathes of shade.  I didn't think there was much chance of completing the flight but resolved to carry on in my own merry way and make best of what was offered.

 

On the northern outskirts of Sheffield I was low again (1,100' AGL) but found a little something which took me back to 3,500’.  I didn't know at the time but passed just 500m south of Chapeltown where Richy & Indy had landed.  A lovely 2.5m/s climb over Parsons Cross took me back to 4,800 with 23km to go.  Ahead a few patches of sun could be seen on the moors behind Stanage Edge - somewhat off course line but if I could make it there might still be a way to close the flight.  

As it turned out I never did reach that patch of sunshine. At 17:15 I passed over Guy who had landed 10km short continuing my own death glide towards Lady Bower Reservoir.  However, down to 900AGL I did find a patch of zeroes.  I was hoping it would develop into something stronger but after a few 360s I was happy just to be drifting in roughly the right direction.  Bamford Edge, bathed in sun, was tantalisingly close, just 2km south west but would’ve been cross wind to the south-easterly.

Twenty minutes of patient drifting in zeroes paid off.  As I drifted over the high ground immediately east of Lady Bower it released and developed into something better.

Just a second.  

Hold on a cotton pickin’ minute.  

This ain't just zeroes anymore.

This is a 0.1 up, an absolute rocket compared to my familiar zeroes.  Yeah baby !

And that developed in to my saviour 1m/s climb back to 3,100' with 7.7km to go. I left on a necky final glide but the outlook improved when I connected to Lose Hill and the gentle meteo allowed me to surf the final 3km gaining altitude over goal.

Landed at 6PM for a 6.5hr 100km declared FAI triangle, 105.9km undeclared. Chuffed ? Not half !

 From left. Richy, myself, Guy and Indy.  Sartorial elegance ? I think not.
