Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

Only 6 (out of 16) committee members made it to last month’s committee meeting at the Red Lion in Burley in Wharfedale.  Many thanks to those that made the effort, and especially to Tom and Rob who have over an hour’s drive each way.  We welcomed Rob to his first committee meeting in the new role of First Aid Officer.

We talked through the incident on 20 Apr that resulted in the death of our good friend Kev McLoughlin on Park Fell.  Having landed, taken off his harness and helmet, and started packing away his wing, Kev died of natural causes (pulmonary embolism).  There was standing room only at his funeral in Lancaster, and the family was very grateful to all fellow pilots that turned out to see Kev off on his final XC.  The comment, from a family member, that sticks in my mind was, ‘it would be the perfect way to depart this earth: it’s just a shame that it wasn’t 20 years later’. Kev was 63.

We discussed whether there was anything to learn from a club perspective, and concluded that the advice on our current safety card is sufficient: in the event of a serious incident dial 999.  The police hold the responsibility for notifying the next of kin, and the BHPA if an investigation is required.

I won’t steal too much of Rob’s thunder when it comes to First Aid, suffice to say that he’s hoping to organise a course over the winter months.

Those of you that have attended recent club nights will be aware of interoperability issues between our projector and a selection of laptops.  Stef has researched and costed the various options, and the committee has authorised him to spend £400 on a new projector.  With our contribution to DEI, a new projector, and a possible subsidy towards First Aid, it looks like it could be an expensive year.

Planning continues on both the Fly-In (5 – 8 Sep) and the Farmers’ Dinner on 12 Oct at Knights Stainforth.

We received a note of thanks from the BPCup organisers for allowing them to use our sites (less Semer Water).  Particular thanks must go to Ed for his guidance.

We’re still trying to unravel the ownership issues at Windbank.  Until we do, the advice to members (and visiting pilots) is only to pay your £3 per car if challenged.

Richard Meek reports

Back to the Peak district on a similar forecast to yesterday (I bombed at 56km against a goal of 186) although wind due to have a little more east in it (NNE-NE) which should make it easier to negotiate the airspace.  Launch from Treak Cliff, cross Winnats Pass and start the task from Longcliffe.

187km task to Lydney

Launch 11:40 soar around for 15 mins til a lovely 2ms climb out.  Good gaggle formed including Jacob Butterworth, Richard Butterworth, Alex Colbeck and Richard Carter (1) and we worked well spreading out to increase our search pattern.

11:54 on the climb out.  Looking north east from Long Cliffe.

For 65km to Stafford we mainly bounced along under the 4,500' airspace rarely dropping below 3,000' AMSL, however, the ground is often around 1,000' so we're not talking huge clearance.

12:22 3,800’ south east of Buxton looking south west under some top cover

Whilst we kept high we weren't really fast, around 25kph, the top cover slowing things down.  

Looking west towards Stoke on Trent. Alex to the left, Butty ahead to the right

Between Stafford and Telford we got low, taking turns to get really low and either dig ourselves out or, in the case of Jacob and RC1 their feet met ground.   My own low save came in the form of a bubble from 500' AGL.

14:30 500’ AGL.  Brown fields + woods = source & trigger.

Scrabbling around low slowed us allowing a following gaggle including Jacob Aubrey, RC2 and CJ, to merge at 14:50.

I wasn't confident that goal was achievable with 115km to go but south of Telford things started to improve with a climb to 4,900', the next 5,200 and so on.

The view to the south from 4,900’

Having safely negotiated the airspace we started to put a bit of east into our glide to maintain our line to goal passing west of the Malverns just after 5pm.  

16:57 Malverns to the left of my pod

We've still got 48km to go and the cumulus are beginning to be less numerous and less well defined.  Undeterred we crack on and since Telford we've been above 2,700' all the time, finding climbs just when we need them.  The gaggle was down to four by this stage; Alex, Richard, Jacob and myself.  Good comms between 3 of us, dodgy radio for #4, we were sharing our ideas for each move and then committing as a team.

17:54 looking south to the Bristol Channel

At 17:40 with 30km to go we climb to 5,100 and get our first view of the Bristol Channel and our goal on the north shore.  There are some clouds ahead along our course line, this may actually be possible.  I check my notes to confirm last train leaves at 19:14.  OK, that gives us some time to work with, let's go for it.

A good line for the next 7km sees us back to 5,1 with 23km to go ahead is Cinderford with a growing cloud to the south.  We glide on and connect with that climb which is our last giving us  glide to goal.

18:05 Cinderford ahead with cloud marking our final climb

It occurred to me that this climb just may be a sea breeze convergence, a moment of roughness and decreasing wind speed raised that suspicion and fearing our tailwind would turn into a headwind we each took the final climb higher than we needed.  Alex and I sailed in with >3,000' to spare.

18:32 A well researched LZ is a low stress LZ

On final glide I'd reminded the gang that time was of the essence; a quick pack and jog to the station to ensure we made the last train.  They heeded my advice and t'was I the laggard of the bunch being assisted to pack my glider away, how embarrassing !

As we made our way to the station I received a call from a local pilot, Simon Ford, who somehow knew where we were - the miracles of live tracking eh ?  Simon offered a lift, what a generous fellow !  I thanked him but confirmed we were on schedule for the train which we made with a few minutes to spare.

On the safety of the train we could catch up with all the messages (congratulations and wind ups) from friends.  Change at Cheltenham, grab a cheeky chicken and chips from the fast food place whilst waiting for the next train to Birmingham.

Minor issue in Brum - Jacob and I sat gabbling and forgot to get off and change to the Manchester bound train. Doh !  We figured that out as our train setoff with the announcement advising it wouldn't be stopping where we expected.  Ooops.  An alternative plan was hatched to take this through to Sheffield and then an Uber.  A few quid lighter but similar time to the train; we were at the cars by 23:00 and then home by 00:40.

A full day.  Almost pleased Monday not flyable so I can sleep in and catch up on the to-do list.

Clive Sury reports

Lessons from a trip to Ager, Spain

At the end of April I went on a trip with FlyAger.  I’ve never been and it’s the base for Passion Paragliding and FlyAger.  The course was categorised as Pilot Plus for flyer development coaching run by Nicky Moss and her partner Jordi from FlyAger.

Ager is a small medieval village below a ridge that stretches for over 50km and it is just below the Spanish Pyrenees, west of Barcelona.  I’ve never been here before and was keen to visit another flying region.  Plus, given the flight to and from Barcelona, it afforded an opportunity to tag on a few days' stay in the city itself, with my wife joining me on this part of the trip.  Happy days, what more could you want? - Flying, great scenery, good food and city sights as a bonus. However, I’ll stick to writing about flying here and leave anyone who wants to know about the tourist scene in Barcelona to contact me separately.

The Ager ridge is dramatic overlooking the valley with the village nestled in the valley with a thousand year old castle and a monastery prominent at its peak.  A scene reminiscent to me of an archetypal painting by Monet, albeit that’s France but I hope this evokes a sense of the expanse of the valley and that it’s a view that gives the impression that it has been unchanged for many centuries.  The accommodation provided is in the village in a medieval town house that had been renovated by Jordi to a very high standard and whilst it’s a small village, there are up to 5 restaurants within it.  At the time I was there, there was only opportunity to visit 3 of them with the food excellent and very reasonably priced.  Below is a photo of the petrol station which is actually more of a cafe/ restaurant and very popular with the locals as well as, visiting flyers, walkers and bikers - during the week their 3 course home-cooked lunch menu is just €14, which was hard to say no to, even if one’s normal lunch routine is a just a sandwich.

All good so far with ticks for the flying site, accommodation and local environs.  The disappointment was the weather! Two days were unflyable and on a third it was only possible to practise ground-handling in a highly variable wind, changing directions 180 degrees with very high-end gusts. Previously I’ve been very lucky with good weather on trips with normally just a day being unflyable.  Bad weather is always a risk and this time I was unfortunate.  However, with this experience of spring weather with the thermals being tight, punchy and intermittent, if I went again it would be at the end of the summer.  I was informed that this is when there are more consistent thermals coming up from the dry rocky valley below.  

Despite fewer flying days I managed to learn a lot in a short time.  I’ve always viewed being post CP as like passing one’s driving test where learning to drive more completely comes later through more experience and constant learning.  In the same way, I still have much to improve regarding flying and general paragliding knowledge - yet with limited flying time in the UK available in which to do it.  Therefore, this time I decided to keep notes on what I’d learnt each day which are detailed below.  I hope they are useful to others and happy to discuss with anyone should they contact me.  Martin Baxter has read through these and sent to me some useful comments which I’ve included by the relevant point - thanks Martin.

Trip notes:

  • Have your arms next to risers when flying. Ensures greater stability to react to the glider for collapses and aerodynamic
  • Launch as if ground handling. Take pressure of launch in one’s mindset and ensure the glider is inflated and under control
  • Landing. Maintain focus on landing spot the whole time coming in to land. Ensures maintaining projection of glider to landing spot
  • Landing flare when feet are a table height off the ground. Hands up for full speed then hard flareI (Martin Baxter suggests that it depends on the wind strength.  In stronger winds a progressive flare might be all that's needed.  In lighter winds a dynamic flare not only gives greater speed allowing you to punch through unwanted lift, but gives added drag whilst arresting your descent).
  • Figure of 8 approach to landing. Maintain a figure of 8 and can be either side of the final approach if the direct approach into wind is busy with another glider. (Martin Baxter comment - I was quite surprised that they encouraged the figure of 8 (S Turn) approach: very much of a UK (bad?) habit.  It's often frowned upon by our European friends because it tends to monopolise the final approach.  Most Europeans tend to favour the circuit approach (lose height upwind, downwind, crosswind and in).  At busy sites this makes it much easier to work out who has landing priority, deconflict, and adjust if you hit sink or lift).
  • Punchy thermals. Quick judgement to assess thermal’s ability to use for a climb. Work the thermal to maintain height and eventually (hopefully) gain height
  • Active flying in mixed wind air.
  • Check lines setup correctly (risers not twisted) and nothing in lines before launching
  • Pay attention to wind direction in mountain flying. North wind from the back but comes round the valley from the west. Means that if getting close to the mountain wind can be different to takeoff and therefore, pay attention to any turbulence from near the mountain (can’t take ridge soaring for granted)
  • Test radio pre take off and also just after launch
  • Adjust the pod harness for a more upright seating position. Improved stability
  • Test pullout reserve on the first day to ensure it comes out clean.
  • Cauliflower. Put the wing into a bunch to launch. It opens out more smoothly in the stronger wind and less pressure (Martin Baxter note - This was demonstrated to me in Spain.  I tried it at Wether Fell, got hoiked off my feet with a knot in my lines and had to bottom land to sort it out.  I haven't used it again!).
  • Orange b line on the chilli 5 is the line to the wing tip. Pull on this to get a cravat out
  • Launch technique. Brake to stop overshooting. Take big steps to move. Move backwards to keep power in the glider.
  • On a windy day see if there’s a building or other feature to pack up the glider in its lee side
  • For a quick glider pack grab the wingtip and fast roll up the wing to the centre. May need to do this if packing up in a high wind situation
  • Don’t land in a circle in tight landing fields. On downwind leg grave risk of being sent back to any obstacles or short of landing area. Always do figure of 8
  • With mountain thermals look for the front of the mountain range in the morning as thermals coming off the lower ridge. Later in the day / pm thermals on top of the mountain ridge. Use but don’t go too far back, use a thermal but exit to join another thermal at the front. Like a staircase
  • Avoid where possible headwinds on cross country. V hard to enter thermals this way as descending air in front of thermal will likely take the gilder down.
  • Wait for 4 seconds upon entering a thermal to have greater chance of being in it more fully to turn and use it
  • Use all the thermal up to the cloudbase. As the thermal fans out it will be smoother and less strong but the extra 20% of height gain will be invaluable
  • Practise ‘dolphins’ - this is pitch pendulums - note practice this at height and clear of other pilots.
  • Keep awareness of other pilots in the air to avoid collisions. Especially important when thermalling.
  • Use a speedbar in a headwind to move more quickly and arrive higher. Practice on same beat with and without speed bar to compare where you are height wise
  • Think security. Think through options on height and landing options before making a transition eg crossing a gorge
  • Control and pitch to avoid collapses.
  • Rolling. To overcome, go for a turn 360. Create energy with turn to stop the roll
  • Thermalling. Efficiency is maintaining bank angle throughout the turn circle. Use weight and inside brake to maintain turn.
  • Look at other pilots. If they’re in a stronger thermal close to use, change to theirs. ‘Change elevators’
  • Beware of buildings, trees, obstacles at the landing. Turbulence can be up to 2-3 times the height of the obstacle. Therefore avoid flying directly above or behind them approaching landing
  • Look to land at the front (downwind) of the landing area to avoid overrunning. (Martin Baxter feedback -  I aim for the middle as there is as much chance of undershooting as overshooting).
  • Daisy chain risers to have neat and avoid tangles if temporarily packing equipment. Make a fist loop and feed through next loop
  • Look at topography as well as wind direction. The wind may change on a hill if the topography is causing turbulence
  • In mountains a rule of thumb is 50-75% cloud can be ok (pending other conditions ok). Over 75% cloud not likely flyable
  • Rain on a wing is risky as it shortens the stall point on the brakes dramatically. Keep maximum speed and much less brake travel
  • Have the brake handle held within the hand with a half wrap. This way can quickly release if needing to pull reserve
  • Fold away glider attached with riser lines set correctly for a quicker launch setup

Naturally, I regret not being able to fly as much as on previous trips but hope that these lessons keep me in good stead to progress in flying competence - with much still to learn.  I would recommend flying in Ager, noting later in Spring or end of the Summer probably being better weather conditions.  Included here are a few more photos showing the ridge and village:

Incident at Model Ridge as reported by a club member:

Whiteout at Model Ridge

Model Ridge is a members’ site for the North Yorks Sailwing club, with fantastic views, loads of parking and Lordstones café at the top. It takes a Northerly round to NNE and offers the option of walking up to Cringle moor if it’s too light, or a short drive to Marske/Saltburn if it’s too strong (all taking roughly the same wind direction).

The 7th of May had a promising forecast: at 10:30am there was a 12mph breeze onto the hill but Cringle moor was covered in cloud, so I went to get some breakfast at the café. At 11:30 the sun hadn’t broken through, but cloud had lifted so you could just see the top of Cringle, and there were 2 pilots up, soaring Model ridge, so I got my gear out and ground-handled to get a feel for the wind.

The speed and direction were perfect so I decided to try a few beats up and down the ridge. My goal for later in the day was to try and fly all the way round to Cringle: something I’d never managed before, as you have to gain a bit of height, and the walk up is arduous if you miss it.

Because I was only planning a short flight and top landing I didn’t bother putting my flight instrument out, but did turn my radio and vario on as I wanted to map where the lift was. The top of the ridge and the moor were clear of cloud, so I took off. Into some of the best soaring conditions I’ve ever seen at Model…

I’ve previously had to scratch around a bit to find the best areas of lift, but this time the whole of the ridge was soarable, so I used it as an opportunity to explore the gulley further along: it doesn’t look like it gives much lift and I’d always shied away for fear of losing too much height. I made it across without losing much height, but thought it best to gain a bit back before attempting to cross it and land back on the ridge. Fortunately, there was a bit of lift the moor just before the corner onto the main bowl that worked well and I knew I’d make it back easily.

But I could also see the bowl open up before me as I lifted. If I crept round then that would be me for the day: I would have made it on my first flight! I had forgotten my actual flight objective (a few beats up and down to feel the air). I knew it was cloudy but the takeoff was completely clear of cloud, and in any case, the cloud was lifting.

As I turned into the bowl, I saw a pilot about 100m out, heading back the other way, which did register as a potential warning, but visibility was still good, so I thought ‘fine, I’ll go to the end of the moor then head back’.

Flying about 30m out from the ridge I was in about 1m/s lift without having to look for it: wonderful. I’d now flown twice as far as I ever have and was feeling elated. As I turned back I remembered how quickly it went white. 1s, maybe 2s. Every direction was the same shade of white: No horizon, no sun, no ridge. Just white.

I remembered to try and face away from the ridge, so because I was probably facing into wind when I lost sight, I resisted the temptation to steer and kept my weight central. I pulled both ears in, then pushed the bar out gradually until it was fully out. I was desperate to lose height, longing to see the ground, so hearing the vario climb at one point didn’t raise my spirits. From hindsight, this was probably due to where I crossed the ridge, going downwind…

The ground did eventually appear; thankfully I had about 50m of height and could line up for landing, albeit in a field full of bracken and tree stumps, moving rather quickly. I found this puzzling as there was a decent breeze when I took off but turning ‘into wind’ i.e, pointing downhill was just as fast as going along the hill.

I didn’t recognise the landing field and assumed I’d managed to keep myself into wind and flown out past the bowl onto a shallower hill. I was actually flying in the (thankfully shallow) lee of Cringle moor trying to figure out how to avoid breaking my ankles on what cannot be described as ‘even ground’ moving under me at about 20mph. One late flare and a bruised knee later and I’m down. I text the 365 group to let them know and gather up my (wet!) wing into a field for packing.

Debriefed with one of the pilots on Model, laid out my wing to dry out, had a couple of flights to ‘get back on the horse’ then headed home.

A couple of things struck me that might be due to the mindset of being a low-airtime pilot

  1. I’ve never gone XC, so I didn't feel I really needed (or even deserve) to use a flight instrument. This took away my compass and flight log
  2. When people talk about flights, it’s usually about distance. 4 years and 7 hours is a lot of top to bottoms, so there is an internal push to experiment with flying longer distances. At what point this moves from being helpful, to unhelpful might only be possible with hindsight. I’m lucky in that I can get out on most flyable days and imagine the pressure to progress is greater for weekend warriors.
  3. I’m not confident with my PLF and feel like a wally practicing it. I have never seen anyone else practice it ever, and don’t think the ‘standing and jumping sideways’ method prepares you for when you need to use it in anger.

Causal factors: task blindness, not having a compass and not keeping a proper lookout for clouds are the main mistakes. Seeing a pilot lower down flying back from Cringle should have rung alarms bells but I got carried away.

Coach’s Comments

Thanks for sending this in and I’m glad to hear it turned out okay.

I think your conclusions about the cause are right and it's worth remembering that any one of us can find ourselves in a sticky situation like this.

Cloud is always going to be disorienting, especially so without any way of maintaining a heading. You have some visual cues that can help orientation, but not always. Looking down will give you a misty view of the ground below for a while. Thinner cloud which forms quickly is often not vertically developed enough to completely block out the sun, so look up too. Even when concentrating on flying a specific heading from a flight instrument, your sense of balance over-reacting and the lag from control inputs makes it difficult to steer consistently.

Landing downwind needn’t be too much of a cause for concern. Avoid the temptation to progressively put more brake on to reduce your groundspeed. A well timed deep flare can scrub off most of the speed. Practise this when you’re landing in nil or light wind. Keep your hands up until it’s time to flare and then a deep application of brake will convert to some height and a light drop onto your feet. Downwind, you’re likely to still need to run off some speed. Greg Hammertonhas some nice videos on this. Chrigel Maurer’s fly on a wall slope landing technique shows how much speed control is possible with good timing.

A couple of things you maybe didn’t consider or ruled out. A spiral out of cloud would not have been wise when close to a ridge. A hook turn, low down to avoid your downwind landing would have risked a spin or hitting the ground, mid turn, when the glider still has energy.

First Aid

Rob Urselmann reports

As our honourable chair already mentioned; a FA course will be planned for the winter season.

The idea is to host a one-day FA course with more focus on most likely PG related injuries (i.e. crash injuries) than the default one-day FA syllabus and use more PG focussed scenarios (i.e. people wrapped in lines and cloth in a (semi-) remote location rather than a motor-vehicle crash on a city street or chainsaws in a forest). This means the ‘default’ one-day/8 hour syllabus will need to be adapted a bit, which has already been discussed with potential providers and can be accommodated. Also we want to host it at a location in the Dales central to our members, which excluded one potential course provider who teaches only at their own venue in a non-central location.  

Whilst under discussion in the Committee, at the moment there has been no decision on any possible financial incentive from the club. The other thing under consideration is whether club coaches, who are a. more likely to be a present at group events and b. looked upon to take charge, should get ‘preferential’ access. However, I suspect this could easily be a mute point unless the course becomes oversubscribed.  

As usual: we love to hear your views and you can reach me on firstaid@dhpc.org.uk or you can find me on the club WA & TG groups for a DM.

Questionnaire results

There were 58 responses to the First Aid (FA) questionnaire, or about 1:4 of our members. That’s pretty good in my book, so a big thank you to all respondents (you know who you are).

Why: Well here’s a breakdown of the responses and some other interesting insights from the questionnaire.

From the 58 respondents,  38 (66%) are interested in doing a course, 20 are not. I’ll refer to these as the ‘Yes’ and ‘No’ groups for brevity.

When are you interested in doing a course?

From the 38, apart from two people who’d like a course asap, the numbers were pretty even split between ‘coming winter season’ and ‘whenever’.  So to me that was a clear indication to not bother organising anything this summer.

Those who wish to do a course asap, you can do any one or two-day FA course and even if the scenarios might have a  paragliding element (i.e. a person bungled up in lines and cloth to begin with) it will be totally relevant.

In order to assist the club committee in how this endeavour might (or not) be sponsored by the club, we also asked how many hours respondents fly in the Dales with other people each year.

The majority of respondents in both camps fly between 10-60 hours, which is most likely in line with the general hours people fly.

A good number of people are actually (at the moment) current on First Aid, and we even have a number of qualified emergency care providers in our midst. Of the 33% of respondents not interested in our DHPC FA course, over half are current. Fantastic !   On the other hand, there are several people who’ve never done one and are not interested. Thank you for your honesty, but maybe have another think? None of us are invulnerable, and I’d love for you to know what and what not to do if you happen to be the person on-scene when I have an incident.

Do you carry a First Aid kit when flying?

Again, very similar responses between the Yes & No groups.  Over half of respondents carry NO first aid kit at all. Not even a plaster or a paracetamol. Not all incidents are massive: consider what you would like to have if you sprain your ankle on landing or long hike in or out (as a matter of fact, just last week I jerked my knee when I slipped into a hole whilst walking down on a too-high wind day).

At first glance I suspected the highly trained people would be the ones carrying the full kits, but this is actually not the case.

Do you carry an emergency satellite beacon (InReach, Spot, Bivy, etc)?

As expected, three quarters of respondents do not carry a beacon.  However, the ‘Yes’ group is twice as likely to carry a beacon as the ‘No’ group. One would think they like to be more prepared for any scenario and carry the ‘full’ FA kit, but a third of those who do, do not carry a FA kit and the rest a ‘medium’ one.  

Interestingly, there is nobody with the ‘full package’ of being FA trained and carrying both a full ‘wilderness’ FA kit and a satellite beacon.
