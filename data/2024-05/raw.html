Kevin McLoughlin, Grandad, Flyer and Club Member. Died this year after doing something he loved.

Kevin McLoughlin

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

It was with some sadness I heard that it was Kevin McLoughlin who had died at Park Fell on Saturday April 20th. He had been flying and it looked as though he’d had a medical incident whilst packing up after landing at the bottom. The emergency services and Cave Rescue were called in but could not do anything for him.

He’d been flying regularly and part of the club since around 2000 and first became a club coach in 2003 (photo below). Contributing back to others became something he just did. He’s been a coach for 20 years, has been the Dales Club’s comp organiser in 2006/7 and then again in 2009/10. The BPCup won’t be quite the same without Kevin too. He’s been competing in it, probably for about 17 years and helping the organisation when it comes to setting up the Dales BPCup round too.

In his time, in and out of competition he’s had his fair share of flights too. From Mallerstang across the southern Lakes to the coast at Seascale would be a flight any of us would be privileged to have experienced. And an epic, starting from Wild Boar Fell, across the central Lakeland Fells to the coast at Cockermouth must have put a grin on his face.

One thing stood out to many, he was an easy guy to spend time with. Whether on a long walk up a fellside or in a thermal or on retrieve. Perhaps a little quiet when you first meet him and then interesting, stoic, understated but imaginative thereafter. This is reflected in the comments I see from the fliers and friends alike, “lovely man” is the phrase that seems to get repeated. “Gone too soon” is another. He’d kept himself well and didn’t seem to struggle when it came to getting up to launch. Definitely a reminder to all of us to use our time. We don’t know how much of it we have.

I remember the first Dales Hike & Fly, it was getting late in the day and the group I was in met up with Kev on the gruelling walk from Brantside to Barkin. Forty odd kilometres into the route and in his sixties, he was not for giving up if he could help it. His cheeriness and a few shared biscuits spurred us on.

He’ll be missed as a coach and as a regular in the western Dales crowd. I’m thankful I had him on the coaching team and for what he gave of his experience to others. He also found me the odd thermal from time to time too :-)    

If you spent time with him on the ground or in the air, perhaps you like to listen and remember.
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n6jqG4j10J8 

Rest in peace Kev.

Coaching course at the Dalesbridge Centre, 2003. Kev on the far right hand side.

Thanks to the North Yorks Police, Yorkshire Ambulance Service & Cave Rescue Organisation, Alan, Ed, Tam and Martin who’ve either helped on scene or with family contact.

Tam has been in contact with Kevin’s daughter. The funeral is on Tuesday 7th May at Lancaster Crematorium, 1.30pm. Followed on at the Vale of Lune rugby club, Lancaster. They did say that fellow fliers are very welcome to attend. Tam will have spaces in his car going from Bingley and I’m sure a lot of the Junction 36 / western Dales crowd will be there. Use Telegram Chat to organise lifts (https://t.me/+v9PY0V20iZQxMTI0 if you’re not on it already).

Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

Quite a lot of contentious stuff discussed by the committee over the last month.  In ascending order of controversy…

In order to help to assimilate recently qualified CPs into the club, our chief coach is going to set up a WhatsApp group for new pilots who join the club each year – i.e. class of 2024.  Each group will include a coach to give general advice and answer (no such thing as) stupid questions, but the main aim is to connect newly qualified pilots together – something that has been shown to help with that first intimidating season of flying.

For some time, it has bothered me that the committee decides upon their own rate of travelling expenses.  The committee agreed with me so, in future, the proposed rates will be agreed by the members at each AGM.  Of course, any member contesting those rates will be invited to join the committee!

We’ve concluded that we’re not going to spend money upgrading the website and forum.  It’s all backed up and if it disappears for a few days most people will be none the wiser.

The farmers’ dinner, in lieu of site fees, has been set for 12 Oct 24 at Knight Stainforth.  Members are, of course, most welcome but, in recognition of the fact that committee members have a duty to do most of the hosting, they will receive one free meal each, not including drinks.

Rob Urselmann has been co-opted onto the committee as First Aid Officer.  Whilst the committee has some ideas, Rob has been given a ‘blank sheet of paper’ to propose a long term First Aid strategy for the club.  Nothing has been decided yet, but it has been suggested that we should budget for a sum of £500 (£2.50 per member) each year.  Rob’s has outlined some initial thoughts later in this edition.

But, by far the most controversial topic of discussion this month has been Diversity, Equity (not Equality) and Inclusion (DEI).  Some of you attended Rhian’s thought-provoking presentation at the last club night but if you didn’t, you can see it here.  I’m reasonably confident that, just like the BHPA, whilst our membership doesn’t reflect society as a whole, our committee does represent our members (see penultimate paragraph).  We don’t actively discriminate against anybody (except hang glider pilots perhaps😉) and as a result of Rhian’s presentation and subsequent discussion we are now more DEI aware.

Westie has managed to convince top female and X-Alps contestant, Kinga Masztalerz, to take part in the X-Lakes in June.  She’s also agreed to run a masterclass for female pilots only, and do a number of presentations promoting DEI whilst she’s in the UK.  Rhian plans to make a film about Kinga’s visit promoting Hike & Fly, DEI and paragliding in general.  She’s asked the club to sponsor the project.

We don’t normally hold club nights in June, but the committee is agreed that if Kinga is able to come to a venue of our choice, we’d be willing to cover local travelling expenses, a meal and drinks.  Stef’s on it.

The controversial bit was how much extra we’re prepared to pay towards the making of the film.  Some of us felt an instinct to support Rhian’s project, whilst others feared that it amounts to pouring money into a black hole with little evidence of any practical benefits.  After much discussion the committee took a vote on the matter and 66% agreed that a £500 donation would be appropriate.  But some members of the committee felt that we should also seek approval from the membership, so we conducted a similar poll on the members only forum.  The overall results were reassuringly similar (62% in favour of £500).  We have now made the £500 commitment; and we can be confident that the committee accurately reflects the views of the membership, at least on this subject.    

The CSC has committed £750 to the project.  The BHPA has decided not to fund it, citing a lack of a coherent and long-term strategy. They have however agreed to set up a working group to formulate a strategy and drive DEI forwards.  Whilst supporting the principle, the PSC only felt able to make a donation of £50 because they already give quite a lot of money to charity.  

For the record, the previously agreed DHPC policy is that we don’t give your money to charity – that’s an individual’s choice.

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Parlick is 10 times riskier to fly than Windbank!

Why is this?  Well, according to incident reports to the BHPA there were 10 reports over the last 2 years at Parlick to just 1 report at Windbank.

OK, I’ll confess, this is a complete misuse of statistics and I’ve written this to get your attention.  I don't know the topography and meteorological conditions of both these sites to make a judgement regarding their safety per se – and certainly not by way of comparison.

However, why would I like your attention to this? I hope you can guess the point I’m making, which is that there is a massive difference between the level of incident reporting between these sites.  Furthermore, the Dales Club of nearly 200 members reported just 2 incidents last year and (not) coincidentally I know both the pilots concerned very well.  Bearing in mind that this is for any incident both inside and outside the UK.

Many of us read the incident reports published in Skywings each month to keep awareness of what the causes are and apply any lessons learned from them.  Clearly this is dependent on people reporting incidents and by way of reminder, an incident is an event not just resulting in an accident but could have resulted in harm to self, others or property.  Each report helps towards understanding what types of events are likely to happen, how much risk the activity is and what to take into account for personal or professional training programmes.

Reporting an incident to the BHPA is done online at Submit Incident Report (bhpa.co.uk) with the club safety officer requiring to be informed at safety@dhpc.org.uk

It takes very little time to complete and is published anonymously.  The flying season is now upon us.  The intent behind this article is to raise awareness of how useful incident reporting is and to encourage a more accurate number submitted.  Thereby, increase all flyers' knowledge of incidents affecting their safety and to consider how to implement mitigating actions into their own flying practices.

Safe flying,

Clive.

Do You Know What To Do, Right Now?

Rob Urselmann reports

Note: I first drafted this article a few days before the terrible news of Kev’s passing on the hill and, being new in this role of co-opted first aid officer, considered pulling this version in lieu for a ‘softer’ approach. I was however encouraged by the committee to keep it as is.

Imaging this:

You are out flying with your mate (he/she/they*) and are having a grand time under beautiful skies. Suddenly, you realise you can’t see your mate’s wing in the air, and after some looking around you see them on the deck …wing out… not moving… no response on radio.   SHIT SHIT SHIT.    You are the only other person around.

Close your eyes, imagine this scenario: What do YOU do right now?!    Be honest.

  1. I have no clue and ‘freeze’: I convince myself they’ll be alright, probably just radio not functioning and taking a breather, and I continue flying (whilst secretly shitting myself).

  1. I have no clue and panic: I fly over and drop down to land quickly. After I miraculously manage to land unharmed myself, I drop my gear, run over and rip their helmet and gear off to see how they’re injured. Need to call 999. Shit my phone is still on my gear deck which has now blown into a tree. Once I recover it, shit no signal.

  1. I recall a bit from reading, youtube, or a first aid course many years ago: I fly over and descend with my heart in my throat, landing a bit rough but ok. I quickly bundle my wing and throw my harness on it to secure it, grab my phone and run over. Something “ABC” but what exactly. My mate is breathing so I don’t touch them. I secure his wing and run up hill to find a signal.

  1. I’ve got this: I take a deep breath and slow my heart rate.  I fly over and descend within my capabilities (aided by the SIV course I did not long ago) and land a bit away from the emergency site. I quickly secure my own gear, pull out my phone, DHPC Emergency Card, compact First Aid kit and my tracker, and start applying “DRC ABC”. There’s no phone signal so I activate my emergency satellite beacon.

Are you #4? Please become my flying buddy !

For everybody else, how do you feel right now? Do you wish you knew better what to do?

Maybe you think this is not going to happen to you?  

BHPA statistics 2020-23 show that every year there are 150-180 accidents outside a school setting, 100-120 resulting in injury, half of those serious**. With 7000 members, that means roughly each year 1:100 sustain serious injury.  DHPC has over 200 members.

As you might have seen or heard, the club is once again considering organising a (possibly subsidised) outdoor First Aid course. With existing committee members already loaded, they saw fit to introduce the new role of First Aid officer. I have a bit of background in emergency care and was already well aware how out of date I myself am getting, so this is a topic near my heart.

Considerations to the club are

  • Current level of FA experience in the flying membership.
  • Level and duration of course required/desired: one v two days
  • Cost of course
  • Level of club sponsorship (if any)
  • Timing: Course asap or next winter: balancing the conflict of members wanting to be OTH instead of in a course.
  • Location and currency: do pilots who are out regularly in the Dales with others take preference in order to maximise ‘return’ to the wider membership.
  • other

Course:

A comprehensive, remote-setting, substantially hands-on course is two days, but provides a solid basis for all types of emergencies in a remote incident environment. A standard one-day version does not cover crash injuries (fractures and spinal), the main risk in PG, and would have to be adapted.  Examples of FA providers are Jocky Sanderson’s Wilderness FA course,  First Aid Cumbria or High Peak one or two day PG focussed course.  Some clubs seem to fill FA courses quickly, others have had a substantial challenge filling even a one day course (especially during the flying season). Based partly on the feedback on Joe’s earlier Telegram poll, the current plan is a one-day PG trauma focussed course.

First Aid kits:

If you run into even a relatively minor accident and you don’t carry even a very basic FA kit, you quickly have to tear up your clothes (or worse, wing) to make a bandage to stop bleeding or support a fracture. On the other end, all-encompassing first aid kit bags are wonderful, but also virtually useless during an accident if it sits in somebody's car at the bottom of the hill or even further away when on XC. Hence I am also looking into putting together a few options for a first aid kit to live in your kit bags, from a low-cost very-light-weight (~100gr) basics first aid kit to stop a minor issue turning disastrous (e.g. paracetamol and a bandage to stop bleeding or support a sprained ankle) to the gold standard such as Jocky’s X-light trauma kit). With a suggested ‘bill of materials’ provided, each pilot can then compile their own kit to best fit their circumstances.

Survey:

In order to better judge the interest in FA training and FA ‘readiness’ in general, I’ve set up a small interest form and survey, click on the picture or link below, or click here.  Even if you’re not interested in a course, please take the time to respond!!

https://forms.gle/6NoxEVdCN4UjAk

If you have any questions, suggestions or comments for club First Aid, please contact me via the dedicated forum post or by email  firstaid@dhpc.org.uk

Season Opener

Richard Meek reports

Sunday 14th April. Met Rahul and Chris at 06:45 near Hebden Bridge train station and headed to the highway. Looking for adventure. Whatever came our way. Sorry, drifted off there a bit. We hit the motorway heading south.

Chat with Alex Coltman and Kirsty Cameron over the previous couple of days was a down wind flight from either Malverns or Frocester. Early on Sunday morning they're about 3 hours away so not too unreasonable if you're in a car share. As we were driving word came through from the southerners that Frocester was the place to be. Smooth journey barring one missed turn due to inattention arriving just after 10 AM.

Frocester located south west of Stroud.  Drive from Hebden Bridge isn’t too onerous early on a Sunday morning.

Already a good group are there, as we walked the 50m or so from car to the hill face Alex launched and demonstrated it was eminently soarable.

First UK XC flight of the year for me and I had a bit of equipment faffing to do - nothing like being prepared ! It's a small launch area so I'd laid out my gear to the side so as not to block others. Ready by 10:45 and there's not a lot of room available. Bunched the wing and walked across to an open area; a bit close to the trees and with a small thorn bush around which I carefully laid my lines either side of.

Flying my old Enzo 3 which I’d bought primarily for SIV training coupled with the Niviuk Arrow harness (love it).  This glider has the wing tips trimmed fast.  It was brisk breeze, there's no denying it, but I mis-handled the wing and allowed the right wing tip to flip over. Humph, that's a pain. Double humph, lines all tangled in this thorn bush.

Took over half an hour to clear my lines and fail another couple of launches whilst the majority of pilots climbed out and were off down wind. Finally off at 11:25 under the watchful eye of Matt, thank you much appreciated, and there's only a handful left including Helen Gent on the S&W logo'd Photon.

Spring days are cold.  I find putting the hood up makes a huge difference.

Air low down was reasonably feisty and one had to be on-it when low over the terrain. Initially not much altitude to be had, what lift there was allowed barely one 360 before you were back over the hill. But after 20 mins Helen found a thermal which I and couple of others joined.

Three turns later we were 400' AGL but already committed to leaving. Better not lose it then ! It was only 0.7ms but it was consistent. Tracking 500m to the south and parallel to the runway so causing no interference to the Nympsfield gliding club. Two Artiks were sharing the climb; AdamK on his orange v5 and ChrisJ on the two-line Race version.

Adam on the orange Artik 5 goes on to fly 252km

Chris on the Artik Race

That first climb to 2,500’ took 12 minutes and averaged half up in which time we’d drifted 4 km. I was still on the radio frequency with the top guns and could occasionally hear a helpful commentary of their progress along the lines of “at cloudbase, 5,000’, 40km, down wind looks epic”. Yes, yes it’s all very well for you lot but some of us are still scrabbling around uncomfortably close to the deck !

12:12 looking west towards Stroud from 3,500’.  Frocester launch is just left of this picture

It was clear early on that Adam had his own, plan (turned out he’d set goal at Huntingdon). As he topped out each climb he would track north. My goal was set at Ely and as we progressed down the course line I expected the wind to back around from almost due west at launch to a full south-west. That change in direction would give me the clearance around the airspaces ahead – firstly Brize Norton and then later on Luton and London TMAs. Chris’ goal was at St Neots which was an almost identical direction to Ely so we stuck together. At the fourth transition we parted ways with Adam, for us, pushing north now would likely create more issues down the line than it solved.

Route flown passing well clear of Brize Norton and the TMAs

We passed 5km north of the Brize Norton airspace at 12:55 exceeding four thousand feet for the first time. Now, an hour and fifteen after the initial climb, it feels like we’ve a decent bit of altitude to play with and we’re thirty km into the flight. The climbs were beginning to consolidate and we'd encountered a couple of 2.5m/s. Maintaining good altitude across the Cotswolds we passed to the south of Jeremy Clarkson’s Diddly Squat farm at 56km.

Looking north east across the Cotswolds from 4,000’.  Diddly Squat down there.

Being high opens up your options and touching 5,000’ near Enstone the sky obliged by presenting a cloud street directly on course line. I rode that line for 38km bobbing between 4,400 and cloud base at 5,200’ to Buckingham.

Tally ho ! Cloudstreet ahead.

Half past two, 5,150’ and 99km from launch, objective number one (100+) in the bag. Similar clouds a few km ahead, pushed on feeling confident but didn’t connect with any lift worth talking about under the first cloud. With altitude now 3,800’ I mentally changed down a gear.

14:30 5,150’ looking east towards Milton Keynes

Cloudbase was 5,200’, ground about 400’ giving 4,800’ to play with. Split that into thirds and my 1-2-3 system suggests:

- top section only stop for the best,

- middle something decent,

- bottom section engage survival - anything will do.

Down to 1,200’ AGL before I found anything. At first some zeroes. When I’m low and in survival mode I remind myself of a quote by Mark Watts on the Cloudbase Mayham podcast “just stay in the air for 10 minutes, something will come along”. I’ve found that really useful to settle myself and not desperately push on looking for something – just hang around and wait. That time allows me to take in more of my surroundings, looking for better clues. In this case as the zero-bubble drifted over the Great Ouse river it released and zeroes became one’s and continued to strengthen to 2.5ms which I rode back up to 5,150’.

14:41 low north of Newort Pagnell.  Thin top cover suppressing thermals and the few attractive Cu.

Time for some quick (alright maybe not that quick) mental arithmetic. It’s 3pm, 120km in 3:15 that’s, er, bit less than 40kmh. OK. Sixty km to go, should be possible before 5pm and I’m expecting the day to be working til beyond then. The London TMA 5,500’ section is due east of me, downwind track should see me comfortably clear and I’m on line for goal. All is looking good in Meekiland, let’s crack on.

15:17 3,000’ north of Bedford.  Less top cover directly ahead, need to keep ahead of it.

The next climb I take, north of Bedford, from 2,600’ AGL is a half-up. Bit weak at this altitude I should possibly have pushed on for something stronger until I was below 1,600’ AGL in the desperation zone. Instead I stuck with that weak dribbler for 20 mins and passed by St Neots at 15:40 and 4,400’ AGL.

15:37 4,500’ looking SSW over St Neots

Perhaps subconsciously I had recognised the last climb wasn’t strong enough so when I got the next sub 1ms I took half a dozen turns then hightailed it across to a better looking patch of sunny ground with cloud above. Good move – that was going up nicely at 2ms. I should’ve taken it higher but spied a village bathed in sunshine which was the obvious source for a really tasty looking cloud much better than the straggly things I’d been using of late. I left that solid 2ms in search of better but my timing was poor, what I found was the half-up dregs. I was kicking myself, what-a-muppet.

15:54 tasty looking cloud front and centre

South east of Huntingdon at four o’clock, my half-up has petered out and I have 26km to goal which I can’t quite see  from my vantage point at 3,800’. There are a few straggly clouds ahead but a lot of shade on the ground making not such a promising outlook.

26km to goal

That almost became a death glide. Down to 800’ AGL with 13.5km to Ely the required glide-to-goal is 60:1 and the computer says I need to climb 1,700’ to make it in. My last roll of the dice is to aim for a patch of brown ground in weak sunshine. I find some buoyancy, not even zeroes, I’m going down but only at -0.1ms.

Survival mode engaged "stay in the air, something will come along".

800’ AGL so close but yet so far

Looking ahead as I drift across a road, the next is around 5km and in between are a lot of muddy looking fields. Might be a slog if I land in the middle – good incentive to at least make the next road.

Stick with it.

Concentrate.

Make every turn the most efficient.

Don’t look at the gadget.

‘Use the force Luke, let go’.

The meandering Great Ouse river came to my rescue again, as the bubble released and just-maintaining became 1 then 1.5 up.  This was my ticket to goal and I kept reminding myself to maintain focus and take it all the way to safety. Wind was showing as 32kmh WSW, likely to be lighter on the ground but still a fresh breeze worthy of caution.

Part of my flight preparation is to carefully select a safe landing; I scour online maps to check for obstructions, minimise tracking over built up areas and ensure there’s an easy way to exit my chosen field. (Note. School playing fields may not be good landing options as they’re often surrounded by high fences and locked.)

Leaving the climb at 3,200’ with required glide of 7:1 and 1,700’ above goal to give me a healthy margin to confirm my chosen landing field is good. It is, almost perfect in fact. Best part of 500m long, almost in to wind with just a few low trees along way upwind.

16:28 3,200’ Ely just 7.5km ahead, goal is in the bag

As is often the case, having battled for the a climb to make goal you get there and struggle to get down ! There was a good climb kicking off the edge of the town and I had to search out a patch of sinking air and spiral to get down in good time. As all race pilots know the race isn’t over til your on the retrieve bus, on in this case train.

16:42 perfect landing field

I touched down at 16:43. Relieve the call of nature – my in-flight plumbing was not connected. I’d missed phone calls from Chris and Rahul so knew they were trying to arrange retrieve. Chris had messaged that there was a train at 17:15 so I got a wiggle on. Quick pack and brisk walk to the station arriving just a couple of mins before the train.

On the train I took time to review the retrieve options; #1 to rendezvous in Leicester but the boys would have to wait 1:10 for me, #2 I take the train back to Hebden Bridge arriving 30 mins after the guys but saving us all 1:40. Option 2 it is then.

A jog through Leeds station allowed me catch an earlier connection and shave 25 mins off my ETA, I rolled in to Hebden station 20:05, a couple of mins after Chris and Rahul. Relocate for a beer and curry.

What a splendid end to a splendid day. Roll on Season ’24.

Successful Failure

Richard Meek reports

Tuesday, 23rd April

.

Off to Tinto, Lanarkshire, for a 5-star RASP forecast borne out of the northerly polar airmass.  Flush with confidence from achieving the 187km goal flight on Sunday 21st I set an ambitious 210km goal at Skipton.  Yup, you guessed it, a couple hundred metres from the train station.

No interest from the usual flying crew but Gary Stenhouse drummed up a van load from the north east including Ali Guthrie and Ian Miskin. I left home at 07:00, rendevouz with them outside Carlisle at nine which got us to the hill just after 10.  

Gary’s van full.

My first time at Tinto, I've flown over it from the south before but not launched there.  Very easy walk in, 100m vertical and 1km horizontal at most.  Our van of eight joined by Nick Pain and Steve Gee plus Tim Bridle and half a dozen Scottish pilots.

Sky looks great, Gary was first off and effortlessly climbed to around 3,500'.  Alistair and Tim followed but were unlucky in their timing and had to side land.  I was off at 11:30 and had a nervous first ten minutes soaring a little above and sometimes below take-off altitude.  Decent climb to 2,500' at 11:45 but only one other glider with me - pushed back to the hill to gaggle up.  

Tinto looking NNE.  Ali and Tim had to slope land on the right.

There were lots of bubbles kicking off on the ridge line and some out front, by 12:10 most pilots were established in a good climb and committed to going over the back.  I took this to 4,000' before leading out directly down wind to the SSE.  There were two main navigational considerations; sea breeze at the Solway Firth and Spadeadam danger areas.  Ideally we would track a little east of course line initially to clip the east side of Carlisle which should see us clear of the sea breeze influence.

Task for the day - 211km to Skipton

Off to a good start, the next cloud 5kms downwind was working and pushed on again from base at 4,200'.  5km sinky glide to 2,200', just 600 AGL, before connecting with the next climb.  Bottom of the stack and not in the best of it, I was a little behind Alistair, Tim and Ian.

Uncomfortably low just 10km from launch

Radio discussion of route options, the consensus being; given the relatively low cloud base, bear slightly west of course line to follow the roads rather the optimum line to the east for fear of the boonies.  Caught up with Ali & Ian by 1pm and our trio worked well together bouncing between 3,000' and a rising cloud base up to 5,200' for the next 55km until Gretna.  

13:20 Looking south past Moffat from 4,900’ cloud base

We weren't quite as far east as would have liked but approaching the border down at 2,200' the wind had come around to NEly which seemed to be holding the sea breeze at bay - there were plenty of Cu to our right over the Solway Firth.  Ali and I rode a modest 1- 1.5ms to 3,800' but Ian didn't connect with the core and we got separated by altitude.  East of Gretna we topped up to 4,900' which bolstered our confidence of successfully navigating this tricky area.

 Looking west into the Solway Firth

We remained above 3,500' for the next 15km approaching and passing Carlisle.  It's 15:10, we've covered 95km with 115km to goal - not looking particularly promising but from Sunday's experience we could expect the sky to be working til 7pm.  Not completely out of the question but we would need to pick up the pace somewhat.  

15:27 above Carlisle looking south.  Line of clouds on the right probably convergence influenced.

With the wind being very slightly east of due north we began to put some more east into our track to position ourselves through the Eden valley and into the Dales.  Forecast was for a line of convergence due south from Gretna through Carlisle and on to Penrith with another line of convergence to the east over Appleby and clipping the west side of Warcop danger zone.  The sky looked pretty consistent with that forecast which wasn't ideal as the line south of Penrith would mean more cross winding on the final 70km whilst the Appleby line is 10km away and takes us close to Warcop airspace.

Deciding to risk-it-for-a-swisket  we bore south-east half expecting a decent climb over Centre Parcs.  That didn't materialise and we pushed on til down at 1,000 AGL found a life saver east of Temple Sowerby.  After some searching but eventually found the core which strengthened to 2ms which Ali and I took to 5,000'

Passing over Centre Parcs. Surely the brown fields will generate and the edge of forest trigger ?
But no, Centre Parcs didn’t work.  Down to 1,000’ AGL west of Temple Sowerby

We had 5km under convergence which left us with 74km to goal at 17:20.  Meh, goal probably out of reach at this stage but we'll press on anyway.  A couple of cloud streets could make all the difference.

Cloud base at 5,000’.  Ali ahead.

Gliding on we didn't find anything under the next cloud, the last under this street, but had eyes on a good looking cloud a ways in the distance.  That makes sense; the flats are in shade and the warm air is pushed up then releases from the north side of the Howgills.

On reviewing video footage I can see I had object fixation on that cloud and failed to recognise the ground in front becoming shaded out whilst a minor deviation to the east would have kept us in sunshine.  I also didn't commit to a weak climb found on the ridge of the rising ground.  The tasty cloud had now drifted deep into the Howgills and now sat over Cautley Spout.  Between me and it was a N-S oriented,  shady valley; unlikely to be generating thermals I would need to drift with this 0.3ms dribbler.

North edge of the Howgills with the mesmerising cloud dead ahead.  Unfortunately about 4km up that valley.

It looked a long way down that valley, about 4km, and if I landed in there I'd be adding at least an hour to my retrieve.  I chickened out and pushed back towards Ali who was a little lower and just connected with the hills.

 

We soared the hills waiting patiently for some sunshine to come along but threw in the towel after half an hour at 18:04.  The decision may have partly influenced by a message which flashed up on my screen from Robin starting "I'm in the van .....".  We were low and I couldn't take my hands off the controls to read the message fully but I assumed/ hoped Rob was offering a lift as he lives not far away.

Sure enough, as we glide in to land I get a call from Rob as he pulls up at the entrance to my landing field.  What an absolute legend !  

18:06 Landing next to A685 midway between Tebay and Kirkby Stephen.  

Rob came across to chat and we were joined by the farmers wife checking (a) I was OK and (b) we weren't trying to steal her sheep or ponies !

Meanwhile, Gary had landed, worked out the optimised retrieve which included relocating my car from Carlisle to Penrith train station.  R-e-e-esult 🙂  Rob drove us there, bought him a pint by way of thank you and then just 1:45 drive home.

Ended up with a 6:09/ 160.9km flight through some magnificent countryside.  Great flight but that goal was just bit too ambitious.  https://www.xcleague.com/xc/flights/20241170.html...
