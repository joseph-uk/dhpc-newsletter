Skywords - July 2025

Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

Two months ago, I twisted my ankle walking down Dodd Fell.  I then did much the same at Stags Fell a month later.  I really must revert to wearing boots.  After 2 months of following the optimistic advice to ‘keep moving’, I finally resorted to hobbling off to visit the GP.  He diagnosed a possible hairline fracture.  Apparently, it’s defined as ‘chronic’ if you’ve had it for more than 6 weeks.  I’ve been restricted to light duties for a while, which means no paragliding.  The bad news for you is that you get another dose of me reminiscing about the Army Pilots’ Course…

Survival Week

I’d survived the fixed wing phase of the course but before getting to grips with helicopters we faced the delights of survival week – actually pretty chilled because there was little chance of being chopped from the course.

Flying Soldiers Episode 2 (Youtube) Not my course, but it gives you an idea.

As groundcrew I’d already done the Underwater Escape Training or ‘Dunker’ before, as described in Skywords Feb 24, so this didn’t hold any fears for me.  The only difference was that we got to sit in the front of the mocked-up aircraft with a personal life raft strapped to our backsides.  The extra baggage made it more difficult to get through the doorway, especially upside down in the dark.

Underwater Escape Training - The ‘Dunker’.

We also experienced the delights of the decompression chamber where the pressure was reduced to simulate flying at altitude.  When taken off oxygen it didn’t take long before even the simplest of tasks became impossible.  Watching how quickly other people become helpless is perhaps the most useful reminder that you don’t recognise that your decision-making skills have been corrupted.  Hypoxia creeps up on you without warning and the effects start at about 8,000’.

The final exercise of the week took place in the English Channel, and followed on from the skills acquired in the Dunker.  Kitted out in a lifejacket and individual life raft, we had to somersault into the sea off a fast-moving boat (to simulate ditching), inflate the life raft and protect ourselves from the elements.  The photo shows me posing for our Corps magazine.  Apologies for the poor quality: despite the smooth conditions my camera let some water in.

Somewhere in the English Channel in 1992.  Individual life rafts.

I actually found the single life raft quite comfortable and, as I could see the horizon at all times, I didn’t succumb to sea sickness.  On another occasion I was subjected to a 12-man life raft.  Closing the door to protect yourselves from the elements deprives you of that all important view of the horizon, and fresh air.  Once the first person throws up it’s inevitable that the rest will follow.  Somebody once told me that in most survival situations (arctic, jungle, desert) the human spirit will strive to survive.  Sea sickness is different: ‘just let me die now!’

Not relevant to survival week, but space allows me to explain that throughout the course we attended ground school – lectures on tech (how the aircraft worked), navigation (dead reckoning – no GPS in those days), meteorology, principles of flight, etc.  I remember one particular instructor, a retired Lieutenant Colonel, particularly well.  With only a little persuasion he could quickly be distracted from the topic at hand, and would tell us fascinating tales from his flying career.  To this day I can’t recall what he was supposed to be teaching us, but I do remember some of the stories – I’ll tell you over a beer sometime.

The Lancashire to Lothian Line

Richard Meek reports.

Saturday, the 10th of May. We'd already had a remarkable run of good days, and personally, I'd flown five days in May. The forecast promised a broadly southerly airflow. I live beneath the Manchester–Leeds airspace, an invisible west–east barrier blocking most of the width of the country. A route starting north of the airspace and heading for Scotland made sense. With a few launch sites to choose from, there was a difference of opinion within the group I usually fly with; many factors play into this: proximity, previous experience, difficulty of walk-up, and not least the route aesthetics.

I chose to launch from Parlick Fell near Chipping, Lancashire—an obvious starting point given the wind direction—and began plotting a route downwind. The stars, or more accurately the wind and the weekend (Spadeadam restricted area inactive), aligned to present an opportunity to reach Dunbar, on the southern shores of the Firth of Forth, east of Edinburgh: 230 km through varied terrain of northern England and southern Scotland.

I was late to bed the night before, having returned from a Yorkshire Dales–to–Scotland flight at midnight. Still, knowing the day could start early, I was walking up the hill at 9:30 with wings already in the sky.

It was a busy day with around thirty pilots, including a raiding party of Scottish pilots who had made the long journey south, lured by the potential for a big flight. We're rivals in the UK XC League, but the friendships are stronger—sharing flight plans, revelling in each other's successes, and flying as a team whenever possible. Wind on the hill was initially more easterly than expected, but as we prepped, it swung around. I launched at 11:05 a.m. straight into a decent thermal, climbing swiftly to over 3,000 ft.

Unfortunately, no one else caught that cycle, so I hung back to gaggle up. By 11:40, a good group was airborne, and we left the hill together.

11:36 leaving the hill.  Photo credit: John Oliver

The drift was SSE, and in the early stages we edged east to avoid Morecambe Bay and the sea air, which regularly sweeps in, killing thermal activity. Ironically, while our group worked hard to push crosswind, other pilots opted for a pure downwind route, flying within 10 km of the coast with no issue at all—while we battled lower down to stay clear.

We had a strong gaggle including Richard Carters 1 & 2, Julian Robinson, Ali Guthrie, Rob Cruickshank, John Oliver and Phil Wallbank —a who's-who of experienced UK pilots and locals who know the terrain well. The first climb out was excellent, averaging 2 m/s and topping out around 4,600 ft. About ten of us in the gaggle climbed together, while another 20 or so were still searching for the climb out.

Flying with good friends creates a subtle rivalry—kudos for whoever pushes out front. That’s how it was. The leading group worked in harmony, spreading just enough to offer an effective search pattern under a sky littered with clouds.

The Three Richards (you know what I wanted to say).  RC2 blue Photon bottom right, RC1 lime Photon top middle, yours truly lime Zeno 2 left.  Photo credit: John Oliver.

We had the luxury of choice in those early stages, taking a 45-degree crosswind line to stay away from the coast. Twenty kilometres in, leaving the Forest of Bowland behind, the cloudbase lifted above 6,000 ft as we cruised over the low ground west of Ingleton, approaching Kirkby Lonsdale before the hour was out.

From there, we reduced our crosswind deviation and followed a more direct line.  Our gaggle had thinned out but Julian, who’d climbed out twenty minutes after us and taken a more direct line past Milnthorpe and Morecambe Bay, overtook and then joined us.   Our gaggle had stayed high throughout, rarely dropping below 3,500 ft.

12:52 Howgills Ahead. Photo credit: John Oliver.

As we entered the Eden Valley, loosely tracking the M6 northbound, we found a strong convergence line which carried us for 20 km at good speed. Just before Penrith, we fell off the end of the convergence, and it was beginning to look a little desperate down at 1,000 ft AGL—but we clawed back a climb on the town's northeastern edge.

Climbing out from a low point north of Penrith.  Photo credit: John Oliver

From there, we rode a solid 2.5 m/s climb back to 6,000 ft. Our next challenge was navigating past Carlisle and the Solway Firth—where the sea breeze often barrels in from the west and kills the lift. We threaded a line about 5 km east of Carlisle, taking another climb to 7,000 ft as we passed the 100 km mark. Spirits were high—we thought we’d cleared the danger zone.

14:30 Solway Firth to the west.  Photo credit: John Oliver

The rhythm changed as we crossed the border into Scotland. The booming climbs gave way to scrappy, weak lift, and we were now scratching across sparsely populated ground. The gaggle fractured; four together, I was in a pair with Ali, with John isolated.

Low again over the Kielder Forest.  Photo credit: John Oliver.

The lead gaggle was about 4 km ahead, southeast of Hawick. Ali and I saw them climbing slowly in what looked like a thermal triggered by conical hill convergence. We pushed on and rejoined the group.

At 170 km, we looked ahead and saw the sky—finally—transform. A convergence line stretched due north to our left. A quick radio debate followed. The climb we were in was poor—less than 0.5 m/s—but we decided to stick with it just long enough to get within glide of the convergence highway.

That decision paid off. A couple of kilometres southwest of Kelso, we connected with the convergence, which delivered a steady 2.5 m/s all the way to 6,000 ft. We were only 45 km from goal now, and for the first time, the goal seemed possible.

Limping towards the convergence line which is like a highway to goal.  Photo credit: Richard Meek

Riding the convergence was a massive contrast to the limping of the previous 30 minutes. A glide and climb to 7,000 ft put us on the edge of final glide. Julian led into goal from 15 km out, entering the goal cylinder with 700 ft to spare. I took it more cautiously, arriving overhead with 3,000 ft in hand. With a stiff southerly wind on the ground and turbulence around our goal field, we each chose a landing option on the outskirts of town.

17:50 7,000’ the coast and goal are within glide.  Photo credit: Richard Meek.

On the ground by 18:40, it was a quick pack and jog to the station to catch the 19:29 heading home. We even had a couple of minutes to pick up a pulled pork baguette from a pub by the station—delicious after almost seven hours in the air.

Looking south from above Dunbar, the convergence line still very evident.  Photo credit: Richard Meek

The train schedule would have me home by midnight—but without the car. With a good forecast for Sunday, the retrieve journey was spent planning the next day and arranging transport to launch.

No rest for the wicked, nor for the committed UK cross-country pilot.

A Summer Pantomime or…

The Tale in Which Peter goes to the Ball, and a Shoe is Lost

Pete Logan reports

From the title you can probably guess that this is going to be an odd one of a write up. It concerns the North South Cup held in mid-June down south. There are a bunch of bits that turn out to be straight out of Cinderella, at least if you squint a little. As Eric Morecambe once said at the piano; “I'm playing all the right notes—but not necessarily in the right order!” If you don’t want to come on this magical journey with me, and I won’t blame you, the short of it is that the North Team won again!

A long, long time ago (two weeks) in a land far far away (the south) I was forced to work away from home. Perhaps not quite as bad as Cinder’s drudgery since I was at a technology conference in Paris. The NS Cup (the Ball) had been delayed by a week due to poor weather the week before and it was confirmed as on whilst I was away. If I return all the way home then I miss most of the weekend, but how to get this to come together? As a good omen of the luck to come I randomly meet two people at the conference I would not have expected to. The guy that hired me for my current company, but who I had never met as he left before I joined and then Harry Bloxham in the waffle queue, he’s ex British champ and current distance record holder. Nice chats with both.

After some hasty WhatsApping, my wife has packed what she thinks is most of my gear and Chris Fountain takes on the role of a Cinderella footman to pick up my gear and bring it down to Milk Hill in his car (first pumpkin). The day of the ball begins for me at 5am in Paris. I can get to take off after a hasty bit of travel and expenses rearrangement. The Eurostar leaves me in St Pancras at 9am and I tube across London to Paddington and take the first cheap train out west after the rush hour. By 10.30 I’m at Swindon and an Uber is thankfully not going to cost the earth to get me to take off. By 11:30 I’m walking up a lane at the foot of Milk Hill White Horse. The wrong lane as it turns out, but eventually I’m on the right track. Ruth & Chris have done a great job and my gear is ready to lug up the hill from Chris’ car.

As I’m climbing, the first gaggles are easing away in threes and fours. It looks light and tricky. Commit low over the back and hang on kind of thing. A couple of the fifty or so contenders don’t like it and push back to the hill for a sweaty walk up nearby. I dump my gear down at the top (Milk Hill is like Baildon on steroids) and chat with the locals. They’re saying it’s all likely shut down now and maybe will get flyable again at 4ish. I unpack and clip in, there’re wafts coming through and a few NS pilots still left. I’m here now so I should make the best of it. Apparently the goal is Newark on Trent. Fat chance.

One of those wafts comes through and I’m second up into it. We work bubbles of lift on different ends of the hill, sometimes up, sometimes struggling. The pilots joining below help me to work out where is best. Five of us climb out and it’s immediately obvious that we should settle in for the mince. Climbs are slow and multi part, but I’m joined by a group that’s working well and doing their fair share of searching and marking. Scenery floats past and the landscape is as magical as any fairy tale; Alton Barnes White Horse, Silbury Hill, Avebury Stone Circle, Hackpen White Horse, Liddington Hillfort.

Further north and the proper Bronze Age Uffington White Howse eases into view but this is a distraction from the fact we haven’t been staying terribly high. I lose my last two companions just before the M4 and I manage to climb out with a one two thermal punch of Wroughton village and a solar farm.

Uffington White Horse.

 

The magic landscape gives way to Swindon. It’s booming over the town so I turn to navigation. I have to head for Oxford and that means getting around the massive Brize Norton CTR. That’s my undoing really. I make a turn east and start the long run to get a decent clearance. After Swindon I never really get high again and land after about 40km in all. Still very pleasant for being the last gaggle off the hill and making it so far as conditions blued out.

A quick pack and I’m off down a quiet country road under a blazing sun. Still, plenty of time to get back in time for tea at the campsite. I’m deciding that I’m not having much luck with a lift when Colonel John plus daughter out for an afternoon ride pull in just ahead of me in a spotless Jeep. “Hop in” he says, followed by “where you going?”. He’s happy to get me to Swindon and after a bit of good conversation which included sympathising with his daughter about having to maintain a set of imperial tools when she inherits the Jeep, he soon suggests taking me back to maybe Marlborough – he doesn’t want me hanging around for buses at the rough end of Swindon :-D. He drops his daughter at home and we swap to more practical transport. Obviously my second and third pumpkin coaches of the weekend! After a bit more chat, he soon suggests dropping me back at Milk Hill. It doesn’t get better than that!

 

Col John and his daughter in the day’s second pumpkin a 1944 Willy’s MB Jeep left over from France.

People start dribbling in for the evening and some catching up was done. Saturday was due to be a wash out so I felt safe being offered some Czech rocket juice from the Polish contingent. They were southern pilots in the comp so this could have been an attempted sabotage or maybe it was a “Drink Me” potion making an appearance from another fantastical tale. I suffered a bit on Saturday but we were only doing the raffle, prizegiving for all the XC winners of ’24 plus relocating north to be better placed for the weather.

Sunday dawned to low cloud passing by at a rate of knots. We were headed for the Malverns and the Worcester Beacon TO. It’s a spine backed ridge running north south with geology way more ancient and just as magic as the Neolithic stone circles of Friday. It marks the eastern edge of what I can best describe as a cut and shut landscape and is made of Precambrian (Uriconian) rhyolite rocks which filter excellent ground water. These poke straight up into the flatter geography of the surrounding area making it look like the Malverns and similar formations at the Mynd and Wrekin have been bodge welded in from other landscapes.

Enough of the geology, it’s time to be above it. Meeky sets a massive task for Ipswich which no one flinches at. His nifty online tool for task setting has been proving reliable since Brazil last year. Technically the North has won on tasks with distance flown one day the previous week and the top ten pilots from Friday giving us two task wins. We still have a task today though to see if the southern pilots can stage a comeback.

I prep and want to take off early as the wind feels strong and isn’t going to ease. A cobra launch gets reset by a bit of turbulence and I recover with a standard reverse launch. I have to correct by moving sideways and forward and… arse! I’ve got Gary Stenhouse’s lines wrapped around my feet. I’m in the air and beginning to lift his packed glider and harness off the ground. I shake my left foot and it comes free but the right is too entwinned. I shake the trainer off entirely and it drops away with the lines (there goes Cinder’s slipper). I’m free and immediately make the decision that I’m not top landing. It’s way too tight and lifty, plus there will be wings popping off every few seconds.

The lift is good and there’s plenty of penetration so I concentrate on the flying and decide to worry about my footwear later. I do have a concern that my foot will get cold in just a sock but I can probably magic up a solution from stuff I have in my cockpit if it gets desperate.

Ten minutes later I leave the hill at base with the first gaggle. We’re treated to a view of the Great Malvern Show as we enter the flats heading east. I take it steady as the day is early and we begin to separate as the difference in glider speed comes into play. I’m on a Cayenne 5 from 2017 but trying to stay with the latest crop of two liners. It is really nice in the climb though now I have it teamed up with my Arrow harness. They’re a great combination.

I’m trying to find a decent climb around Banbury when I’m joined by the main gaggle; Chris Fountain, Richard Meek, RCs 1 & 2, Jacob Aubrey. It’s heavy with northern pilots. We should do well today. We’re able to progress quickly and the next feature of note is Silverstone. There’s some kind of race on, but we have our own to concentrate on.

Silverstone Race Track

We’re all fairly high after Silverstone but the group of tenish gliders just seems to be committee flying a bit too much for my taste. I decide to lead out SE to a nice set of clouds towards Milton Keynes avoiding a blue hole dead ahead. Checking back after a few minutes and I see no one behind me. Oh dear chuff, what have I done? I’ve thrown away being with a decent gaggle for the flight. They’ve taken the north way around the hole and there’s just me and a Alex Coltman (305km to Driffield! last week) left on this track. He gets a nice climb and crosses north across the hole to the gaggle. I’m left at Newport Pagnell at about mid height and under a decaying cloud set with shaded ground.  

There’s no option (because I missed the upcoming Cranfield ATZ everyone else was setting themselves up to avoid) but to cross into the hole with the ground in sunlight and see what I can find. Now I’m low but at least approaching something interesting. A rise with dry fields and trees studded about it. On the far side is a small row of wind turbines which will be mixing the air nicely. There’s something there wanting to lift but I’m 200m off landing. It has to be now or never. A hunt upwind does feel like it’s in that direction so I turn down wind hoping. It seems to take an age but a field later I make contact. I centre in a few turns and then it lets rip! I’m getting lofted at up to 6m/s. It’s not a decent flight unless there’s been a bit of drama.

A speedy climb like that gives you options and I set off on bar after the gaggle. I meet them at Bedford, but not before I flew briefly with Chris. He set off further north under a line of dark cloud which I didn’t think looked so good. Unfortunately it didn’t work out so well for him and he was on the ground for about 130km as I met up with the gaggle roughly an hour after I’d gone off on my hair brained scheme. We crack on to Cambridge and start planning if we need to avoid airspace. It seems to get a bit tricky in this section but I don’t mind a bit of hanging around, our ground speed even when we’re circling is 29km/h.

Meeky gives us a wave over Trumpington as we pass Cambridge.

I know Cambridge well, as my kids are there and I take time to savour it. Diverting to land on Jesus Green in the center of town would be nice and dodgy) but they’re probably busy and we still have a task ahead of us. There’s even more of us now as 170km from TO passes and another gaggle joins (Westie and Bud). We’re at least 11 gliders within a kilometre or so of each other. That can’t happen too often in the UK, surely? We part ways with both Richard Carters, they’re on a task to his sister’s house at about 200km. We have what looks like a deadish area ahead to pass before we reach Ipswich. We’re giddy but there’s technical flying still needed. To add to the difficulty, Jacob Aubrey’s lost power to his vario and has no backup so we’re talking him to the lift and what glide ratio he has left to go.

Ipswich. YESSSSS!!!!!!

We mince, search, separate and eak out lifty lines watching the glide to goal on XCTrack go down from 15 to 10 to 9 to 8. Not wanting to be sure of making it until it says I arrive with 1500ft above goal. I hear a couple of calls ahead on the radio as each person crosses into a tiny imaginary cylinder 240km away from where we took off. Unbelievable. Then it’s my turn to tag goal and a double PB for the UK. Very much the fairy tale ending. 242km, 295 with the declare. 6h 30 mins averaging 38km/h. And to cap that off it turns out that was the best flight of the day on XContest too!

XContest Tracklog. Wow.

Joe tells me I bagged XContest’s biggest flight worldwide that day. I’ll take that!

I land next to Richard Meek with Stephen Ashley and Guy Anderson already down. Westie, Jacob and Bud join us next. The sock hasn’t been an issue at all except I can feel the time on bar I’ve put in just on my left foot. We pack and sprint for the train and it’s noticeable how many people don’t seem to clock that I’m walking around with only one trainer. I decide to grab a bed at my lad’s halls and see if a pair of his trainers fit me so wait for the Cambridge train. I say goodbye to the other six who are now stuffed into a taxi van which will take them back to Malvern.

My trainer took a slightly more convoluted journey. I’m not sure about being Prince Charming ;-D but Gary Stenhouse was certainly a gentleman. He sorted his glider out and then took off after me with the trainer in his harness. I had a couple of IMs during the flight that I couldn’t reply to until I landed. Gary had gotten as far as Cambridge and I just missed texting him to say hide my shoe under a bush and send me the pin. The next weekend I had it unceremoniously thrown at me during briefing for the Lakes Charity Classic. It fitted but thankfully Gary’s already married so we end happily ever after.

A new fashion is born outside Ipswich station. Due to a wardrobe error, that sock’s been on for four days 😐

Oh and the Fairy Godmother? Probably Viv Fouracre, she puts in so much effort to make sure the weekends run smoothly. Thanks Viv, Ruth, Chris Jocky, Meeky, Jim Mallinson, Geordie, Col John, Dan and everyone that made it a fairy tale weekend.

The future of the NS Cup is always open and discussed about. It will still remain a comp where the flying takes precedence – i.e. travel to wherever we need to be and make do. We’ll be making concerted efforts to bring in new blood, those younger pilots who could do with some exposure to experience and who would benefit.

Lemming Effect and Competitions

Joseph Edmonds reports

I’ve just come back from doing 3 separate competitions in Europe that were conveniently located pretty close together in time and space, making it worthwhile to do the multi day drive there and back in the motorhome.

Unfortunately the first two comps were pretty much a washout, with very little flying to be had. The third competition was flyable every day, with challenging tasks that for me, took hours to complete and make it to goal.

Also, the third comp had a level of accident and mishap that I don’t recall ever seeing before, and by the end of it I was pretty unsettled by to say the least, to the point that I actually threw in the towel on the final task and just flew directly to goal rather than pursue a course line which, in my mind, had crossed over into something pretty dangerous. As I was halfway to goal, a “level 2” was called on the radio - due to the risk of mid air collisions on the course line. I had made the right call for sure.

If you are not sure what a level 2 is - it’s pretty simple. Competition tasks that are underway are always at one of three levels, conveniently named level 1, 2 and 3. Level 1 is “all good” and level 3 is “land immediately, task is over as it is unsafe”. Level 2 is the bit in between - it's not safe, but not so unsafe as to warrant cancelling the task.

Normally these levels are a proxy for the weather conditions. Wind getting a bit strong, clouds getting a bit tall, some isolated showers - that would be level 2. I’ve never before heard a level 2 called due to the other pilots in the competition being a hazard to each other, but I can definitely agree that it was the correct call.

Many pilots, on hearing a level 2 in a comp task, should probably think about landing. If you’re not a serious competition pilot then you have to wonder why take the risk to continue flying when you are only one level away from it being declared unsafe to fly.

The reality though is that no one responded to the level 2 call. Most pilots kept flying and many made it into the goal. What this tells me is that either I’m a coward, or other people in competition settings have a much higher risk threshold than me.

I found it pretty surprising how blase people were about their own collapses, some of which were serious, mid airs, tree landings, helicopter rides. I’m not sure if competition pilots are super brave, in denial or fully understand the risks they are taking. I think some paraglider pilots in general operate in a kind of denial/ignorance of the risks they are taking.

One real nexus of risk is the start gaggle. On some particularly tricky days, there was really only one place you could climb to base in the start cylinder, so this meant that almost the entire competition was in a “super gaggle”. Flying in a huge gaggle like this is pretty much all about trust. You simply can’t see and avoid everyone so you have to trust that they are going to see and avoid you, and that they are not going to turn into, or have a collapse or midair above you.

Collapses in massive start gaggles are increased likelihood due to the fact that everyone is pretty much forced to fly continuously in and out of the smooth core of the thermal, constantly transitioning through the turbulent outer layer of the thermal.

If you are someone, like me, who has an ongoing risk/reward evaluation going on in your subconscious pretty much continually, then you might decide - as I usually did - that being in the start super gaggle was just not worth it. I was able to mill about on the outskirts and then quickly climb up once the main armada had got underway.

One day, I was able to get up to base in only a few turns and then get underway on bar and quite quickly catch up with the mid pack of the armada. On another day I was lucky enough to find a good climb away on my own just before start and could glide alongside the main armada at full height.

However - if you want to place well in the competition then you are obliged to be as close to the top of the start gaggle as possible. That means that you are mixing it up with the people who inevitably end up cloud flying at the top of the gaggle. So not only are you milling around with a huge number of pilots - but now you have the added bonus that visibility is reduced, perhaps completely.

The general wisdom is that competitions are an excellent way to progress as a pilot, and I think this is really true. You learn so much by flying a predefined course line. There are usually safe but challenging “crux” sections which will see many pilots on the ground. Normally the crux is a tricky bit with a safe landing nearby. There may be multiple crux points in a task. Generally these are placed some distance around the course so people aren’t just decked very early in the task.

Task setting in general is hugely complex and requires a deep understanding of the local area, the day's meteo forecast and micro meteorological conditions. You also need to understand the cryptic and arcane scoring rules to try to get a “1000 point task” which is when everything lines up with predefined parameters so that the task is regarded as fully valid.

It’s like a heavily formalised goldilocks zone - the task needs to be “just right” to be able to score 1000 points. It is hugely complex and I suspect that this focus on getting the 1000 points can sometimes be at the detriment of safety, encouraging meet directors and task planners to push things more than might be sensible in order to achieve the required distance etc.

So what do I think about competitions now? Well I had one of the best flights of my life. But in that same flight, I flew over one person in a tree and immediately then watched someone else spin their glider into the ground and is now hospitalised and facing at least 3 months in bed. Talk about cognitive dissonance…! 🤔(this is amazing && this is horrendous)🤯

The pilot who was hospitalised had a side swing into baked hard ground. His koroyd protector that covered only his back was totally undamaged. His helmet was ruined but definitely saved his skull. His hips, back, ribs and shoulder are mashed though.

I fly a harness with heavy protection including side impact. I like to think that I would have fared a little bit better should I have suffered the same mishap, but I am not sure by how much, and I don’t want to find out!

One big advantage of flying in a comp, and especially this one, was that the post incident response was truly excellent. Tracking is now very good, with a mesh network FLARM system meaning that even in mobile dark spots people can still be tracked most of the time. Helicopter response time is very rapid in Italy. Skilled mountain rescue people are on site and on standby. The safety net is way above and beyond what you could expect free flying or even on a more simple guided holiday.

Does this extra safety just lull people into a risk homeostasis mindset though? I think maybe it does.

If you want to truly benefit from the extra safety then the homeostasis needs to be resisted. With that “fly as safe as you normally would” mindset then I think comps can be a much safer choice than free flying. But when the start goes and the armada sets off it’s pretty much a default reaction to push the bar and follow the armada into whatever may lie ahead…

My Second Large Asymmetric Collapse

Paul Rowntree reports

This was different to my first a month ago and I thought worth sharing.  For context, I have c80hrs experience over c20yrs, I’m Pilot rated and fly an EN-A rated wing with an open harness.  I’m sharing in case one reader can take something away from my experience that may aid them.

It was on the weekend of the X-Lakes/LCC on Blease Fell.  On arrival at the lower take off just after 9am, the wind was southerly and seemed smooth.  The forecast was for it to strengthen becoming too strong by 11am.  There were few clouds in the sky, other than wispy clouds signifying the sea breeze was about to pass over from the west.  When I was there a month ago, it passed over c1pm.  By the time I was ready to take off, the wind had dropped and turned to SW, the sea breeze had passed.  I walked along the hill to take off from an angle facing more into the wind.

It was choppy, with some sharp thermals in the cooler air that I couldn’t do a lot with staying broadly around take off height.  On a beat heading more to the east, the collapse occurred.  I immediately leant to the inflated side with some break on that side and maintained my course of direction, then waited for the wing to re-inflate.  Flying an EN-A wing, and from my experience a month ago, I expected the re-inflation to be fairly instant.  Checking my track log, it took 4 seconds to re-inflate and I lost 28m, therefore I was descending at 7m/s.  With this descent rate, from a Google search I estimate I had a c60% collapse.

I had my eyes fixated on the approaching land below me, I suppose to some extent I froze.  I didn’t look up at the wing.  I didn’t check the direction I was flying – in staring at the ground, I somehow got the impression I was flying away from the hill, but I was actually flying diagonally towards it.  The track log showed it re-inflated when I was 13m above ground.  I stupidly didn’t consider throwing my reserve in this time just working out how to deal with a fast landing.

After the event, I was more than happy to call time on the flight, I wasn’t high enough to chance the rough air again.  Flying east to the landing field, it was very buoyant smooth air, I was back in the convergence.  Therefore the collapse may not have been as a result of a strong thermal, but whatever the air was doing on the dividing line of the two air masses.

Should I receive a large collapse again, once I have achieved a straight course, I will next check my surroundings and the direction of this course, flying towards a hill can quickly reduce a safety height margin you may initially have had.  I will then look up at the wing.  I may gain some information on how long it may take to re-inflate, if I need to do anything, or if it is looking unlikely it is going to re-inflate, get the reserve out.

There is no doubt that I had a lucky escape.  My other takeaways are that if I could be near the boundary of the sea breeze, make sure I am high or take a breather on the ground.  Even though I’m flying an EN-A wing, there is no guarantee it will snap back into shape quickly.  In turbulent or potentially turbulent air, keep as big a margin as possible from the ground.

From the Archive

Martin Baxter reports

In January 1994 Iain Fairbrother died following an accident at Wether Fell.  The keel on his hang glider snapped.  He pulled his reserve but tragically the handle came away in his hand.  He survived the fall but died in the ambulance on the way to hospital with Bob Delahay.  At the funeral Bob promised Iain’s father that he would erect a solid teak cross at Wether Fell in memory of Iain.

Unbeknown to us his relatives continued to visit this memorial on an annual basis.  It came as quite a shock when, in 2009, they made contact with us to report that it had ‘gone missing’.  After much research (thanks to Pete and Sara Spillett) we established that it hadn’t been stolen or vandalised; it had just rotted through and fallen over.

The committee discussed the options and agreed that monuments to fallen members were not appropriate at flying sites: not only do they present an additional obstacle, but they’re not exactly the best advert for our sport.  We were greatly relieved when Iain’s family took this decision in good spirit.

In April this year, easy soaring conditions allowed me to scour the ground and get a fix.  Last week I returned on foot and managed to find it - still in one piece and lying peacefully on the ground.  I sent a copies of the photo both to Bob and Iain’s family.  

Iain’s name lives on in the club trophy awarded every year to the Pilots’ Pilot.

The following article by Tony Gardner was published in Skywings in April 1994.

On 16 January a tragic accident on Wether Fell resulted in the death of Iain Fairbrother. It is so hard to adequately convey someone's character, or what made them really tick. Those of you reading this who knew Iain will remember vividly. For those who have not spent much time in his company, these few words may help. Firstly, he was a pilot, and so anyone who flies will recognise elements of themselves in him. Iain learned to fly gliders about four years ago, being taught by Ian Currer and Keith Cockroft. He rapidly became very enthusiastic and took to the sport like the proverbial duck to water.

He was fortunate that his work as a research scientist allowed him to live in Harrogate, within spitting distance of the Dales. As time marched on, Iain's abilities and self-confidence steadily improved. He broke the occasional upright and took the resulting ribbing in good spirit. He also took an increasing part in club life, thoroughly enjoying his weekends in the Dales with us, and suffering the same withdrawal symptoms we all know when the weather kept him on the ground. He was a true enthusiast who contributed greatly to the Club, working on the Committee and editing the newsletter for the last couple of years. After a while, the luckier ones amongst us discovered another of Iain's talents; he was a curry-cook skilled enough to rival the best that Bradford can offer. Away from the kitchen, his piloting skills were really beginning to blossom, and last year he started to fly cross-country and to think about competition. He was awarded the Club's Beaumont trophy for the best first XC in 1993. If only it did not have to be a posthumous award. Iain loved his flying and there is no doubt that he would want all his friends to carry on enjoying the Dales just as he did. We shall, but for many of us it will never be quite the same because we will never forget him.

At a moving service at Harrogate Crematorium, many of Iain's friends gathered to wish him a final farewell. His parents particularly asked that their thanks be passed on to all the Dales Club members for their support.
