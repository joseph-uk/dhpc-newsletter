Chairman's Chat

Tam (AKA Glorious Leader) reports

I am nearing the end of a 3 week+ holiday in Spain. We left the UK on June 19th, which I believe was roughly when the weather changed after weeks of incessant wind and rain. Having seen some reports of great flights, (well done all!) I am hoping that the better weather continues when we return next week. You can blame me if it reverts to the wind and rain of early June as we land. It was particularly good to see Sonny back on the horse after his accident in Oz earlier in the year, with a new pb of 166km, fantastic!

Just before my hols started we had  the incident in Littondale with the Metcalfs complaining about people landing in the valley bottom. Our thanks are due to both Ed and Stephen Craven for calming the waters. Hopefully we will have a better relationship with the Metcalfs going forward. Littondale is an important site for us, and a beautiful place to fly. Let's all simply treat the residents with the respect that everyone deserves - all it takes is a few ill-considered words and the whole club and all pilots are targets for the wrong sort of attention.  

The site guide for Windbank was updated after the incident. It is worth a read if you haven't seen it. As is the updated Dodd guide now that the TDA is active. It's possible that the Wether Fell guide will change soon. Please keep up to date with all these changes - whilst any changes will be notified on SM, these are soon lost as new information is added. The website will always be the repository for club information.

Enjoy the weather everyone, and fly safe. See you on a hill soon.

Tam

Worcester Beacon to somewhere quite a long way away!

Chris Fountain reports

June 29th.  Big trip planned for a possible big Monday....and it was.

It started with a race to get booked in at the travel lodge in Worcester.  Not many rooms left and every time someone else booked a room the price went up for the next person.  In my haste I booked a room for 13th July,  but a very grumpy receptionist managed to change it for me telling me it was the last available room left and I was very lucky.  Funny thing was I now know of 2 other people that booked the exact same incorrect dates as I did.  Martin didn't realise until he arrived at 9pm that night but somehow they still had a room for him.  Always check the default date before you click 'book'

Anyway,  early to bed and early to rise.  Breakfast at the travel lodge then out into the carpark ready to drive the 20mins to the hill.

No sun.. cloud everywhere,  not looking good.  This was a bit worrying.

Our group was first on the hill at around 10.15.  Again a bit worrying.  No locals.  Anyway we all got ready and made our plans for the day and slowly people began to arrive.

There were a few goal plans floating around but we decided on Diss for 237kms.  The QR codes were shared and we started to get clipped in.

It was starting to get a bit busier now so I decided to get in the air early to get a bit of height and stay away from the building crowds below.  

Worcester beacon is an incredibly thermic hill when it gets going but never seem very good for soaring and very soon there were large numbers of pilots all getting in each other way trying to find the first climbs of the day.  A few landed, a few went down but most of them just seemed intent on getting in my way as much as possible.  Full concentration needed.

Weak thermals started coming up the hill after about 30mins or so.  Plenty of pilots now climbing but the climbs were only rising to around 2000ft and with the wind being quite fresh lots of people ended up low over the back trying to decide to stay or go or whether they could even get back to the at all!.  I climbed 2 or 3 times quite well but never felt it was quite the right time to leave or good enough to cut the cord and take the plunge into the flats.

Finally, after returning back to the front of the hill for the third time I flew into a far more positive climb with very few other pilots in it. Perfect. Richard Meek came to join me and we both climbed out in a great 3m climb up to base at I think around 3500ft.  

So off we all went downwind.  The sky was still very cloudy with very little sun on the ground but it did appear to be working quite well.

The key was to stick together and take it slowly to start.  There were loads of pilots around as markers so that helped with the searching.

Now I must be honest here... I find flying in the flatlands a bit none descript really.  Far less interesting things to look at than in our lovely hills in the north.

The next few hours went by flying with lots of pilots I didn't really know over lots of fields I also didn't really know.  It was fairly steady away. The clouds were there just not very defined but they all seemed to be working ok.

Silverstone race track was the first landmark I recognised,  then the HS2 scar across the country,  but that's really about all I can remember.  Just fields everywhere. (just remembered there was a small lake too. Grafham Water)

I passed 100km at 15.30.  Slow going.  We needed to pick up the pace if we were going to reach our goal of 237k.  The day was due to shut down around 7pm so still another 3 to 4 hrs if we were lucky.

Chris’ track…

Richard was now about 10kms in front of me but I did hear some voices I knew somewhere nearby.  After a bit of searching I met up with Jake Ali and Pete D.

It was nice to fly with friends again.  After a bit of banter on the radio it was decided that our goal was probably a bit out of reach now and also not exactly down wind any more so we decided to optimise our heading and try to push as downwind as possible and maybe reach 200kms instead.

We flew well together for the next 50k or so.  Someone marking the weaker climbs while one of us widened the search looking for a better climb.  It kept us tracking along at a reasonable pace.

By 6pm the climbs were weak.  I was getting tired.  We were around 170k with 30k needed for that 200k glory.  We were approaching Ely and it was very cloudy now with very little sun on the ground.  We split up a little,  all searching for anything to keep us in the air a little longer.

We drifted along in the weak stuff for what felt like forever until a patch of sun hit the ground just on the edge of Ely.  The whole area lifted in the most amazing 2m climbs everywhere.  We all had our own personal 2m super smooth climb back up to 4000ft in the evening sun.  It was the best climb of the day.  just me and my friends and Ely Cathedral below us.

Now one more decision had to be made...  15kms to 200.  But Ely has a double MATZ around it.  Mildenhall and Lakenheath.  We had been asked not to fly in or around either of these two areas as there were some sensitive discussions in progress with the military.

The sky was filling in again and the only cloud that looked like it may work was directly above Ely in the middle of Mildenhall.  I was at 4000ft so decided to eek in to see if it was working.  it wasn't,  so hastily beat a retreat back to the edge of the MATZ and back to safety, still with 300ft spare.

I think it was then decided there was no way around the airspace and our goal of 200km was not to be.

Jake, Pete and Ali headed back to the nearest train station at Littleport and I landed a little short worried about crossing some power lines.

We all re-grouped,  got an Uber into Ely, then a train to Peterborough, and on to Leeds.  Home by 11.30

It did turn out to be a very good day, and we did fly a very long way.  I have only ever once flown further. Lots of other pilots all got PB's including our very own Dales legend Jake Herbert,  still on form even after a year off.

A few did actually make that elusive 200k including the infamous Geoff Crossley.  Well done Geoff. New favourite hill maybe!  What a legend.

Coaching

Pete Logan, Chief Coach reports

How big is your toolkit?



I was unfortunate enough to be at an incident last month but fortunate for the pilot who had me and plenty of others around to jump on the wing and help with the incident. He was thankfully walking wounded from TO, at least until the adrenalin wore off. For this article I want to concentrate more on the lead up to what happened. He’d been plucked into the air by the strong wind… the take off was involuntary.

Do we have the tools in our bag to deal with the conditions we have on take off and landing, including not being a passenger when our flight starts? This translates to whether or not we have practised the techniques we need to fly more varied conditions safely. If not, then every condition we encounter, say on take off, would have to be a standard reverse launch. A little like only having a hammer to deal with nails and screws.

Cobra - for strong wind. It usually goes with your wing setup perpendicular to the wind which means getting plucked is preventable. Hand up - I’m guilty here. I got a free(ish) wing to really nail my cobras before going to a windy Brazil in 2024. I had the technique down when I got there but the unfamiliarity of launch meant that I stuck to what I knew and reverse launched, incurring extra time for the others in the tow queue. We need to be practised and confident in ourselves with new skills.

Running reverse - for tight nil wind TOs where you need to see the wing in good shape before committing.

Forward launch - We all started that way but how often does it get used? Do you know how it feels on your new wing yet? What if it needs a bit of a horse shoe layout to stop the tips wanting to come together.

Front/Rear riser launch (As & Cs on a 3-liner, As & Bs on a 2-liner) - A lot of us are still on cross hand reverse. That’s fine, but I recently changed wings to a Photon 2-liner this year and one afternoon on Parlick I was having all sorts of trouble launching when thermals were coming through. Too much energy at the top for the brakes to dissipate without me getting lifted and dumped. I got myself on Baildon on a fresh day to compare and contrast. I wanted to see how the Bs could control the wing in the top third.

Mushing in - just taken off but you’ve seen someone flying in front or had a shout that there’s a knot in the cascade. Can you get back in if you’re still on take off without a stall and prevent a shoot?

Backward landing - touch down and kill the wing on the rear risers whilst turning and running at the side of it, ready to ball up.

Accuracy landing (ish) - constant aspect or Ss for a field that you identified as safe but is on the less than ideal size.

Swoop landing / late flare - lighter wind conditions where you need to scrub off speed.

And why did I choose a rusty spanner up at the top? Occasional use and reuse reminds you that you know how to do this stuff.

British Open Series 2026 - Event 1

Doug Neil reports                 

After a couple of years flying in Scotland, the BOS hang gliding competition returned to the Dales with three classes of pilot - class 1(topless), class 5 (rigid) and sports class (kingposted). Running up to the competition, the weather forecast didn’t look promising but there were several non-flying activities planned just in case.

The 8-day comp ran from Saturday to Saturday and while day 1 was canned, we headed over to Model Ridge on Sunday for task 1. Conditions looked good at launch - an 84km task was set for class 1/5 and 30km task set for sport class.

The launch window opened and those who took off almost immediately managed to work their way around to the bowl on the right of launch and climb to cloudbase. Nick Collins (class 5) and Gordon Rigg (class 1) managed to make it to goal with another couple making a good distance from takeoff. For those, like myself, who were a bit slower to take off, soaring the ridge was difficult and it was congested with both hang gliders and paragliders. After a short while, the lift dropped off altogether and several found themselves in the field at the bottom. Sports class didn’t get activated.         

Searching for lift at Model Ridge

Monday was top-end windwise, but we all headed up to the north side of Semerwater to check out the conditions. A couple of pilots were keen to fly, but it didn’t look taskable and eventually we all made it down the hill to warm up.

The next couple of days were also blown out and rainy so we carried out parachute repacks, learnt all about the details of the GAP scoring system, reviewed everyones knowledge by taking the BHPA mock pilot exam and played at hang gliding on a few sets of VR equipment. For myself, I also bought and ate far too much Wensleydale cheese.

The weather looked like it was becoming better towards the second half of the week and we set up to fly at Wether Fell on Thursday. Over development was expected during the afternoon, so a quick 35km task to Masham was set for class 1/5 and a shorter task down Wensleydale for sports class. Cu-nimbs turned up early though and with rain incoming the task was canned soon after the launch window opened.

By Friday morning the conditions were much better and we headed to Wether Fell again. Class 1 and 5 were tasked to Pontefract (83km) with some airspace on the way to keep clear of. Sports class goal was over the back and down the Kettlewell valley to goal at Howgill for 28km.

Passing Semerwater while climbing to cloudbase on the way to the Kettlewell Valley

Soaring the ridge at Wether Fell was not as easy as expected – maybe some out of phase wave causing things to be rough and spoiling the ridge lift. Small rough thermals were the order of the day on the ridge; there were plenty of cases of scratching below takeoff for a while but eventually quite a few caught something decent and made it to cloudbase at 5600ft. Justin Needham (class 1) and Tim King (class 5) battled on to goal, with several others landing out partway down the course. In Sport Class, Tony Woodley and myself got to the head of the Kettlewell valley easily enough where we split up and I dribbled my way down the valley in zeroes for several more kilometres before landing at Threshfield, about 5km short of goal, but in first place for the day.

Trying hard to stay in zeroes while dribbling down the Kettlewell Valley

Saturday was the final day of the comp and we headed to Tailbridge. An approaching warm front shortened the tasks, but class 1/5 goal was about 52km (close to the A1) and sport class about 31km to Copley Road, north of Barnard Castle. Nearly all pilots made it away from the hill. Gordon Rigg (class 1) and three class 5 pilots made it to goal. Donnie Carson won sports class just a few km short of goal, with myself in second place after some errors which put me on the floor next to the A66.

Near cloudbase over the moors behind Tailbridge
About to land by the A66

Considering the forecast shortly before the comp, the BOS organisers did well ekeing out 3 good tasks in the week and providing some great non-flying activities. Everyone went home happy with the competition and looking forward to the second BOS event in Wales in the summer. Leaders in the BOS at the halfway point are:

Class 1 – Gordon Rigg

Class 5 – Tim King

Sport Class – Doug Neil

Safety - Flying white powder ??

Rob Urselmann reports

As those of you reading the club forum will have already seen, one of our members had a hidden equipment failure this month, which may have been a very lucky escape.

On landing (after a fly-down) one of the shoulder buckles on the pilot’s 7 year old, second hand harness, disintegrated in a puff of white powder. On inspection it was found that the other one wasn’t much better. Salt water or another type of corrosion is suspected. An IRF (incident report form) was submitted to the BHPA and supplier, and the harness has been consigned to the bin.

A BHPA Safety Advisory regarding same/similar problem was issued in 2024

- - - - -

“A pilot flying a paraglider at a coastal site recently reported a failure of the aluminium Brummel hook (sometimes called sister hooks) that connected his paraglider’s speed bar. This pilot flew regularly at the coast, and when checking found that the hooks on both sides had been badly corroded, almost certainly as a result of repeated exposure to sea air. The connection point was enclosed in a neoprene sheath and the damage was not apparent until the covering had been peeled away

These hooks are commonly used by several paragliding manufacturers, however it is not only these items that can be affected.

Periodic and thorough checking of all parts that are concealed is crucial for flight safety. Contact with salt water or even sea air can cause significant corrosion to aluminium components, particularly if they are in contact with steel. Similar corrosion and even failure has been reported in the past on hang glider wires and where bolts pass through aluminium tubes.

Aluminium carabiners generally contain a steel pivot pin and sometimes a steel spring; any corrosion of these parts may not be visible.

All pilots should take the time to closely examine their Brummel hooks, carabiners, and any other components that may be affected. If you fly at the coast, the safe working life of these items will be significantly reduced. If you have any doubts they should be replaced.”
