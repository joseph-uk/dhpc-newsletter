Chairman's Chat

Martin Baxter reports

You may recall that, in the January edition of Skywords, I outlined the arrangements in the draft Letter of Agreement (LoA) that allow us to fly inside controlled airspace at Baildon, Ilkley Moor, and, with permission, an air corridor through the controlled airspace between Addingham Moorside and Ilkley Moor.

I’m delighted to report that the agreement went live on 20 Apr 23.  In my previous article I promised to publish a copy of the final LoA but, since it runs to 15 pages, most of which you won’t be interested in, I thought that you might appreciate a quick summary instead.

You can fly at Baildon and Ilkley Moor up to 100’ AGL without additional permission.  If Leeds Bradford Airport grants permission you can fly up to 1,500’ AMSL within a 1nm radius of the site (trig point for Baildon, Pancake Stone for Ilkley).

With permission you can also open up an air corridor between Addingham Moorside and the Pancake Stone.  This corridor extends 1nm either side of a straight-line track between the 2 sites.  If you do try to fly between sites first identify the location of Will Hall’s Wood (approx. 1nm West of the Pancake Stone).  The ceiling of the corridor is 2,000’ AMSL to the West of it, but only 1,500’ AMSL to the East of it.

If you do make a booking, announce it on the WhatsApp Flying Plans group.  Please, please remember to close it when the last pilot lands, and post that on the group as well (otherwise I’ll be chasing you!).

I’ve updated all 3 site guides accordingly.  So, other than the above, what other useful snippets does the LoA contain?  Mostly disclaimers from LBA:

·        Only fly VFR.

·        If you are ‘uncertain of position’ land as soon as is practical.

·        LBA will not provide an air traffic service and deconflict with other aircraft (including drones).  Just like flying in class G airspace.

·        ATC assumes no responsibility for wake turbulence from nearby heavy aircraft carrying out visual approaches to LBA.

You can obtain the current runway in use, QNH and weather conditions by calling the automated ATIS recording on 01133 911698.  Try it now!

Was it worth all the effort for a bit of ridge soaring that we would probably have got away with anyway?  Will anybody seek permission to raise the ceilings or open the corridor?  Probably not; but it does at least demonstrate to LBA that we are professional aviators.  That might just come in handy when it comes to negotiating the latest iteration of their Airspace Change Proposal, which may have far more serious consequences.

UK XContest 2023

Pete D reports

Long live the King (of XC leagues)

A little later starting this year, but the UK's national XContest is up and running at www.xcontest.org/united-kingdom and promises to be great. This year it has taken on a monumental shift and is supported by some of the biggest names in the UK cross-country scene along with xcmag.com to help ensure its long-term success.

Register and enter your flights and lets see if the Dales can win the flying clubs league for a third year in a row? Its free, simple, pilot focussed, user driven, employs a modern platform and is there for all levels of XC pilot. A/B wings, and C wings have their own league, and when considered in the open or club leagues, they benefit from a handicap to help level the flying field. Hangies are even allowed, with their own league and handicaps.

There are prizes for your first 100km open distance, and for your first 200 point flight. The multipliers for triangles are based on UK cross-country statistics, as opposed to some other ideal, and thus there should be parity between closed circuit and down wind scores. There will also be a people’s choice award for the most interesting, technical and aesthetic flights each month.

So why enter and what is different to the existing UK XCleague?

As it is based on the world renown XContest platform, uploading flights is far less finicky than the original UK XCleague (still good to put your flights here for now as well), even supporting automatic or one-touch uploads from XCTrack and Flyskyhigh. You can see flying buddies, add photos and comment on flights as well.

By removing the declare aspect, XContest is simpler, more accessible and just leaves pilots to try and fly the best for the day. By adjusting the scoring for closed-circuit flights to realistic levels, the challenges of flying triangles are equalised with those of navigating the complexities of open-distance flying in the UK (airspace, sea-breezes, etc).

The inclusion of separate leagues for A/B wings, and C wings, along with handicaps for the open and club leagues, means that the league is a place for all pilots. Pilots that choose to fly wings other than the latest EN-D two liner or competition class have an opportunity to meaningfully participate as opposed to languishing in the depths but still paying for the prizes of others.

The league is based upon a pilot's best three flights as opposed to six, giving far more opportunities to those that either work, have inflexible jobs, or live in a part of the country that has far less reliable flying weather, e.g. the Dales and up North in general. To do well in the old XC league one had to be prepared to travel all round the country, potentially at short notice, just to make sure they did not miss a potentially high-scoring day.

Finally, this is a pilot-represented league, run by a committee, who are there to listen to pilots’ views and shape the contest accordingly.

Personally, and obviously from an unbiased perspective, I think this is a great and long-awaited opportunity to move XC flying forward again in the UK, bring the fun back into the process, be more inclusive, and hopefully tempt one or two old hands back who lost the will due to one or more of the former shortcomings. In the long term the UK XContest may well obsolete the UK XC league, but I would encourage everyone to contribute their Dales flights to both leagues in the short term.

Year One…

Rob Urselmann reports

“I cannot wait to talk to you in a bit of detail about this because there are lots of options.   But I also feel the responsibility of an addict introducing a friend to heroin…!”

  This was the response in January last year when, having seen him gliding overhead on our local hill, I tentatively asked a local pilot about paragliding. A few months of casual research and much swithering between going overseas (sun shine!) and staying local (I was time flexible and found I live a mere 10 minutes from Active Edge’s training hill Tow Scar!), I opted for local and contacted Dean Crosby on a Thursday. It happened there was a cancellation for that very Saturday so I quickly booked and 16th April 2022 was my first day ‘on the hill’.  The weather was sunny but rather windy so most of the day was spent ground-handling the mini wings; good fun, but not what we came for. Then “it might be calmer by evening”  and my first introduction to ‘para-waiting’… Lucky for us by 6pm the wind indeed settled and I had my first tentative glide a few feet off the ground above the bottom of Tow…. and I was hooked!

 

16 April 2022: My very first pull-up and glide on Tow Scar, under close instruction by Dean (photo by unknown fellow student)

  Scouring ebay, I found a local very cheap, very old, very tired wing to practise ground handling, and proceeded to spend numerous challenging and frustrating but happy hours trying to keep this ‘dog’ in the air before commitments and the weather saw my second school day (or rather evening) on the hill not until nearly five weeks later. Still, I completed the EP early June and immediately signed up for CP. Also then the order for my own brand-new wing went in (Ozone Geo 6 MS, ‘only’ 64 days delivery time) and a harness was purchased. Little did I know then of the Telegram ‘Paragliding Gear For Sale’ group…

  Starting the CP course on my own harness was a big improvement over the school one and far more comfortable and enjoyable. It also meant I could get familiar with the harness before my own wing arrived. CPC day 1 was followed by two late evening sessions (the huge advance of being ‘super-local’) but then more typical Dales’ weather put things on hold for a number of weeks. Mid July saw me ticked off on most tasks and the CP exam, but despite a few fly days the weather didn’t cooperate for soaring and top landing. Then I went down with Covid and got grounded. Just as I was getting over that I got an app from Dean “Hey Rob, Toy has arrived!🪂”.  The day I was allowed out after the two weeks quarantine, the 8th August, was a fly day and I was booked on it!   Pulling up my own, crispy-new wing for the first time to ground handle it for familiarisation was nice, but then taking off on it was magic. On my first or second take-off on the Geo that day I was soaring and the next flight I top-landed…and had finished my Club Pilot !  

  Now what???  Living exactly on the boundary, I joined both DHPC and CSC. However, I only vaguely knew one other pilot and wasn’t yet on the various ‘Flying Plans’ messaging groups. That week I graduated was the start of that big heat-wave, and with a little wind predicted a few days later I hiked up Whernside on my own hoping there’d be other pilots. As I arrived at TO there were already a few pilots (“Pheeeuw!”) and I introduced myself. They turned out all DHPC members and we sat out the time for the wind to pick up. After a few hours of pleasant para-waiting (and learning quickly it wasn’t just me as an utter newby swithering if/what/when the wind would be good) the wind picked up, people took off and I followed. It was stonking hot, the geo-wind was a light easterly and (as I was told) the sea breeze kicked in resulting in a very nice convergence smack over Whernside making for superb soaring. That very first flight I was up for 2:16 hours, got to 931m and only came down because I was bursting for a pee! To date, this is still my longest flight.

  The next day was rather windier with TO blown-out when I got there, and no way I was going to take-off. Lucky for me again however, also up there were a few different DHPC members, including coach Minhay Minhay. With Minhay’s friendly support, newby me was able to safely take off from lower down the hill and had another brilliant hour and half soaring. The weather held and the next day I was up Whernside again, now with a pleasant light breeze. After another hour and something soaring it was time for something constructive and I nailed five top landings before gliding to BL as the wind dropped for the day. One week and I had 5.5 hours under my belt!  Little did I know then that that had been an exceptionally good flying week in the entire UK…

  A few weeks later and I had been invited to join a local pilot gaggle of “adventurous/XC/coffee/beer minded bunch that fate finds around [M6] Junction 36”, many of whom are hugely experienced. Soon I was flying the local hills in both Dales and Lakes with always somebody else also keen to go out, introduce me to new sites and generally keep me out of mischief.  Little did I know then just how lucky I was to end up in this very active & supportive gaggle, and the steep development curve it would allow me to go through…

  September was our first family camper hiking trip to the French Alps since covid, and this year it was accompanied by my flying kit for the “occasional flying when my wife’s feet need a break”. With a ton of advice and suggestions from above bunch, a few weeks into our trip I found myself free-flying Mount Lachat at le Grand Bornand (my first pure thermals), La Clusaz (TTB) and then, finally, we found ourselves in the Annecy area.

Glide down from La Clusaz, French Alps, 18-Sep-2022  (Photo Leonie Rutter)

Having been warned about how crazy crowded the Col de la Forclaz take-off (TO) can get, I was a bit nervous about this one, so went up early in the morning to get familiar with the process of getting to TO from Doussard (put your bag next to railing in the mini-bus queue!), TO (three side by side, very well organised) and in near-calm conditions had a pleasant 12 minute glide back to the landing field at Doussard (massive!). I bussed back up and para-waited for the thermals to start working. Eating lunch, I observed the first gaggle take off in light thermals and then got ready. Busier now, but not as crowded as I feared. After take-off I soared the local area trying to find thermals in what was still the most crowded I’d flown in. Unable to get above the treeline, eventually I bit the bullet and followed early leavers along the NE to the next face, le Collet. Once there the wind was more on and it was comfortable soaring beats to get to the top. Having taken that mental first step of going away from a ‘known’ lift area, the next decision was quickly made and I pushed on to the next face in a no-expectations, one step at a time mentality. The glide was smooth and next thing I knew I was soaring up the Dents de Lanfon and then 250+m above it. Holy smoke!!! I had already read up about it but never really expected to get to this point on this trip! I pointed the wing SW toward Lac d’Annecy, and a few minutes later I was floating high above the lake and, now my brain load has reduced for a few minutes, enjoying the spectacular scenery. “OMG, I’m actually doing the famous Petit Tour!!”  Arriving on the other side, not wanting to be exposed to possible lee, I pointed ever so slightly on the windward side of the Roc de Boeufs ridge… and failed to find sufficient lift to get to the ridge top. I tried for a while but unable to gain height and with HV pilons further on I landed out, hiked down the hill and eventually managed to catch the correct bus back to Doussard (note to self: carry my reading glasses).[track]

  The next day, after initially watching the SIV pilots from the beach, I get another pass from my beloved who’s happy to have another off-her-feet day, and (before she can change her mind!) quickly shoot off. Back at Forclaz, after take-off I push a bit too far to the south, find myself in sink and have to fight to find lift and get back to TO height. From there however it’s good and I push out on a repeat of yesterday’s route. Arriving at Col de Frêtes I can see a rescue helicopter on Dents de Lanfon so I’m not pushing on. Passing back on the wall of Col de Frêtes, I pick up a kicking thermal at 1500m and soar up the face and then above it at a for newby-me very spicy 4+ m/s and in no time I’m at the 2000m blue-sky inversion. Two high-aspect gliders also up there disappear NE over the back; no way I’m following them!  I’m on my own but I’m higher than yesterday, so off I go for a second attempt at le Petit Tour. This time I point smack over the top of the Roc de Boeufs on arrival and find sustained ridge lift. However, it is still not enough to make way along the ridge, let be clear the HV pilons, so I soar the head hoping for more lift. Later arriving pilots catch up but don’t get ahead either. As one of them gives up and heads for Doussard I soon follow him, flying over our camper site (“I could’ve landed right there in the mown field across the street”) and toward the beach (leg waving at my wife on the beach although I can’t see her; but she waved at me as she’d followed my livetrack). I finish the holiday with another 5.5 hours under my belt and on an absolute high.

  Back in the UK, autumn has arrived and flying days are getting rather few, but I manage to get out on most flyable days. Many are very short flights indeed (video: Barkin TTB - Ed Cleasby) but late November I manage a short XC from Ingleborough to land west of Ingleton just short of the A65. December and January sees the winter deep frost but still some flights, albeit short & cold! (video: Semer water - Ed Cleasby).

On final approach with ‘observers’ for a parking side landing at Stags 12-Feb-’23  (Photo Ed Cleasby)

Take off on Cautley 14-Feb-’23 (photos Ed Cleasby, montage by author)

  In early February the BPCup application opens and whilst driving to site somewhere, Ed Cleasby suggests it’d be a great experience-builder for me to participate. “What? Me? Already??”. “Sure”. “Emmm. Ok. Cool”.  Just one tiny hurdle: participants must be Pilot rating and I’m only on 21 hours.  The organisers are extremely kind and allow me to sign up “subject to” (Thanks Viv!) and the pressure is on!

  February and March see more very short flights, freezing cold soaring flights, hike-up-top-blown-out-hike-down days, a few ‘interesting’ days (see my article last month), but thankfully also several excellent days.  I manage to clock up more than enough time and the weather cooperates to complete the last few tasks. I study a lot and in mid March sit my Pilot exam… and pass! However, I’m starting to know just how little I really know!...

Part of the J36 gaggle setting up on Pike Fell 10-April-2023: L2R Arthur (visitor), author, Kevin McLoughlin, Phil Dale, Mark Gravestock, Glenn Brookes. (Photo Ed Cleasby)

  The weather starts to warm up a bit and I manage my first short forays into UK XC. On 8 April I take off from Barkin South and step straight into a thermal to cloud base at 1678m / 5500ft.  “Whhooooohaaaaaa”!! “I touched a cloud”!! The views are absolutely amazing!  From there however, unable to find a second climb over the Howgills, I only manage to get to Cautley Spout for a 16.2km turn-point distance. No photos as I still deliberately avoid that distraction, but here is the SportsTrackLive ‘Scene’ with 6 of the pilots that day, including John Hamlett’s 104km Scottish Border Raider run.

  15 April 2023, the last day of my first PG year, is a marginal light SSE’ly but I’m heading out. Together with my now “addiction buddy” (the one from the introduction quote), I spend a pleasant hour or so boating about on “our local”, Gregarath. I even manage to get a bit further in my attempts to complete the Gregarath Kings Road Defined Flying Challenge by just squeezing around the Green Hill dogleg and tagging the Gt Coum turnpoint, but as expected bomb out trying to get back (DFC Gregarath task results).  Nice way to round of my year…

  In numbers, I did 36 hours during 69 flights from 22 different sites and covered 844 km track distance. Overall, my first year paragliding has been far beyond my wildest expectations.  I can’t speak for heroin, but I am definitely addicted!

PS: a nod to everybody in the J36 gaggle for company, support and general para-bollocks, and a special thanks to Ed Cleasby for his coaching and support.

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Last year just 3 incidents were reported officially by DHPC members.  Do you think this is accurate?  Remembering that an incident is an event that ‘could have’ and not just ‘has’ resulted in an injury to self, harm to others or property and equipment.  The intention behind this is that if a pilot has had an experience of such a nature, others can also have the benefit of learning from it and therefore, also improve their risk management practices and collective learning.

Please be reminded to report all incidents which includes equipment failures and near misses to me and also, the BHPA incident online report here:

Submit Incident Report (bhpa.co.uk)

Head in the Clouds, Feet on the Ground

Joseph Edmonds reports from Macedonia

I’m writing this at 1pm. I’m supposed to be right in the middle of a competition task, however the weather has other ideas. I’ve come for the first time to Macedonia, to take part in the very first round of the SRS - Sports Racing Series.

As you can see, we’re not exactly enjoying prime paragliding conditions right now.

We managed to get a practice day and a first task day, but for the last two days, the clouds have descended, the moisture is abundant and pilots are killing time in various ways.

Macedonia might at first seem like a far off country, somewhere miles away in distant lands. In reality it is north of Greece and not as far away as Turkey. It is not currently part of the EU and it definitely has a strong post Soviet vibe.

The flying area of Krusevo is (when the weather is nice) allegedly a paragliding paradise. I can really see why. The wide valley flanked by hills is very reminiscent of Roldanillo in Columbia. The flying is definitely thermic - the hills are generally too shallow and inconsistent to be of any use in a classic ridge soaring sense.

On the practice day we had a task set though there was no real pressure to follow it. On the other hand, if you decided to go your own way then you would be fully in charge of your own retrieval. Not necessarily a problem, but for the first day in a new place I decided to go with the flow and stick with the group and enjoy the benefits of an informal practice day  retrieve service.

Flying conditions were not amazing, it was definitely weak and there is a gap in the mountains that must be crossed that claimed me and many other pilots. It seemed very difficult to get high before crossing which meant many people, including me, just went for it at a really quite low height, just scraping onto the low hills on the other side. These low hills didn’t really work and I landed in the valley. Landing opportunities are plentiful and very friendly. The single small road is ideal if you want to hitch hike - with plenty of cars passing.

The first task day saw me launching about 45 minutes before the start and enjoying a really scenic time lazily thermalling above the town of Krusevo along with a few other pilots. Turns out that I was sharing a thermal with Marcus King, of XC mag and he was taking some nice photos. I’m on the red Delta 4

Due to the fact that takeoff is so high, I found myself flying close to the ground quite a lot - even though I had big altitude numbers showing on the vario. Some people did seem to get a lot higher but it seemed to require a committing line into the back mountains which as a total newbie to the area was not something I was particularly interested in doing. The top places of the first (and only task) were all taken by local pilots who were able to really make the best of the conditions and flew quite a long way off the optimised course line and instead took the high but long way around the higher peaks.

You can read all about the competition on the official Airtribune page and can watch a replay if you are interested. We’ve certainly killed a bit of time watching these - it’s quite interesting to see how small decisions can make a big difference in terms of distance flown and also the speed. As we heard on a really interesting talk last night (see best of the internet section for a link) - a two liner with a 60kmh top speed will pull away 1km every minute - so dawdling in a thermal for three more (roughly 20 second) turns will see you very quickly lose position.

SRS is a new competition with four rounds this year - and planning to do 5 next year. The focus is on the sports class and it has really been birthed on the back of new rules allowing 2 liner C class gliders, though low Cs and high Bs are still worth flying and definitely welcome. For people like me who have done some lower level comp and fancy something a bit higher level but without just being left for dead by top end two liners - it’s great.

With a bit of luck I might get my feet off teh ground again before I go home, but for now its head in the clouds and feet on the ground
