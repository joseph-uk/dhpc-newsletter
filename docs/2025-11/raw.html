Farmers’ Dinner

Martin Baxter reports

We don’t pay site fees in the Dales.  Instead, our sites’ officers deliver a bottle of Christmas spirit to each farmer; and we host them for a free 3-course dinner and drinks.  This year the dinner was on Saturday 18th October, returning to the Knights Table in Stainforth.  We laid on a minibus for those in and around Upper Wensleydale.

I got slightly flustered when 4 farmers turned up unexpectedly (due to finger trouble on our part), but organiser Stef Sykes and the staff at the Knights Table took it all in their stride.

The function used to be popular with (hang glider) pilots but, as interest dwindled, we moved it further North to better accommodate the farmers.  Interest from pilots reduced even further, and we now have to give attending committee members a free meal as an incentive to act as hosts.  Last year the farmers still outnumbered pilots by a margin, so this year we also offered free tickets to coaches, by way of thanks for their contribution to the club.  Only 2 took advantage of the offer.

The farmers’ dinner really is a ‘force multiplier’: a little goodwill goes a long way.  We send invitations to numerous farmers knowing that if they all accepted, we wouldn’t be able to find a venue big enough, and that the club would be bankrupt; but experience has shown that they don’t.  Together with Christmas drinks it will cost the club about £2,700 this year.  I suspect that the alternative, site fees, would cost an awful lot more.  I don’t know of any other clubs that have a similar tradition and it’s something that we should cherish and be proud of.

Everybody agreed that the dinner had been excellent, and the drinks flowed freely.  In his speech the club chairman thanked the farmers for their continued support in allowing us to fly from their land in the beautiful Yorkshire Dales.  He explained that the club eventually came 5/37 in the UK XC League (we were in 1st place after a stonking Spring).  Top Dales pilot was Chris Kay whose best 6 flights were all greater than 100km.  Peter Darwood (and Chris) had the longest flight.  They took off from Cautley Spout and landed at the coast, North of Berwick upon Tweed, some 6½ hours later.  Richard Meek completed the best triangle flying from Windbank to Masham to Hawes and back to Windbank.

Ed Cleasby thanked Martin Baxter for his long standing contribution as chairman and presented him with a certificate granting honorary lifetime membership of the club.  It’s reportedly the first time that Martin has been lost for words!

David Lambert responded on behalf of the farmers explaining that this year’s drought has been tough on many farmers, but that they still take pleasure in seeing us soaring through the skies

Regional Airspace User Working Group

Martin Baxter reports

Previous reports are available on the DHPC forum.

Introduction

The aim of each RAUWG is to reduce the risk of a mid-air-collision (MAC) between military and civilian airspace users within its region.

At this latest meeting I was joined by Pete Logan, Chief Coach at the DHPC; and Simon Hulme who will be standing as Airspace Officer at the DHPC AGM on 4 Dec.

There was quite a lot of repeated material and I am only reporting by exception.  If you want to know more see my previous reports, the DHPC Forum or drop me a line.

Electronic Conspicuity (EC) was very much the theme of the day.

Future Exercises

No change to lodger units at RAF Leeming.

10 Nov – 5 Dec: Ex YORKNITE (Swiss F18s)

19 Jan – 6 Feb 26: Ex HYPERIAN STORM

2 Mar – 13 May: Ex AGILE WARRIOR

16 Mar – 27 Mar: Ex COBRA WARRIOR

Pilot Aware

Keith Vinning delivered another presentation on Pilot Aware.  He claims the solution to all our EC interoperability issues.  In addition to the existing network of ATOM ground stations he’d like to add more in the Yorkshire Dales (the site of our Holfuy weather station is unsuitable because it doesn’t have a power source).  I agreed to send him a link to PureTrack, which he didn’t seem to be aware of.  If you have a Flarm device the good news is that the signal will be picked up and relayed by other Pilot Aware devices.

DHPC/BHPA

Simon Hulme introduced himself.  Subject to confirmation at the DHPC AGM, he’ll represent the club as Airspace Officer in future.  I’ll continue to attend as BHPA representative.

I confirmed the BHPA’s position on EC (we oppose the mandatory use of ADS-B, instead supporting the European Hang gliding and Paragliding Union solution of mobile phone technology ADS-L).  I explained some of the particular issues we face:

·         Obscuration and antennas.

·         Unknown effects on the human body.

·         Requirement for OUT only.

·         Shortfalls in the concept of an Atypical Air Environment.

We talked about NOTAMs and the unassured data provided by third party software (XCTrack, Notam Info, etc).

The published opening times for the Leeming MATZ are (UTM/GMT) Mon-Thu 0700 – 1700, Fri 0800 – 1600.  For Topcliffe it’s Sat/Sun and public holidays 0730 – SS+15.  I had always considered the ATZ to be permanent, with the timings simply reflecting the hours when ATC would be manned.   To my astonishment they confirmed that the ATZ were not active outside those times, other than by NOTAM.  Unless there is a NOTAM in place you can legally fly through the Leeming ATZ at weekends, and through the Topcliffe ATZ during the working week!

BPCup 4 – 7 Jun 26, based at the Dalesbridge Centre, near Settle.

Civil Aviation Authority

Confirmed receipt of about 800 submissions to their EC consultation (actually 743 see here), some of which came from BHPA members.  They will all be considered by people rather than AI.

There was mention of a trial conducted by the BHPA to erect temporary EC beacons when flying sites are active.  Personally I can’t see it working from a logistical perspective.

Defence Airspace & Air Traffic Management

Amazon Air has requested a Temporary Restricted Area in the area of Darlington, 12km radius up to 700’ AGL, active Mon-Fri 0900-1700.  Activation planned for 6 months from Dec 25 (with an option to extend to 12 months).  If approved this will be an additional obstacle for PG pilots attempting to fly to the coast North of Teesside.

The Topcliffe MATZ is under review.  In my personal view it doesn’t warrant an ATZ or MATZ, but it’s sensitive to the RAF because it’s where young cadets take to the air, in close proximity to Sutton Bank.

Following an AIRPROX report, the wording in the AIP regarding MATZ will be clarified.  Civilian aircraft will still be legally permitted to enter, even though it may not be sensible to do so.  The RAF Safety Centre recently issued a gentle reminder that you aren’t legally required to clean your teeth!  Not all common sense is mandated by law!

From 25 Dec all ATZ and MATZ will be displayed on charts in AGL rather than showing the airfield elevation (shown in brackets).

In future military TRAs (mostly offshore) will be activated by NOTAM rather than the default of being active between published opening hours.  This represents more flexible use of airspace.  (Comment: it would be helpful if other danger areas (ranges, etc) followed suit to save individuals having to phone through to check if a range is actually active.)

Other  

A number of gliding clubs provided updates.  Rufforth will be hosting a gliding competition 8 – 15 Aug 26.  Sherburn, just South of Leeds East is a very active airfield which all XC pilots should be aware of.  A ‘heatmap’ showing (ADS-B?) activity showed that, whilst RAF Linton-on-Ouse has closed, the airfield still acts as a magnet for training, and can be quite busy.

We had an interesting presentation from Durham Constabulary on their drone capabilities.  They have proved very successful in detecting criminals and missing persons, and at less than 10% of the price of launching a helicopter, very cost effective.  The force plans to train more operators.

We’ve Been Doing it Wrong All This Time!

Daedalus reports

Young spiders can migrate quite long distances by spinning out a thread of gossamer which catches in the wind and carries them away. A long fine fibre of this sort makes a splendid spider parachute - an ingenious use of the principle that the viscous drag of a fibre in a gas stream increases with its length, but doesn't depend very much on its diameter.

Daedalus calculates that a similar parachute for a man would need about 10 000 km of fibre. A single length wouldn't do for one thing, it would snap and for another, the atmosphere isn't high enough. But 10 000 or so each 1 km long should work nicely, and if made of the finest possible glass or carbon fibre need only be 0.01 mm in diameter, giving a total weight of some 2 kg.

Daedalus did wonder if a dedicated hippie could grow enough hair on his head to enable him to leap safely from buildings or aircraft in flight; but even a thick 200 000-hair thatch with every hair a metre long is hardly adequate.

Besides, the hair is packed too densely for each fibre to be fully exposed to the airstream. Daedalus's fibre-chute will deploy an extending framework from which the filaments can spread out freely as they unwind.

This novel parachute will have many advantages. Not only will it slow its user gently as its fibres extend, instead of jerking brutally open, but it will be entirely invisible from the ground. The military would be able to drop parachutists relatively inconspicuously, and in any case the sight of seemingly unsupported personnel drifting down from the sky might demoralize the opposition.

Daedalus also sees uses in aircraft-safety. A big parachute with its disastrous opening jerk cannot be used to save a stricken plane (well maybe a microlight), but a mere ton or so of fibres payed out from a 70-ton craft could gently slow it to a few metres per second falling speed.

And on the personal scale, Daedalus is devising a sporting fibre-pack which releases fibres or even melt-extrudes them spider-fashion. If released into the base of a thermal, the filaments would climb in the rising air and lift the wearer joyfully into the sky, later depositing him or her safely miles away. This new sport, combining the delights of gliding and ballooning, should perhaps be called Arachnautics.

Book Review - Hanging in There by Jon R Chambers

Martin Baxter reports

One man and his Dad take on the Alps in the World’s Toughest Race (X-Alps 2011)

I’d seen this book knocking about in the club library box (available at club nights) but never got around to reading it.  When Ed gave me a second copy to pass on to the library it came with his recommendation so, even though I’m not into Hike & Fly, I decided to give it a go.  I have to say that I thoroughly enjoyed it.  My only criticism is that the maps are pretty poor and the ones on his blogspot (which hasn’t been updated since 2013) don’t seem to work.  I was going to write a review but then discovered that our club librarian, back in 2013, had already done it for me.

You could pay £49 for it on Amazon, or borrow it for free from the club library.  Over to Melise Harland in 2013:

xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx

With the 2013 X Alps commencing on the 7th of July I thought this was a good time to review Jon Chambers book on his experiences in the 2011 event. I confess at the start that competition paragliding doesn’t interest me in the slightest, but this race, this is a completely different beast.

So let’s start with a bit of background on the race. The Red Bull X-Alps is the world’s toughest adventure race where athletes race across the Alps from Saltzburg to Monaco, by foot or by paraglider. This is a straight line distance of approximately 1,000km. Once the first pilot reaches goal, the others have 48 hours to get there before the race ends. It sounds simple put like that but it is far from it with some athletes hiking over 100km a day and covering over 900km by foot over the length of the race.

The paragliding can be rather hairy with pilots attempting to fly in less than perfect conditions to take the burden off their wrecked feet for a while and make up time lost on the ground. Each team consisted of one athlete and one supporter in 2011 but for 2013 they can have up to two supporters. The support team are there to help with logistics, food and much needed encouragement and support. The athletes can race between the hours of 5am and 10:30 pm and if not flying they have to carry their paraglider and various other bits of safety equipment.

The book follows Jon through his 2011 race in which he finished a cracking 5th, a day short of Monaco, with his dad providing support. There is a forward by Tom Payne then a chapter on what the book is about. There is then a chapter for each of the 14 days of the race. At the end are two appendices giving the equipment and preparation for the race and paragliding terms for non-pilots.

This book is a great insight into the race from the athlete’s point of view. It goes from giving some idea of how the previous year of planning came into play to how desperate you can feel watching other pilots fly over your head. I hadn’t appreciated how much strategy was involved really; I’d imagined it was more brute strength and meteorology than anything. I also hadn’t realised how much the pilots communicated with each other for safety and in some instances walking together for company. It’s very obvious from some sections how tiredness affected the decision making so this  communication was a very good thing. The mention of how many locals seem to go out to greet the pilots and give occasional advice after seeing the pilots nearby on the live tracking is a nice bit of detail as well.

Having watched the 2011 race on the live tracking myself the book brought back a lot of memories of me sitting there at home thinking “why has he gone that way?”, as he describes why he made the decisions he did at various places. I found the chapter on equipment and preparation really interesting mostly as the training seemed a lot less harsh than I had imagined, though I don’t think I’ll be doing it any time soon. I liked the open and honest style of the book, it was good to see that these super humans do get tired and scared sometimes as well as us mere mortals.

A fast paced book that I found gripping, well worth a read.

 

Safety

Clive Sury reports

Reviewing this year’s flying goals

 

 

This year my goal was to develop my skills further through finding thermals away from the hill with potential to go XC.  Like many pilots, I’m keenly aware of the need to keep developing skills in order to stay focused with flying and, by way of developing my flying abilities, tangentially increase my margins of safety.  It also affords a tremendous sense of achievement in getting better at this sport.  Even if for those that know me, you ignore my shouts of frustration sometimes when I’m flying if I’m about to land precisely where I didn’t want to!

To attain this year’s objectives, I applied two principal strategies.  The first was to undertake Ed Cleasby’s NSC and other tasks as detailed on XCMap.net.  The other approach was to enroll on a XC Course with Chris Williams at High Sierras.

Prior to this year, I admit to having technophobia regarding using map functions on a vario and especially, getting to grips with setting up waypoints and tasks.  Indeed, my attempt at doing this at the beginning of the year was extremely frustrating since my vario denoted the way points as tiny dots.  Needless to say, on what was a booming day, I couldn’t get my vario to acknowledge I’d flown over a pinprick and had to give up.  However, fortunately on another visit to Windbank, Jack Herbert was there and fixed my Air3 to show the cylinders (thanks Jake).  I’ve found flying to waypoints has stretched my flying to places outside my natural comfort zone and given me a welcome additional focus in my flying.  If being a little over focused on getting to a waypoint and then having shorter flight.  Resulting in landing down the valley through not taking time to gain sufficient height to get to the first waypoints and carry on the rest of the task.

Despite this, for those that haven’t done these tasks, I’d definitely state that it’s really helped my skills in reaching out to the far edges of hills such as Gragareth and Whernside.  Plus, I’ve finally learnt how to download igc files and upload them to various portals to record the flights.  Although again, I wish I’d known about the XC League requiring flights to be loaded within 4 weeks (who knew?!).  I’ve finally been talked through how to do this in October and doing better on this has gone into next year’s objectives.

I’ve learnt how to set up tasks in an Air3 and now Naviter OMNI vario that I now use.  Like many things, it becomes easier the more one does this, with the OMNI working really well in indicating where the next waypoint is and confirming when the cylinder has been crossed.  One aspect of the OMNI is that the screen size is small as a tradeoff for its size and weight.  It is possible to scroll out and it does have an automatic function to zoom in and out to see the map.  Upon Ed’s suggestion I’ve also created laminated mini maps of the task which help focus on where the goal after next is and placing this on my flight deck.  Jordan’s face was a picture when he saw such a map this week, with his comment of me going ‘old school’.  I can confirm that pride is the first thing out the window in this sport.

The safety aspect in these tasks is that it enables me to still fly in the company of others, remain within the bounds of the hill and valley being flown; building my skills in a gradual way, such as landing out but within easy reach for pickup and hopefully maintaining radio contact too.

Regarding going XC, I felt I needed to understand at a deeper level about how to build flights, from finding thermals to continuing onwards on a flight path.  Chris Williams on his XC course showed how to look at the terrain on a flight for where the likely lift points for next thermals would be.  Plus, how to optimise the weather at cloud base, look for thermal activity and any convergence to optimise lift opportunities.  This was instrumental in helping me to see how to complete the famous Ronda run in Algodonales and give me the confidence to try it.  Happy to say on my recent return trip that I achieved this and also, many of the waypoint tasks that Chris has created there.  In many of the XC flights I found that I’m behind the gaggle and playing catchup.  However, the communications from the flyers ahead, plus the detailed morning briefings by Chris really helped me push further than I would naturally have done.  The outcome with Chris’ and Keiron Ripley’s guidance at High Sierras is that I’ve definitely attained a higher level of flying.

Photos of Ronda taken by a fellow paraglider on the trip.

 

From my recent trip I should mention one incident though (now submitted to BHPA incident report).  We were on a XC flight from the El Bosque launch site.  The plan was to get height and go along the mountain range to then either fly over the lake by Zahara or along it, with sufficient height for either route.  I made it to Zahara but had lost a lot of height on the last leg.  I was planning a landing in one of two fields when I got a thermal.  Following the mantra of never waste an opportunity, I worked this lift but was then taken back behind the castle on the hill above the town.  This led to a severe wing collapse through being on the lee side of the wind.  I can’t guarantee that having attended a SIV two years ago saved the situation but I do know that I consciously reacted by pulling my feet in from my pod to maximise any pendulum effect, plus placed my hands up, wait for reinflation and weight shift with brake input on any inflated side, pending size of said reinflation.  The wing suffered a further collapse, albeit less severe, and I made to come into land further down the area away from the hill.

What I found interesting was that before this I’d been very focused on not being on the lee side of the prior mountains and ridges yet in an instant I had fallen foul of this from a momentary lack of thought and incorrect focus on getting a save climb.  Lesson learnt to stay focused, and re-apply judgement to land first in safety, which in this case was in front (upwind) of the town. I’m also strongly considering doing another SIV next year.

In summary, this year I set out to know how to use the map and task setting within a vario for tasks and also, develop XC skills which have taken me out of my comfort zone but still within my own safety margins.  Thanks to Ed for setting up these tasks and Chris Williams I’ve managed to achieve and enjoy a higher level of flying.  Also, thanks to numerous others both inside and outside this club that have helped me this year with invaluable advice and your good company.

Looking forward to next year’s challenges.

Safe flying

Clive.

 

BHPA membership cover clarification for trips abroad

This note is no way intended to inflame anyone or to be seen as the proverbial ‘flight prevention officer’.  However, for those that need to be aware, please know that if a Club Pilot intends to go XC on trips abroad, then they are only covered under the BHPA 3rd party insurance if each XC flight has been submitted by the instructor (or Senior Coach) in advance of the flight to the BHPA and gained approval as a XC flight under supervision.

The rules for insurance mean that people with Pilot status are covered for 3rd party insurance for XC flights.  Therefore, to progress one’s flying for XC or to fly away from sight of the denoted landing area, it is beholden on the flyer to attain Pilot status.  The Pilot syllabus covers practical flying skills in flight theory, weather and air law to support the flyer to do this.
